# Aerobic Base & Zone Training

## Summary
The aerobic base is the foundation of all ultrarunning performance. For events lasting hours, the ability to produce speed at the aerobic threshold determines finishing pace far more than VO2max or anaerobic power. All three experts agree that the majority of training volume should be easy, but they diverge sharply on how to define intensity zones, whether Aerobic Deficiency Syndrome is real, and whether heart rate or perceived exertion should govern training intensity.

## Key Concepts

### Aerobic Threshold (AeT)
The highest intensity at which the aerobic system can produce ATP without significant anaerobic contribution. Corresponds to blood lactate of approximately 2 mmol/L. For ultra events exceeding 3-4 hours, race pace aligns closely with AeT.

### Anaerobic/Lactate Threshold (AnT/LT)
The highest intensity sustainable for roughly 30-60 minutes. Lactate production equals clearance at approximately 4 mmol/L. The gap between AeT and AnT indicates aerobic development status.

### The 10% Test
Divide AnT HR by AeT HR. If the result exceeds 1.10 (>10% spread), the athlete has an underdeveloped aerobic base and should prioritize Z1-Z2 training before adding intensity. Elite athletes achieve 5-7% spreads. Spreads above 30% indicate severe aerobic deficiency.

### Aerobic Deficiency Syndrome (ADS)
A term coined by Dr. Phil Maffetone (first described to Johnston in 1985) for the condition where excessive high-intensity training detrains the aerobic system. Athletes feel fast but can barely jog before exceeding AeT. The only cure is months of consistent low-to-moderate intensity training. Johnston estimates 80-90% of amateur athletes have ADS.

### HR Drift Test
Uphill Athlete's primary field test for AeT. Warm up 10-15 min, then hold constant pace for 40-60 min. Formula: ((HR second half / HR first half) - 1) x 100%. Result of 3.5-5% drift = you have found AeT. Below 3.5% = retest 5 bpm higher. Above 5% = retest lower. Correlates 95%+ with lab-based metabolic efficiency tests.

### Intensity Distribution
Across endurance sports, elite athletes consistently perform approximately 80% of training sessions below AeT and 20% above. By volume, this works out to roughly 90% aerobic, 10% high-intensity (Seiler research).

### AeT Testing Methods (Johnston, in order of ease)
1. **MAF method** (Maffetone): 180 minus age, with modifiers. Conservative; tends to set AeT HR too low.
2. **Ventilatory marker**: HR at which you can breathe through nose only or carry on conversation. Correlates with lactate ~2 mmol/L. Works only for athletes with good aerobic backgrounds.
3. **HR Drift Test**: Detailed above. Uphill Athlete's go-to field test. Included as the first workout in every plan.
4. **Lactate step test**: Treadmill at 5% grade. 15-min warmup. Series of 3-min stages at increasing speeds (HR bumps of ~10 bpm per stage). AeT = stage where lactate reaches 2 mmol/L or first 1 mmol/L jump.
5. **Gas exchange test**: Lab-based, gold standard. $150-$350. Most valuable data: AeT and LT HR/pace, fat vs. carb usage at various intensities.

### AnT/LT Field Test (Johnston)
Maximum sustained uphill effort of 30-60 minutes. Good warm-up (15-20 min), finish with 2-3 min Z3, then settle into maximum sustainable pace. Average HR over the test = AnT/LT. Less well-trained: use 30-min test. Well-conditioned: up to 1 hour. Reduce training load for a few days beforehand.

### The Endurance Tripod (Johnston)
1. **Speed at anaerobic/lactic threshold**: Best predictor of endurance performance. Most trainable.
2. **Movement economy**: Mechanical (technique) + metabolic (fat adaptation). Highly trainable through specific practice.
3. **VO2max**: Least trainable in well-trained athletes. Poorest correlation to performance. Largely genetic.
Controllable factors (threshold speed and economy) provide more practical returns than chasing VO2max increases.

### Polarized Training Distribution (Book Data)
Stephen Seiler's research across cross-country skiing, running, cycling, rowing:
- ~80% below AeT (Z1-Z2)
- ~6-8% between AeT and LT (Z3)
- ~12-14% above LT (Z4-Z5)

Kilian Jornet's 2016 distribution (1,300 hours total):
- 675-700 hours Z1-Z2 (~54%)
- 350 hours Z3 training (~26%)
- 275 hours Z3-Z5 high intensity, mostly racing (~20%)
Note: This was a heavy race year; younger years were more heavily weighted to base aerobic work.

### Energy System Physiology
- The aerobic system produces ~30 ATP per cycle vs. glycolysis producing only 2 ATP per cycle. (Magness, Ch. 1)
- The aerobic system becomes predominant around 90 seconds into exercise. (Magness, Ch. 2, citing Spencer & Gastin, 2001)
- "After you get past just a minute or two of an all-out effort, nearly 100 percent of the energy contributions will come from an aerobic pathway." (Koop, Ch. 2)
- All three energy systems (ATP-PCr, glycolytic, aerobic) are always active simultaneously -- no on/off switches.
- Low-intensity training (Z1-Z2) optimally stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis. Type I (slow-twitch) fibers have "far more and larger mitochondria." (Johnston, Uphill Athlete blog)

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Heart rate zones are the primary intensity tool.** Johnston uses a 4-zone system (simplified from the 5-zone system in the book) anchored to individually tested AeT and AnT:
  - Zone 1: AeT minus 20% to AeT minus 10% (very easy aerobic)
  - Zone 2: AeT minus 10% to AeT (maximum fat oxidation, aerobic capacity builder)
  - Zone 3: AeT to AnT (mixed aerobic-anaerobic, no more than 10% of annual volume)
  - Zone 4: AnT to max HR (aerobic power, volatile gains)
- **AeT speed is the single most important measure.** "You will never maximize your endurance potential without first maximizing your basic aerobic capacity (AeT)." (Ch. 2, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **ADS is real and prevalent.** A world-class cross-country skier with 90 ml/kg/min VO2max had AeT at only 135 bpm (8:30/mi). After 4 months of remedial base training, AeT improved to 165 bpm (6:15/mi). He was selected for the 2014 Olympic team. (Ch. 2)
- **No intensity until the base is built.** "We want to see a spread of less than 10 percent before adding any intensity into the training program." (Uphill Athlete blog)
- **Elite ultra-runner intensity distribution:** Z1 35-45%, Z2 44-54%, Z3 7-9%, Z4 less than 2%. (Evoke Endurance)
- **Race intensity for 100km+:** Z2 dominates 62-76% of race time. (Evoke Endurance)
- **Aerobically deficient runners can see 20-30% speed gains at AeT within 6-12 months** of consistent Z2 training. (Evoke Endurance)
- **"High-intensity training, while important for any endurance athlete, should be used as a supplement to, and not a substitute for, aerobic base training."** (Ch. 2)

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **RPE is the primary intensity tool, not heart rate.** Heart rate is unreliable due to terrain variability, temperature, caffeine, hydration status, altitude, and fatigue. "Your brain is the most valuable tool you have for monitoring and evaluating your intensity." (Ch. 6, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **ADS is "complete and total nonsense."** Koop explicitly calls it a made-up term that "appears nowhere in any scientific literature." "Don't be fearmongered into not doing high-intensity work because of a fake syndrome." (Ch. 2)
- **VO2max matters and is often underdeveloped.** Elite ultrarunners commonly test with LT at 95-97% of VO2max, indicating the ceiling is too low, not that LT is excellent. "VO2 max is your maximum potential -- the ceiling or roof of the building. If you want to fit more stuff in the building, raise the roof!" (Ch. 6)
- **RPE scale with talk test:**
  - RPE 4-5 (RecoveryRun): full conversation, "story time"
  - RPE 5-6 (EnduranceRun): comfortable conversation with pauses
  - RPE 7-8 (SteadyStateRun): 2-3 sentences
  - RPE 8-9 (TempoRun): 5-7 words max
  - RPE 9-10 (RunningIntervals): expletives and grunts only
- **The Endurance String Theory:** "Targeting VO2 max has a greater lifting effect on lactate-threshold fitness and aerobic endurance than training at aerobic intensities has on lifting lactate threshold or VO2 max." (Ch. 2)
- **Volume is king.** Total volume of workload sits at the base of Koop's hierarchy of ultramarathon training needs.

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **VO2max is NOT the gold standard for trained runners.** Among well-trained runners, VO2max does not correlate with performance. Paula Radcliffe's VO2max remained stable at ~70 ml/kg/min from 1992-2003 despite dramatic performance improvements. An Olympic female runner improved 3,000m time by 46 seconds while VO2max *decreased* from 72 to 66. (Ch. 5)
- **There are no magic training zones.** "There are no magical training zones that maximize adaptation or 'dead' space where adaptation is less. The body responds to the demand it is placed under." (Ch. 15)
- **Volume of easy training drove performance gains, not intensity.** World-class rowers from 1970-2001 improved performance 10% by increasing easy training from 30 to 50 hrs/week while supra-maximal decreased from 30 to 7 hrs/week. (Ch. 11, citing Fiskerstrand & Seiler, 2004)
- **Dual pathway to adaptation.** High volume signals via the calcium-calmodulin pathway; high intensity signals via the AMPK pathway. Both lead to PGC-1alpha and mitochondrial biogenesis -- two different routes to the same destination. Both stimuli are likely needed. (Ch. 11)
- **80/20 distribution confirmed in practice.** Moses Mosop (world-class marathoner): ~81% of training at steady paces or slower. "Research and practical experience has demonstrated that with elite runners 70-80% of their training volume is either General or Recovery paced training." (Ch. 18)
- **Problems with %VO2max zones:** "There was a 40-fold range for increases in lactate levels at 70% VO2max among individuals." Two runners at 80% VO2max can be below and above LT respectively. (Ch. 5)
- **Prefers Canova's classification:** General (far from race pace), Support (moderate proximity), Specific (at or near race pace), Pure Speed (neuromuscular). (Ch. 15)

## Where They Agree
1. **The majority of training should be easy.** All three converge on approximately 80% easy / 20% hard distribution by session count, roughly 90/10 by volume.
2. **Aerobic development is the foundation.** Regardless of how they define it, all agree that building the aerobic engine is the most important long-term investment.
3. **Easy must be truly easy.** Running too hard on easy days is universally flagged as a common and destructive error.
4. **The aerobic system is supreme for ultras.** After approximately 90 seconds, nearly 100% of energy comes from aerobic pathways. In ultra events, this dominance is absolute.
5. **Training at specific percentages of VO2max is unreliable** for prescribing individual intensity. Johnston and Magness both cite wide individual variation.

## Where They Disagree

### Is ADS real?
- **Johnston:** Absolutely. Coined by Maffetone, observed in 80-90% of amateur athletes. The 10% test is the diagnostic tool. No intensity work until it resolves.
- **Koop:** "Complete and total nonsense." A made-up term with no scientific backing. Athletes should not fear intensity work.
- **Magness:** Does not use the term but implicitly agrees with the principle -- easy running volume, not intensity, drives the adaptations that predict endurance performance.

### Heart rate vs. RPE for intensity control?
- **Johnston:** Heart rate is the primary tool. Individual zones set by lab or field tests. HR drift test is the first workout in every plan.
- **Koop:** RPE is superior. Heart rate is too unreliable on trails. RPE "effectively alchemizes all of the environmental and physiological phenomena" encountered.
- **Magness:** Neither strict zones nor pure RPE. Prefers paces standardized to recent race performance or percentages of goal race pace. "There are no magical training zones."

### Should VO2max be trained early?
- **Johnston:** Only when AeT/AnT spread is within 10%. Most athletes need months of base work first.
- **Koop:** Yes. VO2max intervals belong early in the training cycle. A higher ceiling lifts all thresholds below it.
- **Magness:** Acknowledges dual pathway -- both volume and intensity trigger mitochondrial biogenesis. VO2max plateaus early in trained athletes and is a poor predictor of performance.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Is the athlete new or returning from extended break?**
   - Yes: Follow Johnston's approach. Start with Z1-Z2 only. Perform HR drift test to find AeT. Build aerobic base for 8-12 weeks minimum before any intensity.

2. **Does the athlete have a 10% test spread >10%?**
   - Yes: Prioritize aerobic base building (Johnston framework). Limit intensity to strides and hill sprints only. Retest every 4-6 weeks.
   - No (spread <10%): The athlete has earned the right to add intensity work.

3. **Is the athlete experienced (2+ years consistent training, >400 hrs/year)?**
   - Yes: Can follow Koop's approach -- include VO2max intervals early in the training cycle to raise the ceiling, then progress to threshold and endurance-specific work.
   - No: Stay with Johnston's conservative base-first approach.

4. **What terrain does the athlete train on?**
   - Highly variable trail terrain: RPE may be more practical than HR (Koop's argument has merit here).
   - Consistent terrain (roads, treadmill, consistent trails): HR monitoring is reliable and valuable.

5. **How far out is the goal race?**
   - 6+ months: Aerobic base emphasis regardless of approach.
   - 3-6 months: Can blend approaches -- base volume with targeted intensity.
   - Under 3 months: Specificity and race-pace work take priority. The base is what it is.

6. **What data is available?**
   - HR data from watch: Use Johnston's HR drift test to establish AeT. Monitor cardiac drift during easy runs to validate zone boundaries. Flag sessions where average HR exceeds AeT during intended easy efforts.
   - RPE-only (no HR): Use Koop's RPE/talk test framework. Enforce easy = full conversation, hard = 5-7 words max.
   - CTL/ATL/TSB from Intervals.icu: Monitor aerobic fitness trends. CTL building at 3-5 points/week = good base building. TSB chronically below -20 = overreaching, pull back.
   - Pace at AeT trending faster over weeks/months: Aerobic base is improving. Continue current approach.
   - Pace at AeT stagnant despite consistent volume: Consider whether athlete is training in "no man's land" (too hard on easy days, too easy on hard days). Enforce separation.

7. **Warning signs that aerobic base is insufficient:**
   - HR drift >5% on standard easy runs (Johnston test)
   - 10% test spread >15%: significant aerobic deficiency
   - Inability to hold conversation during easy pace
   - Rapid HR rise in first 10-15 min of easy running
   - Pace at AeT is slower than expected for the athlete's race history
   - At moderate fatigue, speed is limited by legs, not breathing (suggests muscles can't utilize delivered oxygen -- Johnston/Evoke)
