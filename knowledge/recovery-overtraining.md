# Recovery & Overtraining

## Summary
Recovery is where adaptation happens -- training is the stimulus, but without adequate recovery, the stimulus becomes destructive. All three experts agree that overtraining syndrome (OTS) is a real and devastating condition that can cost months or years of development, and that prevention is far more effective than treatment. Johnston provides the most detailed staging model (FOR to NFOR to OTS), Koop reframes the problem as "under-recovery" rather than "overtraining" and establishes a recovery hierarchy (sleep > energy > scheduling), and Magness contributes the stress competition model and active recovery protocols. The cardinal rule across all three: "It is far better to be slightly undertrained than to be even a little overtrained."

## Key Concepts

### Three Stages of Overreaching and Overtraining
1. **Functional Overreaching (FOR):** Normal part of training. Performance decreases then rebounds with recovery (supercompensation). Every hard training block involves some FOR.
2. **Non-Functional Overreaching (NFOR):** Performance plateaus or continues declining despite normal recovery periods. Critical intervention window -- weeks to reverse if caught early.
3. **Overtraining Syndrome (OTS):** Accumulated fatigue overwhelms the body's ability to adapt. Requires weeks to months of complete rest. May never fully reverse in severe cases.

### Symptoms Progression
- **Early (sympathetic nervous system):** Raised HR for submaximal exertion, flat feeling, lack of pep, elevated cortisol, declining workout quality.
- **Advanced (parasympathetic nervous system):** Persistent deep fatigue, lowered testosterone, decreased HRV, irritability, depression, weight loss, amenorrhea, insomnia, lowered libido, suppressed immunity, loss of motivation and appetite.

### Recovery Hierarchy (Koop)
1. **Sleep** -- the gold standard
2. **Energy balance** -- adequate caloric intake
3. **Time between training** -- proper workout scheduling
4. **Active recovery** -- some benefit immediately post-exercise
5. **Compression/massage/foam roller** -- feel-good but largely placebo
6. **Cold therapies** -- can blunt training adaptation with chronic use

### Steve House's Three Warning Signs
1. **Slow to warm up:** Takes 30+ minutes to feel ready
2. **Poor sleep quality:** Overstimulated sympathetic nervous system
3. **Suspected injury:** New pain, swelling, even mild

If any one sign appears: skip the planned workout, prioritize sleep, nutrition, and active recovery.

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Three stages (FOR, NFOR, OTS):** "Functional Overreaching: normal, recovery allows supercompensation. Non-Functional Overreaching: performance plateaus, critical intervention window. Overtraining Syndrome: accumulated fatigue overwhelms adaptation, weeks/months to reverse." (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/overtraining/)
- **The cardinal rule:** "It is far better to be undertrained than to be even a little overtrained." (Ch. 4, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **Early warning signs:** Elevated resting HR on easy runs, persistent leg fatigue, inability to hit interval paces, lack of motivation. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/overtraining/)
- **Advanced symptoms:** Elevated cortisol, night sweats, insomnia, lowered testosterone and HRV, weight loss, amenorrhea, depression. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/overtraining/)
- **Detection methods:**
  - Staircase Test: monitor leg heaviness climbing stairs daily
  - Step-Up Test: record HR after 60-90 seconds stepping, then at 30 and 60 seconds post-rest
  - Track resting and submaximal exercise HR across sessions -- overtrained states show elevated readings where normal training would show decreases
  - (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/overtraining/)
- **Red flag threshold:** Two or more symptoms together warrant immediate rest. (https://uphillathlete.com/recovery/overtraining-symptoms-in-mountain-athletes/)
- **Workout grading system:**
  - A: Felt like Superman, plenty in reserve
  - B: Good workout, completed with no problem
  - C: Did it but felt flat or off
  - D: Could not finish or had to reduce
  - F: Could not train due to fatigue/illness
  - **Rule:** More than two Cs in a row, or a C and D in one week = stop and assess. (Ch. 4)
- **Steve House's three signs:** (1) Slow to warm up (30+ min to feel ready), (2) Poor sleep quality (overstimulated sympathetic nervous system), (3) Suspected injury (new pain, swelling, even mild). Action: skip planned workout, prioritize sleep, nutrition, active recovery. (https://uphillathlete.com/recovery/top-three-signs-you-did-not-recover/)
- **Recovery from overreaching:** Rest 5-7 days with recovery activities, then take as many days to ramp back as you missed. (https://uphillathlete.com/recovery/overtraining-recovery-and-symptoms-explained/)
- **Recovery week guidelines:** 3:1 build-to-recovery cycle (3 build weeks, 1 recovery week). Some athletes may need 2:1 cycle. Recovery weeks: drop training load 40-60% of last big week's volume. No more than one intensity session during recovery week. After intentional overreaching: drop load by as much as 70%. (Ch. 4)
- **Athlete testimonials:**
  - Clare Gallagher: "My legs felt so sore after just easy running... my heart wouldn't stop racing, even on slow, flat runs."
  - Krissy Moehl: "Taking two weeks off completely could save a year of running in your future."
  - Karl Meltzer: "When I feel tired, I rest, period... 60 miles per week is plenty to win lots of 100s."
  - (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/overtraining/)
- **Kilian on overtraining:** "In 2007, I overtrained. I did a big winter with thirty races and then I started running right away. The first indication was that I was not recovering at all... super-low iron levels. So I rested completely for two full weeks. After that I started training again very, very easily." (Ch. 4)
- **Marc Pinsach Rubirola's lesson:** Overtrained for 4 seasons trying to increase from 700 to 850 hours/year. "You can't increase training more than 10 percent between two consecutive seasons." His ceiling: 700 hours/year. (Ch. 4)
- **When to stop:** "When the first signs of an illness show up, stop training. Your body and its limited energy reserves are fully engaged in producing antibodies to fight the infection." (Ch. 4)
- **Return after illness:** One day of easy training for every day sick and away. Return to Z1-Z2 efforts first. (Ch. 4)
- **Return after fatigue-related break >2 days:** Start at 50% duration with low intensity for a couple of days. If those go well, increase to 75% for a day or so. Reassess before returning to full load. (Ch. 4)
- **Ferritin warning:** Standard "low" = 12 ng/ml. For endurance athletes: <50 ng/ml = some reduced performance, <30 ng/ml = dramatically reduced performance and chronic fatigue. (Ch. 4)
- **Recovery enhancement priorities:**
  1. Diet: 100-200 cal in first 30 min post-exercise (3:1 or 4:1 carb:protein)
  2. Sleep: Critical for HGH release
  3. Massage: 10 min deep-tissue can dramatically speed recovery
  4. Reducing life stressors
  5. Recovery workouts at very low intensity
  6. Electro-stim machines on recovery setting

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Reframing:** Better terms than "overtraining" are "under-recovery" or "unexplained underperformance syndrome" (UUPS). "Training isn't the root of the problem -- recovery is." (Ch. 8, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **Symptoms:** Unrelenting fatigue, performance decline, sleep disturbances, elevated resting HR, low HRV, mood disturbances, loss of libido, low motivation, loss of appetite, frequent minor injuries/illnesses. The tipping point is highly individual and hard to quantify. (Ch. 8)
- **Functional overreaching vs. OTS:** Functional overreaching is a normal part of training -- performance decreases then rebounds with recovery. OTS/UUPS: >2 weeks of complete rest is not enough to reverse symptoms. (Ch. 8)
- **Recovery hierarchy (in order of effectiveness):**
  1. **Sleep:** Sleeping >8 hrs reduces injury chance by 61%. <7 hrs sleep = 3x more likely to develop a cold. Sleep efficiency >98% = 5.5x lower cold risk vs. <92% efficiency. (Ch. 8)
  2. **Energy balance:** Adequate total caloric intake; prevent RED-S. (Ch. 8)
  3. **Time between training:** Proper workout scheduling. (Ch. 8)
  4. **Active recovery:** Some benefit immediately after exercise, but by the next day equals passive rest. (Ch. 8)
  5. **Compression/massage/foam roller:** Feel-good, big placebo effect, no proven performance benefit. (Ch. 8)
  6. **Cold therapies:** Can blunt training adaptation; avoid for chronic use. (Ch. 8)
- **Sleep hygiene protocol:** Room: calm, cool (60-67 degrees F), dark, quiet. Consistent sleep/wake times. Electronics off 90 min before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin). Morning: seek light, move (10-20 min), nourish (hydrate + food). (Ch. 8)
- **NSAIDs -- strong warning:** Increases risk of hyponatremia and acute kidney injury in endurance athletes. Can perpetuate existing injuries by masking pain. "The use of NSAIDs in endurance and ultra-endurance sports is dangerous. The research is unequivocal." (Ch. 8)
- **Rest vs. strength training for injury prevention:** Sleep >8 hours provides ~61% injury reduction (von Rosen 2016). Strength training provides ~66% injury reduction (Lauersen 2018). Rest has comparable benefit with zero additional training load. "Adding two hours of strength training per week -- that's two hours you could have spent simply resting." (Ch. 11)
- **Burnout vs. overtraining:** Different recovery timelines and root causes. Burnout is psychological; overtraining is physiological. Both require time away from training. (https://trainright.com/training-burnout-prevention-recovery/)
- **Phase transition triggers (signals that recovery is needed):**
  1. Performance declines 3-8% on 2 of last 3 workouts
  2. Feel progressively worse during runs for 3 consecutive days
  3. Maximum 6 weeks in any phase regardless
  - Recovery phase: 4-6 days of 45-60 min RecoveryRuns. Maybe an extra rest day. Don't exceed 7 days easy -- beyond that, fitness decay outweighs recovery benefit. (Ch. 12)
- **Transition period:** 2-4 weeks completely away from running (not totally inactive -- ride bikes, swim, rock climb, yoga). "Get to the point where they crave running as a desire, not a compulsion." (https://trainright.com/off-season-for-ultrarunners-setting-up-your-transition-phase/)
- **Post-ultra recovery:** 7 key steps. Focus on sleep, nutrition, gentle movement, and allowing full tissue repair before returning to structured training. (https://trainright.com/steps-recovery-running-ultramarathon/)
- **Detraining resilience:** Cutting training to ~50% (3 sessions/week): only 5-10% decline even over several weeks. Return to previous fitness doesn't take long after moderate detraining. (Ch. 3)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye, 1978):** Three stages: (1) Alarm -- initial response, lowering of resistance; (2) Resistance -- body adapts, resistance increases; (3) Exhaustion -- if stress continually applied, resistance lowers considerably. Without adequate recovery, training leads to the exhaustion stage. (Ch. 10, *The Science of Running*)
- **Dose-response relationship:** Inverted U-shape -- too little stimulus = no adaptation; too much = diminishing/negative adaptations. Each athlete needs more or less stimulus depending on physiology and training history. "A child needs less Advil than an adult for the same effect." (Ch. 10)
- **Stress competition model:** The body responds to stress similarly regardless of source. Life stress, work stress, relationship stress, and training stress all draw from the same recovery pool. An athlete under high life stress has less capacity for training stress. (Ch. 10)
- **Three types of fatigue:**
  1. **Calcium accumulation:** Short-duration, high-strength. Recovery: minutes. Self-limiting.
  2. **Metabolic:** Short-term (lactate/H+ accumulation) = minutes. Long-term (glycogen depletion/bonk) = hours to days.
  3. **Neurologic:** Neurotransmitter depletion at synapses. Recovery: hours to several days.
  - (Ch. 10)
- **Recovery times (from Jan Olbrecht):**
  - Low-intensity endurance (2-3 hours or less): 8-24 hours
  - Long-duration (>2 hours) even at low intensity: 24-48 hours for glycogen restoration
  - High-intensity exercise and strength training: 2-3 full days
  - (Ch. 10)
- **Fatigue management framework -- Group A (muscular):**
  - Sore muscles (DOMS): peaks 48 hours. Light aerobic recovery, swimming, e-stim, light massage, ice bath after 12h, extra sleep, extra protein before bed. NO speed/power/strength/HIIT.
  - Dead/heavy legs: Usually from increasing load too fast in past 10 days. Pool exercise, deep-tissue massage, compression, elevate legs, increase post-workout carbs, extra sleep. NO extensive aerobic, zones 3-5, ME, anaerobic work.
  - (Ch. 10)
- **Fatigue management framework -- Group B (energy):**
  - Increase food quality, sleep quantity/quality, social time
  - Blood tests: cortisol, ferritin, testosterone, mononucleosis
  - Easy short Z1 workouts (can include short accelerations)
  - NO intensive workouts, alcohol, added stress
  - (Ch. 10)
- **Active recovery -- mini-workouts at 60-70%:**
  - Short split doubles (e.g., 4mi + 4mi instead of 8mi) enhance recovery
  - Shorter runs minimize mechanical and oxidative damage
  - Easier to restore glycogen after 4-5mi than 10mi
  - May get hormonal release twice per day
  - Growth hormone: ~550% increase from 0-40min of easy running, then only 40-50% more from 40-60min (Ch. 19)
- **Avoiding overtraining:** "The experience of coaches points to a long block of high intensity training leading to decreased performance, mostly likely due to overtraining." (Ch. 11, citing Lydiard 1998). Pain inhibitory system can become "exhausted" from constant activation. (Ch. 8, citing Tesarz 2013)
- **CNS fatigue (often overlooked):** "As distance coaches we are used to recognizing fatigue that results from running lots of mileage or hard workouts. What we don't grasp is CNS fatigue. The fatigue experienced after doing 50m sprints is a totally foreign concept." Must recognize CNS fatigue and implement recovery strategy. (Ch. 8)
- **Lack of motivation as signal:** Lack of motivation on a given day is a clear warning sign -- body telling you to back off. "Don't train compulsively." Willpower "can also be your worst enemy when it clouds your judgment and becomes a merciless taskmaster." (Ch. 4)

## Where They Agree
1. **Prevention over treatment.** All three experts emphasize that catching overtraining early is vastly easier than recovering from it. Days of proactive rest prevent weeks or months of forced rest.
2. **Sleep is the most important recovery tool.** Sleep quality and quantity outweigh all other recovery modalities -- supplements, compression, ice baths, massage.
3. **The cardinal rule: better undertrained than overtrained.** Johnston says it explicitly. Koop frames it through his hierarchy (rest above volume). Magness shows it through the dose-response curve.
4. **Motivation loss is a warning sign, not laziness.** The body communicates through motivation. Persistent lack of desire to train is a signal to back off, not push through.
5. **Recovery weeks are mandatory.** Whether 3:1, 2:1, or phase-triggered, all three require regular recovery periods built into the training plan.
6. **NSAIDs are dangerous for endurance athletes.** Never mask pain to train through it. Risk of hyponatremia and kidney injury.
7. **Life stress counts.** Training stress is not the only stressor. Work, relationships, travel, and sleep disruption all compete for the same recovery resources.

## Where They Disagree

### Framing the Problem
- **Johnston:** Uses the FOR-NFOR-OTS progression model. Training load is the primary variable; the problem is too much stimulus relative to recovery capacity.
- **Koop:** Rejects "overtraining" as the primary label -- prefers "under-recovery" or UUPS. "Training isn't the root of the problem -- recovery is." The same training load that produces overtraining with poor recovery produces adaptation with good recovery.
- **Magness:** Frames it through the General Adaptation Syndrome and stress competition. All stressors draw from the same pool. The problem is total allostatic load, not just training load.

### Recovery Modalities
- **Koop:** Explicitly hierarchical. Sleep and energy balance dominate. Compression, massage, and cold therapy are largely placebo. Cold therapy may blunt adaptation with chronic use.
- **Johnston:** Ranks recovery methods but gives more credit to massage (10 min deep-tissue "can dramatically speed recovery" citing Tarnopolsky study) and electro-stim.
- **Magness:** Advocates active recovery through split doubles and mini-workouts at 60-70% effort. More nuanced about the type of fatigue determining the type of recovery needed (muscular vs. energy vs. neurological).

### When to Use Active Recovery vs. Complete Rest
- **Magness:** Active recovery at 60-70% effort enhances supercompensation. Split doubles provide hormonal benefits. But distinguishes muscular fatigue (needs passive rest) from energy fatigue (can tolerate light activity).
- **Johnston:** Recovery workouts at very low intensity. Swimming excellent for tired legs. But two complete rest days per week recommended for multi-system training.
- **Koop:** Active recovery has "some benefit immediately after, but by next day equals passive rest." More skeptical of active recovery's superiority over simply sleeping more.

### Phase Transition vs. Calendar-Based Recovery
- **Koop:** Trigger-based transitions. Performance declining 3-8% on 2 of last 3 workouts OR feeling worse for 3 consecutive days = take recovery phase. Maximum 6 weeks in any phase regardless.
- **Johnston:** Calendar-based 3:1 or 2:1 build-to-recovery cycles with predetermined recovery weeks. Intensity-dependent: recovery weeks drop 40-60% of volume; after intentional overreaching, drop up to 70%.
- **Magness:** Phase-based with flexibility. "Refresh periods" of 3-10 days inserted when needed. Focus returns to general or support work. More organic than scheduled.

## Coaching Decision Tree

### Step 1: Daily Monitoring
Check these signals every day:
- **Resting HR:** Elevated >5 bpm above baseline = caution. Persistent elevation = concern.
- **HRV:** Trending downward over 7+ days = reduced readiness.
- **Sleep quality:** Consistent poor sleep = reduce training load.
- **Motivation:** Genuine lack of desire to train (not just laziness) = warning sign.
- **Steve House's 3 signs:** Slow to warm up, poor sleep, suspected injury = skip planned workout.

### Step 2: Workout Grading (Post-Session)
Apply Johnston's A-F grading system:
- Consistent A/B grades: training load is appropriate
- Two C grades in a row: monitor closely, consider an extra rest day
- C + D in one week: take 2-3 days easy, reassess
- D or F: stop structured training, focus on sleep and nutrition

### Step 3: Classify the Fatigue

**Muscular fatigue (sore/dead legs):**
- Apply Magness Group A protocols: light aerobic recovery, swimming, massage, extra sleep, extra protein
- NO speed, power, strength, or HIIT until resolved
- Usually from volume increase in past 10 days

**Energy fatigue (lethargy, low motivation):**
- Apply Magness Group B protocols: increase food quality, sleep, social time
- Consider blood work: cortisol, ferritin, testosterone
- Easy short Z1 workouts with short accelerations are OK
- NO intensive workouts, alcohol, added stress

**Neurological fatigue (flat but not sore):**
- Often from high-intensity work or CNS-demanding sessions (sprints, plyometrics)
- Recovery: hours to several days
- Easy running is fine; avoid speed work or coordination-demanding training

### Step 4: Recovery Protocol by Severity

**Mild overreaching (TSB -10 to -20, 1-2 days of feeling off):**
- Insert an extra rest day or easy day
- Maintain normal week structure with reduced intensity
- Ensure 8+ hours sleep
- No intervention beyond this

**Moderate overreaching (TSB < -20, 3-5 days of declining performance):**
- Take a full recovery week: drop volume 40-60%
- Maximum one intensity session during recovery week
- Focus on sleep, nutrition, and stress reduction
- Should feel ready to attack training by end of week

**Significant overreaching (TSB < -25, 1-2 weeks of flat/declining performance):**
- Rest 5-7 days with recovery activities only (walks, swimming, yoga)
- Then ramp back over as many days as you missed
- Start at 50% of normal training load
- If 75% feels fine after a few days, progress to full load
- If not, continue conservative build

**Suspected OTS (persistent symptoms despite 2+ weeks of rest):**
- Complete rest for several weeks minimum
- Seek medical evaluation: blood work (cortisol, testosterone, ferritin, CBC), sleep study if warranted
- Very gradual reintroduction of easy exercise
- "Think in terms of months and years (and even decades) instead of days and weeks." (Steve House)
- This is a medical-level concern -- recommend professional guidance

### Step 5: Structural Prevention
- Build recovery weeks into every training cycle: 3:1 or 2:1 build:recovery
- Target 2 rest days per week during heavy training blocks
- Track TSB: stay above -20 for most of the training cycle; only dip below for planned overreaching
- Monitor ferritin: keep above 50 ng/ml for optimal performance
- Transition periods: 2-4 weeks away from structured running after major races or seasons
- Do not increase volume AND intensity in the same week
- Do not increase any variable by more than 10% per week for more than 3 consecutive weeks
