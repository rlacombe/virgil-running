# Race-Day Execution

## Summary
Race-day execution separates finishers from DNFs and good races from great ones. Fitness is fixed by race morning -- everything beyond that is execution: pacing, nutrition, aid station management, mental strategy, and adaptation when plans fail. Koop provides the most comprehensive race-execution framework with his perceived exertion endpoint interaction model, the ADAPT framework for crisis management, and his bull's-eye nutrition system. Magness contributes the neuroscience of pacing -- anticipatory regulation, the peak-end rule, and associative vs. dissociative mental strategies. Johnston's contributions are largely embedded in his athletes' race reports and the durability-first philosophy. Mastering execution is a learnable skill that improves with deliberate practice across B and C races.

## Key Concepts

### Pacing in Ultramarathons
- Pace is regulated by the brain, not by catastrophic muscle failure. The brain works in an anticipatory manner -- slowing the athlete BEFORE critical limits are reached.
- In ultras, the ability to predict how you will feel 20, 40, or 60 miles ahead is essentially zero. Pacing strategies that work for marathons (even splits, target pace per mile) break down entirely.
- The goal is not to be the fastest but to slow down the least: "The people who finish first aren't necessarily the fastest; they are the ones who are the least broken and who slow down less overall." (Mike Foote, Ch. 3, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)

### Perceived Exertion Endpoint Interaction
- Athletes constantly draw a mental line from current effort to predicted maximum tolerable exertion at the end of the task.
- Works well for short events; backfires in ultras because predictive capabilities cannot extend far enough.
- Solution: bypass forecasting entirely by "staying in the moment."

### ADAPT Framework (Crisis Management)
- **A** = Accept -- take a deep breath and accept the situation for what it is
- **D** = Diagnose -- most diagnoses will be obvious once you have accepted the situation
- **A** = Analyze -- put brain power to the situation and consider your well-thought-out list of problems
- **P** = Plan -- consider what tools you have at your disposal; there are dozens of solutions to running problems
- **T** = Take action -- implement the planned solutions

### Nutrition Targets
- **200-300 calories per hour** (~90% from carbohydrates)
- **30-60 grams of carbohydrates per hour** (up to 60-90 g/hr for trained gut)
- **500-750 mL of fluid per hour** depending on conditions, sweat rate, altitude
- **600-800 mg sodium per liter** of fluid consumed

### The Bull's-Eye Nutrition System
- Develop 3-5 "bull's-eye" foods through training that always work
- Inner ring requirements: at least 1 real food, at least 1 engineered food, something sweet, something savory, something salty
- Second ring: backup foods tried in training
- Third ring: similar untried foods
- Off-target: foods known to cause problems
- Easy to communicate to crew; visual reference that works under fatigue

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Durability-first pacing:** Mike Foote's philosophy encapsulates the Johnston approach -- "Instead of striving for speed, I limit slowing down. To achieve that, I cultivate durability." (Ch. 3, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **Calculated event pace:** For Mike Foote's 24-hour ski record, he calculated event pace of 2,800-3,000 ft/hr to buffer for transitions and descending. Pre-calculate pace budgets for each section. (Ch. 3)
- **Stride length as performance predictor:** "The decrease of stride length during races is one of the most powerful predictors of race results." (Ch. 6) ME training preserves stride length deep into races -- Ruth Croft achieved her highest HR only 5km from the UTMB finish while competitors faded. (https://www.irunfar.com/coaching-the-2025-utmb-champions-scott-johnstons-focus-on-muscular-endurance)
- **Race nutrition -- Mike Foote's 24-hour record:** Consumed ~400 cal/hour. Mix: gels, Coke, soup broth, PB&Js, sweet potato muffins, sticky rice balls with bacon, 2/3 quart maple syrup. Used descents for recovery while still prioritizing speed. (Ch. 3)
- **Post-exercise recovery nutrition:** Consume 100-200 calories in first 30 min post-exercise. Ratio: 3:1 or 4:1 carbs to protein. Miss this window and recovery can be delayed by days. (Ch. 3)
- **Mental framework:** Rod Bien: "Don't nickel and dime yourself out of the satisfaction of crossing the finish line." Separate small problems from DNF decisions. Focus on short-term goals and positive reinforcement. (Ch. 2)
- **Adversity training:** Jared Campbell: "In every challenge there is something to be learned and a way to be a better person for it." Intentionally seek out nasty conditions in training to build mental resilience. (Ch. 4)
- **UTMB fueling lessons:** Over 15,000 calories across 40+ hours. Carry 2,000+ calories between major crew points (sections exceed 10 hours). Nearly 1,000 calories consumed at single major stops. Electrolyte deficiency caused dizziness/nausea; fixed with salt caps and salty soup. Palette fatigue is inevitable -- novelty foods (pizza!) can restore appetite. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/fueling-for-utmb/)

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Perceived exertion endpoint interaction:** "Your ability to predict how you will feel down the trail is clouded by the sheer duration and your impaired ability to comprehend (let's get real, are you smarter or dumber after 80 miles?)" (https://trainright.com/ultramarathon-pacing-strategies-staying-in-the-moment/)
- **Stay-in-the-moment pacing:** Focus on the next short interval -- 10 seconds, one mile, or the next aid station -- then repeat. This eliminates DNF risk from projecting suffering beyond tolerance. Bypass forecasting entirely. (https://trainright.com/ultramarathon-pacing-strategies-staying-in-the-moment/)
- **RPE as primary pacing tool:** RPE "effectively alchemizes all of the environmental and physiological phenomenon" encountered during races. Heart rate is unreliable due to terrain variability, temperature, dehydration, caffeine, fatigue. (https://trainright.com/revolutionize-your-run-training/)
- **Walk-to-run transition:** Flat ground: ~13:30-14:00 min/mi -- below this, walking is more economical; above, running is more economical. No universal grade for the transition. Use RPE: hike when RPE exceeds race plan, run when RPE is in desired range. (Ch. 5, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **ADAPT framework:** Five-step crisis management system for when everything goes wrong. Accept the situation, diagnose, analyze, plan, take action. (https://trainright.com/adapt-a-5-step-plan-for-when-everything-in-your-race-goes-wrong/)
- **Elite racing has changed:** "Runners who want to win big are going to have to take big risks." Start fast, cruise where possible, surge in places that suit abilities. The sport has shifted from time-trial mentality to tactical racing with variable intensity. (https://trainright.com/how-ultramarathons-will-be-won-from-now-on/)
- **Racing underprepared:** Take a more conservative approach to pacing. The ability to adapt during a race may be the most important skill an ultrarunner can develop. (https://trainright.com/how-to-race-ultramarathons-when-you-are-underprepared/)
- **Bull's-eye nutrition:** 200-300 cal/hr, 30-60 g carbs/hr. Consume slightly more early (first 2 hrs) as GI symptoms tend to develop later. Late race (miles 50+): reduce carb density, shift toward simple sugars and electrolyte-based nutrition. (Ch. 13)
- **Fluid strategy:** Separate calories from hydration -- calories in pocket, hydration in bottles. As temp changes, fluid needs change independently from calorie needs. High-carb drinks cause GI distress when you need to increase fluid in heat. "You can come back from a caloric deficit easily and within minutes by eating. Returning to normal hydration status takes much longer." (Ch. 13)
- **Sodium:** 600-800 mg sodium per liter of fluid consumed. Don't need to replace 100% of sweat sodium. (Ch. 13)
- **Massive under-fueling problem:** Ultrarunners report consuming 80-120 cal/hr, compared to the ~240-260 cal/hr they should be taking in. (https://trainright.com/ultrarunning-international-society-sports-nutrition-position-stand/)
- **Gut training:** Practice race-day nutrition 4-6 times during training. GI distress is the leading cause of DNFs. (Ch. 13)
- **Aid station optimization:** Get in, get out, get on your way. Know your action plan precisely so you won't be distracted. Don't leave without adequate hydration. Estimate anticipated duration between each station. Don't be overzealous speeding away -- stick to your general pacing strategy. For longer events, plan gear exchanges and know which stations are accessible to crews or have drop bags. (https://trainright.com/how-to-optimize-ultramarathon-aid-station-stops/)
- **Crew coordination:** Forecasting pace and understanding driving distances between stations is essential. Crew must know the athlete's bull's-eye nutrition plan and drop bag locations. (https://trainright.com/what-ultrarunners-can-learn-from-the-biggest-crewing-operation-at-western-states/)
- **Western States specific wisdom:** Manage pre-race energy. The 2,500-ft Escarpment climb tempts runners to go too hard early -- restraint is essential. Carry adequate water through the first 30 miles. Don't "trash your quads" on early descents -- the net-downhill course rewards patience. Take advantage of aid station foot care professionals. (https://trainright.com/western-states-strategies-from-coaches/)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Anticipatory pacing:** "In a time trial in the heat, your pace slows early on, despite not having enough time to reach a critical core temperature." The brain works in an anticipatory manner -- pace slows BEFORE critical limits are reached. Marathon: "pace slows before a runner truly runs out of glycogen." (Ch. 3, *The Science of Running*)
- **The end spurt / kick:** At end of race, muscle activation INCREASES (EMG increases) -- should not occur if muscles are truly "failing." The brain "loosens the reins" near finish because feedback says completion is near. End spurt velocity is not related to VO2max or running economy but to vMART (neuromuscular abilities). (Ch. 5, Ch. 8)
- **Deception studies on pacing:**
  - Stone (2012): Cyclists racing an avatar at 102% of best performance produced faster times than ever before. They tapped into "anaerobic reserve" more. "It was almost as if their brains loosened the reins just a little."
  - Castle et al. (2012): Cyclists told it was cool (actually 32C) performed same as in actual cool conditions -- external feedback profoundly affects pacing.
  - Ness & Patton (2012): Changing weight labels caused subjects to lift 20lb more than ever before.
- **Feedback and pacing (Amann et al., 2006):** Blocking afferent feedback with fentanyl during 5km cycling trial: participants went out much harder, built greater peripheral fatigue, faded hard, had problems walking afterward. Feedback is how brain ensures exhaustion occurs at the finish, not before. "Part of training is learning what feedback is important and what is not." (Ch. 3)
- **Associative vs. dissociative focus:**
  - **Associative:** Focus inward on body sensations (body scan, breath following). Best for pacing, technical terrain, eating/drinking, beginning of race.
  - **Dissociative:** Redirect attention away from effort (counting, planning). Best for middle stages, managing fatigue/pain perception. Can lower perceived exertion.
  - Key skill: ability to SWITCH between strategies deliberately. "Anybody can zone out... what you're after is the control to move to dissociation when you want to." (Ch. 15, cited in Koop Ch. 15)
- **Mental fatigue impairs performance:** 90 minutes of cognitively demanding tasks before exercise significantly worsened cycle-to-exhaustion performance vs. watching documentaries. (Marcora 2009, Ch. 8) Implication: manage mental energy on race day.
- **Pain tolerance is trainable:** Athletes have higher pain TOLERANCE but similar pain THRESHOLD compared to non-athletes. Pain tolerance correlates with number and quality of COPING STRATEGIES (Ord 2003). Training duration negatively correlates with fear of pain. (Ch. 8)
- **Psychobiological model:** "Your physiological capacity determines the upper limit of performance capacity, but your psychological skills are the deciding factor in how much of that capacity we ultimately utilize." (Ch. 15, Koop citing same framework)

## Where They Agree
1. **RPE is the primary pacing tool for ultras.** Heart rate is unreliable on trails due to terrain, temperature, dehydration, caffeine, and fatigue. RPE integrates all factors.
2. **Stay in the moment.** Both Koop and Magness explicitly argue against projecting suffering forward. Focus on the next aid station, the next mile, the next 10 seconds.
3. **Nutrition is a trainable skill, not race-day improvisation.** Practice race nutrition 4-6 times during training. Gut adaptation is real. Most ultrarunners drastically under-fuel.
4. **The brain regulates pace, not catastrophic muscle failure.** Exercise is regulated, not catastrophic. Fatigue is a signal, not a hard wall.
5. **Aid stations require a plan.** Get in, execute a rehearsed routine, get out. Don't sit down. Don't let small problems become big ones.
6. **Mental skills are trainable.** Coping strategies, pain tolerance, focus switching, and crisis management are skills that improve with practice.

## Where They Disagree

### Pacing Strategy for Competitive Athletes
- **Koop:** Acknowledges elite racing has shifted to tactical, variable-intensity approaches -- "start fast, cruise in places where they can conserve energy, and surge in places that suit their abilities." High-risk, high-reward. This is for elites only.
- **Johnston/Foote:** Durability-first. Calculate event pace, then limit slowing down. Success comes from being the least broken, not the fastest early.
- **Magness:** Emphasizes the neuroscience -- the brain's anticipatory system is more conservative than necessary. Training teaches athletes to narrow the gap between what feedback says and actual limits.

### Carbohydrate Targets
- **Koop:** 200-300 cal/hr, 30-60 g carbs/hr as a starting point. Up to 60-90 g/hr for trained gut. Rates above 90 g/hr likely unnecessary at ultra intensities.
- **Johnston (UTMB context):** Reports ~400 cal/hr for elite efforts (Mike Foote). Less prescriptive about exact targets; more focused on metabolic flexibility and fat adaptation reducing carb dependence.
- **Magness:** Focuses more on the glycogen depletion signal during training than race-day fueling specifics. Advocates some long runs without carbohydrate intake to maximize adaptation.

### Role of Dissociation
- **Magness:** Dissociative thinking can dampen feedback by "clogging the pathway," increasing tolerance to fatigue. Valuable tool but must be balanced with associative awareness for pacing and safety.
- **Koop:** Explicitly teaches switching between associative (body scan) and dissociative (counting) strategies. Dissociate to manage pain; associate to pace, eat, and navigate.
- **Johnston:** Less explicitly systematic about mental strategies; relies more on purpose-driven motivation and adversity reframing.

## Coaching Decision Tree

### Pre-Race: Build the Execution Plan

**Step 1: Set goals**
- Outcome goals: finish time, placing, or completion
- Process goals: calorie targets, effort levels, attitude, crew instructions
- Process goals must be under the runner's control and directly support outcome goals

**Step 2: Build the nutrition plan**
- Determine bull's-eye foods through training (minimum 4-6 rehearsals)
- Calculate calorie targets per hour (200-300 cal/hr baseline)
- Set fluid targets based on sweat test data across temperature ranges
- Plan sodium intake: 600-800 mg/L of fluid consumed
- Map aid station nutrition: what to eat at each stop, what to carry between stops
- For 100+ miles: carry 2,000+ calories between major crew points

**Step 3: Plan aid station strategy**
- Know crew-accessible vs. drop-bag-only stations
- Create a simple, repeatable routine: check water, standard food, standard gear check
- Estimate time between stations based on terrain and pace
- Pre-pack drop bags with specific items for each segment

**Step 4: Prepare the crew**
- Share the bull's-eye nutrition plan as a visual reference
- Provide pace estimates for each segment (with buffer)
- Establish communication protocol (what to say, what NOT to say)
- Map driving routes between crew-accessible stations

### During Race: Execute and Adapt

**Early race (first 25%):**
- Restrain effort. The biggest pacing mistake is going out too hard.
- Establish fueling patterns with easily digestible carbs
- Use associative focus: body scan, breathing check, terrain assessment
- Set a calorie timer/alarm if helpful

**Mid race (25-60%):**
- Maintain steady caloric and carb intake (this is where most athletes under-fuel)
- Switch between associative and dissociative strategies as needed
- Hike when RPE exceeds plan; run when RPE is in desired range
- Fix small problems (blisters, chafing, gear) immediately -- don't let them compound

**Late race (60%+):**
- Reduce carb density, shift toward simple sugars and electrolytes
- Expect palette fatigue -- novelty foods can restore appetite
- Stay in the moment. Do not project suffering forward.
- If things go wrong: apply ADAPT (Accept, Diagnose, Analyze, Plan, Take action)

**Crisis situations:**
- GI distress: slow down, switch to simple sugars, walk for 10-15 min, reassess
- Bonk: eat immediately (simple carbs), walk until blood sugar stabilizes (15-30 min)
- Overheating: slow down, dump water, ice on neck/torso, increase fluid intake
- Dark patch (mental): switch to dissociative focus, set micro-goals (next aid station), use mantras
- Injury pain: distinguish between "race hurt" and "damage hurt." If potentially injurious, stop and assess honestly

### Post-Race
- Consume 100-200 calories in first 30 minutes (3:1 or 4:1 carbs to protein)
- Rehydrate gradually
- Do not make race decisions (DNS for next race, signing up for another) for at least 48 hours
- Debrief: what worked, what failed, what to adjust for next time
