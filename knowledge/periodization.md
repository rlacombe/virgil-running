# Periodization & Training Blocks

## Summary
Periodization -- the systematic organization of training into phases with distinct goals -- is where the three coaching philosophies diverge most sharply. Johnston follows a classical Transition → Base → Sport-Specific model inspired by cross-country skiing and mountaineering tradition. Koop uses block periodization with reverse intensity ordering, starting with VO2max work and finishing with race-specific endurance. Magness advocates a non-linear Build/Maintain/Connect framework drawn from Renato Canova and Arthur Lydiard where all qualities are present in every phase but emphasis shifts. Understanding when to apply which model is critical for effective coaching.

## Key Concepts

### Capacity vs. Utilization Training (Bob Bowman Framework, cited by Johnston)
- **Capacity Training:** Improves long-term potential. Slow to accrue but long lasting. Often not sport-specific. Prioritized in Base Period.
- **Utilization Training:** Improves near-term performance. Quick acting but volatile. Event-specific. Prioritized near competition.
- "Utilization Training will reduce the athlete's capacity" -- must be offset with ongoing Capacity Training.
- Common mistake: amateurs copying elites' utilization workouts without years of capacity building.

### Time Course of Adaptation (Seiler 2006, cited by Koop)
- VO2max adaptations: fastest to develop, plateau soonest
- Lactate threshold: moderate time course
- Efficiency/economy: slowest, can continue improving even after VO2max and LT plateau
- 4-6 weeks needed for training to make meaningful performance impact

### Block vs. Mixed-Intensity Periodization
- **Block:** Focus on ONE intensity type for 2-6 weeks, then recover and switch. Research slightly favors block for trained athletes (Garcia-Pallares 2010; Molmen et al. 2019).
- **Mixed-intensity micro-periodization:** Include multiple intensity types every week (collegiate model: RI + TR + race + long run).
- Koop generally favors block style for experienced athletes who have plateaued.

### Event Priority System (Johnston)
- **A priority:** Major goals. Significant taper and recovery. Need base-building between multiple As.
- **B priority:** Tune-ups/tests. Slight drop in training load. Test fitness, strategy, equipment, nutrition.
- **C priority:** Train right through. Just another workout.

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)

#### Three-Phase Model

**Transition Period (2-8 weeks)**
- Purpose: prepare body for harder training ahead
- Three goals: strength, conditioning, movement quality
- Weekly structure: 3 strength sessions (~60 min each), remaining time split between endurance and work capacity
- Starting volume: 50% of previous year's maximum
- Linear strength progression (add 5-10 lbs per session to compounds)
- Ends when athlete can no longer add weight linearly
- No sport-specific differences between athletes in this phase

**Base Period (many months -- the longest phase)**
- "The most important of all the training we do because it sets a good base for everything." (Ch. 3, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- Build aerobic capacity and metabolism
- Lower-effort, slower-effort training
- Slight daily fatigue is normal. Don't expect best performance.
- Focus: aerobic capacity + strength + muscular endurance
- Minimum 4 aerobic sessions weekly to see improvement
- Long workout = 30-40% of weekly aerobic volume, done at Z1 (if AeT within 10% of LT) or Z2 (if ADS)
- Back-to-back long runs in later weeks for ultra preparation: 60-80% of weekly distance/vertical on these two workouts
- Intensity introduced only after 10% test passes

**Sport-Specific (Pre-Competition) Period (measured in weeks)**
- "Sharpening period" with specificity for the goal event
- Combines fundamental qualities
- Capacity maintenance + Utilization Training
- Event-specific terrain, intensity, and duration matching

**Competition Period**
- Utilize capacity. Balance races with recovery.
- 2+ weeks between shorter races, 4-6 weeks between ultra-distance events.

#### Week Types
- **B (Base):** Foundational. Aerobic capacity, economy, strength. Long workout = 30-40% weekly volume.
- **I (Intensity):** Introduces Z3/Z4. Supplements, does not replace, base. Start Z3 at 5% weekly volume.
- **R (Recovery):** Cut to 50% total volume. No more than one intensity session. Goal: feel like attacking by Sunday.
- **S (Specificity):** Back-to-back long runs (ultra) or double Z4 sessions (VK/skimo).
- **T (Taper):** Drop volume 30%/week. Keep intensity short with long rests. Nothing new.
- **G (Goal):** Race week. Rest and relax but stay active. "Nothing you do this week will make you faster, but many things you could do will make you slower."

#### Category System
- **Category 1 (need to build base):** <400 hrs/year, under 18, or haven't completed target distance in 12 months. Priority: increase volume.
- **Category 2 (supplement base):** >400 hrs/year for 2+ years, over 18, completed target distance. Priority: specificity + intensity while maintaining Z1-Z2 volume.

#### The Three Principles of Training
1. **Continuity:** Regular schedule with minimal interruption. Lapses >5% of monthly volume need plan adjustment.
2. **Gradualness:** Progressive loads, gentle nudges not infrequent bludgeoning.
3. **Modulation:** Fluctuating load. Stress → adapt → repeat. 3:1 or 2:1 build:recovery cycles.

### Jason Koop (CTS)

#### Reverse Periodization — The Logic

Traditional road running periodization goes base → build → peak (low to high intensity).
Koop inverts this for ultras: VO2max → threshold → specificity (high to low intensity).

**Why this makes sense for ultrarunners:**

1. **Specificity, not intensity, should drive the order.** The organizing principle is
   "least specific furthest from race, most specific closest to race." For a 100-miler,
   3-minute VO2max intervals are the *least* specific workout. Long steady-state runs at
   moderate effort are the *most* specific. So VO2max work goes first, endurance last.
   "Starting off with low intensity and moving to higher intensities is a stereotypical
   — and flawed — way of organizing training, particularly for ultrarunners." (Ch. 2)

2. **The Endurance String Theory.** Koop argues that improving VO2max has a "lifting
   effect" on all systems below it — lactate threshold and aerobic endurance both
   improve when the ceiling rises. "Targeting VO2max has a greater lifting effect on
   lactate-threshold fitness and aerobic endurance than training at aerobic intensities
   has on lifting lactate threshold or VO2max." (Ch. 2) Raise the roof first, then
   furnish the rooms.

3. **Time course of adaptation favors this order.** VO2max adapts fastest (2-4 weeks),
   lactate threshold moderately (3-4 weeks), and efficiency/economy slowest (weeks to
   months). Starting with the fastest-adapting quality means you can bank those gains
   early and shift focus before the stimulus stagnates. (Seiler 2006, cited Ch. 6)

4. **VO2max is underdeveloped in most ultrarunners.** Elite ultrarunners commonly test
   with lactate threshold at 95-97% of VO2max — "this indicates underdeveloped VO2max,
   not great fitness." (Ch. 6) Many ultrarunners have trained endurance for years but
   never pushed their ceiling. "VO2max is your maximum potential — the ceiling or roof
   of the building. If you want to fit more stuff in the building, raise the roof!" (Ch. 6)

5. **Race-specific endurance decays slowest.** The aerobic and muscular endurance
   adaptations from high-volume SSR/ER work persist longer than VO2max gains. By
   placing endurance work closest to the race, those adaptations are freshest on race
   day. VO2max gains from months ago have partially decayed but the ceiling they raised
   still supports a higher threshold.

6. **Practical benefit: freshness for hard work.** Athletes do their hardest, most
   demanding sessions (VO2max intervals) when they're freshest — at the beginning of
   the cycle, not after months of accumulated volume. This means higher quality
   intervals and lower injury risk.

**When reverse periodization may NOT be best:**
- New runners (<2 years): they need aerobic base development first, not VO2max work.
  Johnston's classical approach is more appropriate.
- Athletes with ADS (if AeT/AnT spread >10%): the aerobic base is so underdeveloped
  that raising the ceiling won't help — the foundation needs building first.
- Very short timelines (<12 weeks): not enough time for the reverse progression to play
  out. Focus on specificity.

#### Three Principles of Long-Range Planning (in priority order)
1. **Most specific closest to race; least specific furthest away.** Results in plan moving from high intensity/low volume to high volume/low intensity.
2. **At some point, incorporate all three critical workouts** (SSR, TR, RI).
3. **Work strengths closer to race, weaknesses further away.**

#### Block Periodization — Why It Works

"I will generally favor a block-style training structure." (Ch. 10) Each block focuses
on ONE intensity type (RI, TR, or SSR).

**The argument for concentrated stimulus:** When you spread RI, TR, and SSR across every
week (the collegiate model), each system gets a moderate dose. For experienced athletes
who have already adapted to moderate stimuli, this may not be enough to drive further
adaptation. Block periodization concentrates the stimulus — doing RI work 2-3x/week for
3 weeks creates a much larger acute stress than doing it 1x/week for 9 weeks, even if
the total volume of RI work is identical. Research slightly favors block over mixed-
intensity for trained athletes (García-Pallarés 2010; Mølmen et al. 2019).

**Block sizing:** The lower the intensity, the longer the block can sustain. Aerobic/SSR
blocks: 3-6 weeks (up to 12). TempoRun blocks: 3-4 weeks. RI blocks: 2-4 weeks (the
most taxing, shortest duration). ~8-week macro blocks, divided into 2x4-week sub-blocks
with recovery week every 4th week.

#### Phase Duration Guidelines

| Phase | Duration | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| RunningIntervals | 2-4 weeks | Fastest adaptation; shortest phase |
| TempoRun | 3-4 weeks | Often grinds athletes down |
| SteadyStateRun | 3-6 weeks (up to 12) | Longest phase; most specific for ultras |
| Recovery | 4-6 days | 45-60 min RecoveryRuns. Don't exceed 7 days easy. |

#### Phase Transition Triggers
1. Performance declines 3-8% on 2 of last 3 workouts
2. Feel progressively worse during runs for 3 consecutive days
3. Maximum 6 weeks in any phase regardless

#### Front-Loading Within Phases

Koop prescribes the hardest and longest workouts at the BEGINNING of each phase, when
the athlete is freshest from the preceding recovery block. Volume of intensity DECREASES
as the phase progresses. This is the opposite of Johnston's progressive loading.

**The logic:** By the end of a 3-4 week block, accumulated fatigue degrades workout
quality. Rather than building toward the biggest session (when you're most tired), Koop
front-loads it (when you're most capable of executing it well). The quality of the
stimulus matters more than the progression pattern. As the block continues, the athlete
absorbs the adaptation while intensity doses get smaller and more manageable, setting up
a smooth transition into the recovery phase.

**Example TempoRun block (4 weeks):**
- Week 1 (fresh): 3x20min TR — biggest dose
- Week 2: 2x20min TR — slightly reduced
- Week 3: 2x15min TR — managing fatigue
- Week 4: Recovery — absorb adaptations

#### Transition Period
- 2-4 weeks away from running (not totally inactive -- bike, swim, climb, yoga)
- Goal: get to the point where running becomes a desire, not a compulsion

#### The Four-Year Plan
- Year 1: Learn non-running skills, gradually increase volume
- Year 2: Build physical toolbox, first long races (possibly first 100mi, but not the hardest)
- Year 3: Specialize -- fine-tune racing, test equipment and fueling
- Year 4: Pursue ambitious goals with established base

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)

#### Build/Maintain/Connect Framework (5 Rules of Training)
1. **Build and Maintain:** Easier to maintain a quality than build it. Each parameter goes through an emphasis cycle, then small maintenance doses.
2. **Never leave anything behind:** Emphasis changes but nothing is ever completely taken away.
3. **Progress everything:** Same stimulus produces less and less response over time.
4. **Balance:** Training is balancing seemingly opposing adaptations (speed vs. endurance).
5. **Individualize:** Every runner is an individual.

#### Non-Linear Periodization
- During each training period, the vast majority of training components are included; only emphasis changes.
- "Peak fitness is never incredibly far away... and peak racing can be sustained for a longer time period." (Ch. 15)
- Advantage over linear: less risk of losing qualities during extended emphasis on others.
- Study support (Quinn 2002): Base-then-intensity group improved 15% vs. 5.3% for intensity-then-base group. Supports aerobic base preceding intensity.

#### Three Training Phases
1. **Base Period:** Emphasize general endurance + general speed (strides, hill sprints). Progressively increase mileage and long run. Introduce LT work gradually. Small spices of specific work. Last 2-4 weeks: transition toward support paces.
2. **Pre-Competition Phase:** Transition emphasis to support paces. General endurance drops to maintenance. Introduce specific endurance progression. Speed endurance maintained.
3. **Competition Phase:** All about specific endurance and specific speed endurance. General/support at maintenance. Density decreases (more recovery between intense sessions). New specific workout every 10-14 days.

#### Phase Lengths (Guidelines)
- General (base): 3-8 weeks
- Support (pre-competition): 4-8 weeks
- Specific (competition): 6-10 weeks
- Can be shortened or lengthened; not rigid
- Greater lifetime training volume = less time needed in general period

#### Special Training Periods
- **Refresh periods (3-10 days):** Return focus to general or support work. Useful for prolonging peaks or mid-season maintenance. Types: aerobic refresh, anaerobic refresh, general refresh.
- **Special blocks (1-3 days):** Very intense short periods exploiting supercompensation. Double workout days or weekend blocks. Extra rest before and after.

#### Workout Progression Framework (3 Steps)
1. **Adaptation and Direction:** What adaptation is needed? What direction to progress?
2. **Build or Maintain:** Is this an emphasis period or maintenance period?
3. **Degree of Overload:** How big a stimulus jump? Generally favor very gradual increases.

#### Key Quotes
- "This is the guiding plan! Be prepared to rip it apart and adjust along the way." (Step 17 of his planning process)
- "The best coach has no distinct system; he has general principles of training but is not locked into calling himself a 'high mileage' coach for example." (Ch. 17)
- "Even the greatest training system will not work for every runner." (Ch. 17)

## Where They Agree
1. **Training should become more specific as the race approaches.** All three move toward race-specific work in the final training phase.
2. **Recovery periods between training blocks are essential.** Whether it's Johnston's 3:1 build:recovery, Koop's 4-6 day recovery phases, or Magness's refresh periods.
3. **The base/general period is critical.** All dedicate significant time to building the aerobic foundation before layering specificity.
4. **Multiple A-races require careful spacing and renewed base building between them.** 4-6 weeks minimum between ultra-distance events.
5. **Transition/off-season is necessary.** All prescribe 2-8 weeks of reduced or cross-training between major training cycles.
6. **Nothing new in the final weeks.** Race week is for maintenance, not fitness building.

## Where They Disagree

### Order of intensity types
- **Johnston:** Low to high. Base (Z1-Z2) → Z3 (only after 10% test passes) → Z4 (replacing Z3 volume). Classical periodization.
- **Koop:** High to low (reverse). RI (VO2max) first → TR (threshold) → SSR (steady-state). "Least specific to most specific."
- **Magness:** All types present in every phase; emphasis shifts. Non-linear. Base period includes strides, hill sprints, and small doses of specific work alongside high-volume easy running.

### Loading pattern within a phase
- **Johnston:** Progressive. Build volume across 3 weeks, recover on 4th week. Conservative ramp.
- **Koop:** Front-loaded. Hardest workouts at beginning of phase when freshest. Volume of intensity decreases across the phase.
- **Magness:** Flexible. Stimulus should gradually increase but placement depends on individual recovery response, not fixed schedules.

### Block vs. mixed intensity
- **Johnston:** Primarily mixed within training weeks, with intensity types introduced sequentially across phases. A week might include Z1-Z2 base work, one Z3 session, and hill sprints.
- **Koop:** Strongly favors block periodization -- one intensity type per multi-week block. Block training works because "the acute stress is bigger even when overall workload is the same."
- **Magness:** Non-linear -- multiple intensity types in every week, but emphasis changes. "During each training period, the vast majority of the training components are included."

### Length of aerobic base period
- **Johnston:** Many months. The longest phase. Category 1 athletes (under 400 hrs/year) should focus almost exclusively on volume.
- **Koop:** Aerobic base is built through total annual volume, not a dedicated base-only phase. Intensity can be included from the start if structured properly.
- **Magness:** 3-8 weeks of general/base emphasis, but it includes speed maintenance (strides, hill sprints) from week one.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **What is the athlete's experience level?**
   - New runner (<2 years, <400 hrs/year): Johnston's approach. Extended base period (months). Focus on aerobic development and general strength. No formal intensity until 10% test passes.
   - Intermediate (2-5 years, 400-600 hrs/year): Blend approaches. Use Johnston's base-first structure but incorporate Magness's "never leave anything behind" principle -- include strides, hill sprints, and small doses of faster work even during base.
   - Advanced (5+ years, >600 hrs/year, plateaued): Koop's block periodization. Concentrate intensity into focused blocks. Try front-loading within phases if traditional progressive loading has stagnated.

2. **How far out is the goal race?**
   - 24+ weeks: Full periodization cycle. Start with transition/general phase, build base, layer specificity.
   - 16-24 weeks: Abbreviated base. Can use Koop's condensed block approach (2-4 week RI → 3-4 week TR → 3-6 week SSR).
   - 8-16 weeks: Skip extended base if athlete has maintained volume. Focus on specificity and race-specific preparation.
   - Under 8 weeks: Specificity and taper only. The base is what it is.

3. **Single A-race or multiple targets?**
   - Single A-race: Full taper, full periodization arc.
   - Multiple races: Use B/C races as training (Johnston). Refresh periods between targets (Magness). Koop: manage race frequency for long-term health.

4. **Is the athlete responding to the current approach?**
   - Progressive loading working (A/B workout grades): Continue current approach.
   - Stagnating despite consistent training: Switch to Koop's block periodization with concentrated stimulus. Or add Magness's refresh periods.
   - Showing overtraining signs: Immediate recovery block. Re-evaluate phase structure. Consider 2:1 build:recovery instead of 3:1.

5. **Taper protocol for goal race:**
   - Johnston: Drop volume 30%/week for 1-3 weeks. Keep short intensity with long rests. Nothing new. Race week: recovery zone only, with 3-4x30 sec accelerations in 2 workouts.
   - Koop: Fast decay model. Week 1: 40-50% normal volume. Week 2: 20-30%. Week 3: <20%. Maintain intensity TYPE but reduce volume of intensity. Example: 4x10 min TR → 3x5 min → 2x5 min → 1x5 min.
   - Magness: Prefers sharpening over dramatic taper for distance events. Maintain or slightly drop mileage; slightly decrease volume of hard workouts; decrease density. Last week: adjust muscle tension (sprints/strides to raise, threshold to lower). Last hard workout ~10 days before peak race.
   - **For 100-mile races:** 10-14 day taper (Johnston). Fast decay (Koop). All agree: only fully taper for A-level events.

6. **Recommended phase structure for a 24-week 100-mile plan:**

   | Weeks | Johnston | Koop | Magness |
   |-------|----------|------|---------|
   | 1-4 | Transition: strength 3x/wk, easy volume at 50% | Transition: no running, cross-train | General: easy volume + strides + hill sprints |
   | 5-12 | Base: build volume, Z1-Z2, strength 2x/wk | RI block (2-4 wk) → TR block (3-4 wk) | Base: build volume, introduce LT work, maintain speed |
   | 13-18 | Base cont'd: add Z3 if 10% test passes, ME work | SSR block (3-6 wk) with recovery | Pre-competition: shift to support paces, specific endurance |
   | 19-22 | Sport-Specific: back-to-backs, terrain matching | Race-specific: high volume, terrain matching | Competition: specific endurance, reduce density |
   | 23-24 | Taper: 30%/wk volume reduction | Fast decay taper: 50% → 25% → <20% | Sharpen: maintain volume, reduce hard workout density |
