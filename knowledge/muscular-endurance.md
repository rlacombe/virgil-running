# Muscular Endurance

## Summary
Muscular endurance (ME) -- the capacity to maintain a high percentage of muscle contractile force for thousands of repetitions -- may be the most important yet most debated quality in ultrarunning. Johnston considers it a central pillar of training and credits it for coaching both 2025 UTMB winners. Koop argues that ME develops adequately through progressive, terrain-specific running and that gym-based ME circuits offer only temporary, limited gains. Magness bridges the gap with running-based strength endurance circuits inspired by Renato Canova and Arthur Lydiard.

## Key Concepts

### The Frontier Fiber Model (Johnston)
At any speed, "frontier fibers" are the last group of highest-power fibers recruited to maintain pace. Their endurance is what limits performance. When frontier fibers fatigue and drop out, you slow AND your HR drops despite increased effort -- a telltale sign of muscular rather than cardiovascular limitation.

### Hypergravity Principle (Johnston)
Adding external weight (pack, vest, or steep gradient) forces recruitment of motor units that aren't normally trained aerobically. These undertrained fibers become the limiting factor in long events. ME training specifically targets them.

### Signs You Need More ME (Johnston)
- Legs give out before breathing during hard workouts or races
- Slowing in workouts under 2 hours (not glycogen depletion)
- Heavy, dead legs that lose bounce during long efforts
- Low-level burning and acute fatigue in legs during high-intensity training
- Downhills beating up your legs disproportionately

### The Repeated Bout Effect (Koop)
Prior eccentric training provides protection against future eccentric damage. The protective effects last approximately 2 weeks. A small dose delivers a large response due to a strong dose-response relationship. This makes the final 2 weeks of training critical for maintaining climbing and descending.

### Non-Specific Lactate Accumulation (Magness)
Circuits introduce lactate via non-running-specific exercises (different muscles/recruitment patterns), avoiding the negative aerobic effects Lydiard observed from high-lactate running work. During subsequent running, muscles can take up and use the circulating lactate. "Non-specific lactate work is a great way to train not only strength endurance but also moderate lactate accumulation during the base or early pre-competition phases." (Ch. 21, *Science of Running*)

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **ME is the primary limiter in ultrarunning.** "The heart doesn't get tired in these races. What I have seen is that athletes are limited by that fatigue factor out at the muscle." (iRunFar, 2025 UTMB article)
- **"Local muscular fatigue is the single biggest determinant of performance in any endurance sport."** (TrainingPeaks, 2025)
- **A 2025 Sports Medicine paper found 66% of 824 surveyed ultrarunners cited muscular breakdown as their main limitation.**
- **ME workout should produce muscular fatigue, not breathlessness.** "The load you carry must be heavy enough that local fatigue in your legs is the limitation, not your breathing." (TrainingPeaks)
- **"I'm trying to get the same muscular effect that you'd get from VO2max intervals, but without the global fatigue cost."** (iRunFar)

#### Gym-Based ME Circuit
**Warm-up:** 200 total reps of 4-5 core exercises without stopping. Then 15 min aerobic (bike/run/stairs), finishing with 2-3 min Z3.

**Main workout (4 exercises):**
1. Box step-up: 6x10 reps each leg, 1-min rest between sets
2. Front lunge: 6x10 reps each leg, 1-min rest
3. Split jump squat: 6x10 reps each leg, 1-min rest
4. Squat jump: 6x10 reps, 1-min rest

Complete all 6 sets of each exercise. No more than 2-min rest between exercises. Tempo: ~1 rep/second.

**10-Workout Progression:**

| Workout | Weight | Sets | Reps | Rest |
|---------|--------|------|------|------|
| 1-2 | Bodyweight | 4 (book) / 6 (blog) | 10 | Standard (1 min) |
| 3-4 | +10% BW | 5 / 6 | 10 | Standard |
| 5 | +10% BW | 6 / 8 | 10 | Standard |
| 6-7 | +10% BW | 6 | 10 | 30-45 sec |
| 8 | +10% BW | 7 / 8 | 10 | 30 sec |
| 9-10 | +15-20% BW | 7 / 6 | 10 | 45 sec |

**Exercises from workout 4+:** Add goblet squat with overhead press and kettlebell swings.

#### Outdoor ME (Weighted Hiking)
- **Terrain:** 20-30% slope (steeper is better, 30-100% grades preferred)
- **Weight:** Beginners 5-10% bodyweight; intermediate 10-15%; elite 12-15% (12kg water in backpack typical for elites)
- **Protocol:** 1,000m+ vertical gain per session. Dump water at top, run down.
- **Heart rate:** May read lower than expected -- muscle fatigue (not breathlessness) should be the limiter
- **Lactate levels:** Maintained 2.5-3.5 mmol during training (Norwegian methodology)
- **Alternatives when steep terrain unavailable:** Tire-drags, stair machine sessions, pack weight on moderate grades

#### Outdoor ME (Steep Uphill Z3 Method)
- Start with 2x8-10 min uphill with 5-min moving recovery
- Build to 40 min steady uphill grinding
- Three methods to increase load: (1) steepness, (2) stride length/bounding, (3) additional weight

#### ME Timing and Frequency
- Introduce 10-16 weeks before target race
- One session per 7-10 days for coached athletes; elite may progress to 3-4 day intervals
- Separate from Z3/Z4 intervals by 72 hours
- Maintain high volume of Z1 aerobic base training during this phase
- Allow 3+ days recovery initially
- Gains appear quickly (10-20% within workouts) but depend on maintaining aerobic base
- **Critical warning:** "Replacing easy-paced recovery work with ME sessions causes initial gains followed by plateau and decline."
- Drop or reduce ME when starting Z3/Z4 interval work (those sessions will maintain ME gains)
- Award 100-150 TSS per gym ME workout for load tracking

#### 2025 UTMB Case Study
- Coached both winners: Tom Evans (19:18:58) and Ruth Croft (22:56:23)
- Weekly volume: 180-200km, 10-14km vertical gain
- Training structure: 1-2 weighted uphills, 1 downhill tempo, 1-2 strength sessions per week
- Ruth Croft achieved her highest HR only 5km from finish -- evidence that ME training preserved aerobic capacity while competitors failed
- **"If you do not feel improvement to your hill climbing from week to week... there is a good chance that you are doing too much rather than not enough."**

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **ME develops through running-specific work, not gym circuits.** Koop's approach to muscular endurance is addressed through longer intervals at SSR and TR intensity, back-to-back hard days, and matching race-specific terrain. (Ch. 4, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **High-rep circuits have limited value.** "High-repetition and low-weight strength-training exercises work only very temporarily." (Ch. 11) "After the initial adaptation phase (usually just a few short weeks), there is no further progress to be had." (Ch. 11)
- **Eccentric strength is best trained through running.** "A little eccentric training will go a long way in preventing muscular breakdown and those adaptations last a long time." (Ch. 4) Match grade and speed of training to race conditions for sufficient eccentric loading.
- **Downhill prowess is coordination, not force tolerance.** "Downhill running prowess is more about movement coordination than oxygen consumption or force tolerance." (Ch. 4, citing Minetti et al. 2002)
- **Excessive downhill repeats have diminishing returns** and require disproportionately long recovery.
- **Strength training must be heavy and purposeful, not high-rep circuits.** "Even benign glute activation exercises" vs. "bar-bending Olympic-style lifts" -- strength work must produce meaningful force output. (Ch. 11)
- **Weighted vests/packs:** Keep under 10% bodyweight or "just enough to induce a hike when you would normally run." Load actual race pack instead of buying a tactical vest. Be conservative going downhill with extra weight. Skip sleds and tires -- not sport-specific.

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Three-step strength endurance framework (Canova/Magness):**
  1. Increase maximum fibers recruited (sprints, hill sprints, heavy lifting)
  2. Improve ability to use them for prolonged time (add endurance component to strength)
  3. Learn to recruit them under high acidity (specific work under fatigue)

#### Running-Based ME Circuits
- **General strength endurance:** Hilly runs at easy to moderate speeds. Hill repeats at easy to moderate intensity. Circuits (Canova/Lydiard style): general exercises separated by runs at ~70% effort. Each circuit repetition matches the duration of an aerobic interval.
- **Uphill circuits:** Further increases strength component and fiber recruitment.
- **Long run after hard workout:** Residual fatigue forces recruitment of different/additional fibers.
- **Strength work + immediate run:** Forces fatigued muscles to be trained aerobically.
- **Non-specific lactate accumulation:** Circuits introduce lactate via non-running exercises, avoiding negative effects on aerobic abilities that Lydiard observed from high-lactate running work. During subsequent running, muscles clear the circulating lactate.

#### High Intensity Strength Endurance
- Hill repeats (200-600m) at mile effort or faster with long recoveries
- Circuit modifications: increase exercise intensity, increase connecting run intensity, or both
- Running-focused circuits: running at high intensity as main portion; exercises at medium intensity serve as "recovery" that forces fiber recruitment

#### Kick Development (Specific Strength Endurance)
- Broken 500m: 200m at 800m pace, 100m bounding, 200m kick in. 2-4 sets.
- Flat-to-hill: run fast 30 sec, sprint up hill, sprint 15-20 sec at top.
- Progression: start with 200m segments, progress to 1000m intervals.

## Where They Agree
1. **Muscular fatigue is a primary limiter in ultrarunning.** All acknowledge that legs fail before the cardiovascular system in long events.
2. **Eccentric/downhill stress must be trained specifically.** All include downhill work in preparation, though they differ on dose.
3. **Terrain specificity matters.** Training on race-similar gradients is universally endorsed.
4. **Strength endurance requires a foundation.** You must first build general strength and aerobic base before doing ME-specific work.
5. **Overloading ME work leads to diminishing returns.** All warn against excessive volume of ME training.

## Where They Disagree

### Gym-based ME vs. run-based ME
- **Johnston:** Gym-based ME circuits are a pillar of training. Weighted hiking with 10-20% bodyweight is the primary tool. The hypergravity approach targets undertrained motor units that running alone cannot reach.
- **Koop:** "High-repetition and low-weight strength-training exercises work only very temporarily." ME develops through SSR/TR intervals, terrain-specific running, and back-to-back long efforts. Gym circuits plateau after a few weeks.
- **Magness:** Bridges both approaches with running-based circuits inspired by Canova/Lydiard. Exercises are interspersed with running to create non-specific lactate accumulation and force fiber recruitment under fatigue.

### How much external weight?
- **Johnston:** 5-20% bodyweight depending on experience. 12kg (26 lbs) for elite athletes. Weight must be heavy enough that local leg fatigue is the limitation, not breathing.
- **Koop:** Under 10% bodyweight maximum. "Just enough to induce a hike when you would normally run." Skip sleds and tires.
- **Magness:** Does not emphasize external loading. Steepness and running speed provide the overload.

### Source of muscular limitation
- **Johnston:** Peripheral -- local muscular fatigue from undertrained motor units (frontier fiber model). The heart is not the limiter.
- **Koop:** Running coordination and eccentric resilience, trained through sport-specific movement at race-relevant grades and speeds.
- **Magness:** Neuromuscular -- fiber recruitment patterns, rate of force development, and the ability to maintain recruitment under acidosis. The central governor modulates output based on feedback.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Does the athlete report legs failing before lungs?**
   - Yes: ME training is a priority. Assess whether gym-based or run-based approach fits better.
   - No: Focus on aerobic base and threshold development first.

2. **Does the athlete have access to steep terrain (>20% grade)?**
   - Yes: Outdoor ME -- weighted hiking on steep grades (Johnston). Start at 5% bodyweight, progress to 10-15%.
   - No: Gym-based ME circuit (Johnston) or treadmill incline work. Alternatively, use Magness's flat/moderate circuits with exercises interspersed between running intervals.

3. **What is the athlete's experience level?**
   - Beginner (first ultra): Skip formal ME training. Build aerobic base and general strength. Race-specific terrain exposure is sufficient.
   - Intermediate (2-5 years): Introduce one ME session per 7-10 days during the last 10-16 weeks before goal race. Start with bodyweight circuits or moderate grades without weight.
   - Advanced (5+ years, multiple 100s): Full ME programming. Weighted hiking at 10-15% BW. Gym circuits with progressive overload. One session per week, maintained year-round at lower doses.

4. **When in the training cycle?**
   - Base period: General strength only. No ME-specific work.
   - 16-10 weeks out: Introduce ME sessions (one per 7-10 days).
   - 10-4 weeks out: ME sessions at peak intensity/volume (one per week).
   - 4-2 weeks out: Reduce ME to maintenance. Z3/Z4 interval work will preserve gains.
   - Taper: No ME sessions.

5. **How to monitor ME response:**
   - Improvement in uphill pace at same HR from week to week = ME is working.
   - No improvement week to week = likely doing too much, not too little (Johnston).
   - Persistent heavy legs beyond 72 hours post-ME session = reduce load or increase recovery.
   - Falling HR during hard uphill efforts (despite increasing effort) = frontier fiber fatigue. Sign that ME training is addressing the right limiter.
