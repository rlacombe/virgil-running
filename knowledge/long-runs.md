# Long Runs & Specificity

## Summary
The long run is the cornerstone workout for ultramarathon preparation, but its value depends on how it is structured. All three experts agree that duration matters more than distance, that terrain should match race demands, and that ultrarunning requires mastery of four distinct locomotion disciplines. Where they diverge is on execution: Johnston emphasizes time-on-feet at low intensity with terrain-specific vertical, Koop introduces the feet-per-mile gradient concept and concentrated volume through back-to-back long runs, and Magness focuses on how long runs recruit otherwise dormant muscle fibers and train fuel systems. For an ultrarunner, getting the long run right is arguably more important than any interval session.

## Key Concepts

### Time-on-Feet vs. Pace
- Long run adaptation depends on duration at a given effort, not the pace or distance covered.
- A 5-mile run can take 1, 2, or even 3 hours depending on terrain -- distance alone is meaningless.
- Physiological stimulus is driven by time spent working, not miles ticked off.

### The Four Disciplines of Ultrarunning
1. **Flat running** -- standard biomechanics, 2.5-3x body weight ground reaction forces (GRFs)
2. **Uphill running** -- higher cardiovascular response, lower GRFs, greater muscle recruitment
3. **Downhill running** -- eccentric loading, higher GRFs than flat at equivalent effort, coordination-dependent
4. **Power-hiking/walking** -- only 1.2x body weight GRFs, most economical below ~13:30-14:00 min/mi

Each discipline has distinct GRFs, EMG patterns, and joint angles. Training must explicitly address each one.

### Terrain Matching and Specificity
- Training terrain should model race terrain as closely as possible.
- The metric that matters is not total vertical gain but **feet of elevation change per mile** -- matching the gradient ratio of the goal event.
- Example: Western States = (18,090 + 22,970) / 100.2 = **410 ft/mile**.

### Back-to-Back Long Runs
- Compress training load into consecutive days to create greater cumulative fatigue from the same total weekly hours.
- Give a similar training effect to one very long day but with reduced injury risk and shorter recovery.
- For 100-mile prep: back-to-back 8-10 hour days in Z1-Z2, scheduled 3-4 weeks before the race.

### Minimum Peak Long Run Duration
- No magic distance automatically qualifies an athlete. Long run should be at least 25% of anticipated race time (in hours).
- Weekly volume for several weeks should be at least 50% of anticipated race time.
- Minimum 4 hours for practicing nutrition and fueling strategies.

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Time-on-feet philosophy:** Long runs focus on "mountaineering" efforts -- time-on-feet, not pace. Duration at equivalent RPE should be the goal. "Should leave you tired from the duration, not the intensity." (Ch. 10, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **Zone guidance:** Done at Z1 if AeT is within 10% of LT, or at Z2 if the athlete has ADS. For 100km+ races, Z2 dominates 62-76% of race time despite comprising only ~40% of course distance. (https://evokeendurance.com/resources/training-for-mountain-running/)
- **Weekly proportion:** One long workout per week = 30-40% of weekly aerobic training volume. (Ch. 10)
- **Back-to-back specificity weeks:** Used in later weeks of the plan for ultra prep. Aim for 60-80% of weekly distance/vertical on these two workouts. (Ch. 10)
- **Peak weekly distances for mountain races:**
  - 50K: peak week 50-55 mi, avg for biggest 12 weeks = 36 mi
  - 100K: peak week 74-80 mi, avg for biggest 16 weeks = 48 mi
  - 100-mile: peak week 90-100 mi, avg for biggest 18 weeks = 75 mi
- **Terrain specificity:** "You will not learn to play Chopin by practicing 'Chopsticks.'" (Ch. 2) Running on flat surfaces hardwires the nervous system for flat-surface motor patterns -- must run extensive downhills and rough terrain to build proficiency.
- **Vertical as separate load:** Start with no more than 1/3 to 1/2 of goal event's total vertical in first week. Plan to hit goal event's total vertical during at least 2 late Base Period weeks. Cut vertical by 40-60% during recovery weeks.
- **Mountain running requires four locomotion techniques:** flat running, uphill running, downhill running, uphill walking. "Mountain running and road running are, at best, distant cousins." (https://evokeendurance.com/resources/training-for-mountain-running/)
- **Heart rate on downhills:** "Don't worry that your heart rate drops on the downhills... The downhills provide a valuable strengthening effect in mountain sports." (Ch. 5)

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Track by TIME, not miles:** "A time-based method of capturing volume is more consistent compared to a mile-based method." (Ch. 3, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*) "A five-mile run can take you one, two, or even three hours depending on the terrain." (Ch. 9)
- **Match feet-per-mile gradient:** Calculate race's average elevation change per mile: (total gain + total loss) / total miles. "If you attempt to match this average elevation change per mile in a week's worth of training, you'll be on the right path." (Ch. 5) Can break the course into major sections and find local analogues.
- **Four disciplines:** Koop treats ultrarunning as four distinct sports -- walking, uphill running, downhill running, and flat running -- each requiring separate training focus. GRFs, EMG patterns, and joint segment angles are all distinct. (Ch. 4-5)
- **Concentrated volume:** Rather than adding more weekly hours, rearrange existing volume by clustering hard efforts and long runs closer together. Back-to-back long runs create "essentially worth more benefit in the entirety of the training picture." Research shows concentrated block training produces bigger physiological improvements (VO2max, threshold pace) than evenly spread plans with the same total workload. (https://trainright.com/concentrated-training-volume-for-ultrarunners/)
- **Long run length guidance:** Long run should be at least 25% of anticipated race time. Weekly volume for several weeks should be at least 50% of anticipated race time. Minimum 4 hours for nutrition practice. (https://trainright.com/longest-run-ultramarathon-training/)
- **Minimum maximum concept:** 50K/50mi: minimum of 6 hrs/week for 3 weeks starting 6 weeks before race. 100K/100mi: minimum of 9 hrs/week for 6 weeks starting 9 weeks before race. (Ch. 9)
- **Uphill intervals -- 80% are uphill:** Higher cardiovascular response, hedge against injury (slower speed = lower peak GRFs), more time climbing than descending in ultras. (Ch. 5)
- **When to hike vs. run:** Flat ground transition at ~13:30-14:00 min/mi. Use RPE during race: hike when RPE exceeds race plan, run when RPE is in desired range. "Something I don't think enough runners do" -- practice walking in training. (Ch. 5)
- **Annual volume emphasis:** "If you ask me what you should do to improve your performance, I'll almost always say 'train more over long periods of time.' The 'over long periods of time' piece is important, because it de-emphasizes the single long run or single-week volume in favor of annual volume." (Ch. 10)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Fiber recruitment training:** Early portions of long runs use easy-to-recruit slow-twitch fibers. As duration extends, these fatigue and additional fast-twitch fibers are recruited and trained aerobically. "If a run lasts long enough, then you will eventually get to the point where you are training fibers aerobically that seldom get recruited in such a way." (Ch. 18, *The Science of Running*)
- **Fuel system training:** The long run is the ONLY workout that stresses total fuel supply enough to trigger glycogen storage increases and fat utilization improvements. Depleting glycogen triggers AMPK signaling, leading to mitochondrial biogenesis. (Ch. 9, Ch. 18)
- **Add "stuff" to long runs:** Surges and strides during long runs ensure most fiber types are recruited. Pickups and progressions change the fuel system by depleting stores in the first portion then stressing the body in the second half. "We are using the first half or 3/4 of the run to pre-fatigue and then running the last portion at a faster pace." (Ch. 18)
- **Long run after hard workout:** Creates residual fatigue, forcing recruitment of different/additional muscle fibers. Useful for strength endurance development. Training in glycogen-depleted state enhances gene transcription of adaptation markers (AMPK, MAPK signaling). (Ch. 18, citing Hansen 2005; Yeo et al. 2008)
- **Structural preparation:** "Structurally the muscles, bones, and joints adapt to the pounding necessary to run 26.2 miles. With each long run, those structures get more resistant to fatigue from the pounding." (Ch. 18)
- **Specificity principle:** "Running at race pace is the only way to mimic the exact muscle fiber recruitment pattern and fatiguing by-product buildup that will occur in a race." (Ch. 15) But specific training alone is insufficient -- other paces build the foundation.
- **Stride length as performance predictor:** "The decrease of stride length during races is one of the most powerful predictors of race results." (Ch. 6) ME training greatly improves ability to ward off late-race neuromuscular fatigue.

## Where They Agree
1. **Duration over distance.** All three measure long run value by time, not miles. A 5-hour run on technical mountain terrain is more valuable for a mountain ultra than a 30-mile run on flat roads.
2. **Terrain specificity is non-negotiable.** Training terrain must match race terrain. Flat running does not prepare the neuromuscular system for mountains, and vice versa.
3. **Four distinct locomotion patterns.** All acknowledge that walking, uphill running, downhill running, and flat running are biomechanically distinct and require separate practice.
4. **The long run builds structural resilience.** Tendons, ligaments, bones, and connective tissue adapt through sustained duration at moderate effort.
5. **Downhill training matters but is efficient.** A small dose of eccentric loading provides significant protection. No one advocates high-volume downhill repeat sessions.
6. **Annual volume trumps single-session heroics.** Consistency over months matters more than any individual long run.

## Where They Disagree

### Intensity During Long Runs
- **Johnston:** Strict Z1-Z2 effort. The long run should make you tired from the duration, not the intensity. Progressive distance runs (with a Z3-Z4 finish) are separate workouts, not standard long runs.
- **Koop:** Primarily EnduranceRun (RPE 5-6) effort but incorporates intervals within long runs (e.g., "2-hr ER with 3x10 min TR"). Intensity is embedded into endurance sessions.
- **Magness:** Adds "stuff" -- surges, strides, pickups, progressions -- to long runs for fiber recruitment and fuel system benefits. Uses first portion to pre-fatigue, then runs the last portion faster.

### Role of the Single Long Run
- **Koop:** De-emphasizes the single long run in favor of annual volume and concentrated volume strategies (back-to-back days). No magic distance qualifies an athlete.
- **Johnston:** Values back-to-back specificity weeks but also prescribes individual long runs of 30-40% of weekly volume as a standard weekly feature.
- **Magness:** Views the long run as uniquely important for fuel system adaptation -- the only workout stressing glycogen stores enough to trigger storage increases. Advocates glycogen-depleted training for enhanced adaptation signals.

### Fueling During Long Runs
- **Magness:** Some long runs should be done without carbohydrate intake to maximize glycogen depletion signaling and mitochondrial biogenesis. Consuming carbs during a long run may blunt adaptation signals.
- **Koop:** Emphasizes gut training -- practice race-day nutrition 4-6 times during training. The long run is the primary venue for rehearsing fueling.
- **Johnston:** Recommends metabolic flexibility training with some fasted aerobic capacity-building workouts but warns against fasted training for female athletes.

## Coaching Decision Tree

### Step 1: Determine Event Demands
- Calculate feet-per-mile gradient: (total gain + total loss) / total miles
- Identify dominant discipline (flat vs. mountain vs. mixed)
- Determine expected race duration

### Step 2: Set Long Run Parameters
- **Duration target:** Minimum 25% of anticipated race time; build to this progressively
- **Weekly volume target:** At least 50% of anticipated race time for several weeks during peak training
- **Terrain:** Match feet-per-mile gradient of the goal event; break course into sections if needed

### Step 3: Select Approach Based on Athlete Profile

**Newer athletes (< 2 years ultra experience, CTL < 40):**
- Follow Johnston: strict Z1-Z2 effort, focus on time-on-feet without intensity
- Build structural resilience first; add "stuff" later
- No back-to-backs until comfortable with single long run at 4+ hours
- Practice walking/hiking on uphills from the start

**Intermediate athletes (2-4 years, CTL 40-70):**
- Blend approaches: mostly Z1-Z2 with occasional surges/strides (Magness)
- Introduce back-to-back long runs 2x/month during build phase (Koop/Johnston)
- Alternate fueled and partially-fasted long runs for dual adaptation
- Practice all four disciplines weekly

**Experienced athletes (4+ years, CTL > 70):**
- Follow Koop's concentrated volume model: cluster long runs and hard efforts
- Embed intervals within long runs (e.g., SSR or TR efforts during endurance runs)
- Use back-to-back days as primary specificity tool, 3-4 weeks before race
- Long runs serve dual purpose: structural prep + nutrition rehearsal

### Step 4: Downhill Training Within Long Runs
- **Small doses are effective.** The repeated bout effect provides protection lasting ~2 weeks from a single session of eccentric loading. (Koop, Ch. 4)
- **Match grade and speed to race.** If the race has 15% grade descents at 8:00/mi, practice those specific conditions. (Koop)
- Insert short (1-2 min) high-speed downhill reps into weekly easy-distance workouts. Increase number and duration over weeks. (Johnston, Ch. 5)
- Include 2 workouts per week with fast downhill running pick-ups during Base Period. (Johnston)
- Do NOT perform RunningIntervals downhill -- "it will be nearly impossible to achieve >90% of your VO2max in this condition." (Koop, Ch. 9)
- Heart rate will drop on downhills during hilly aerobic workouts -- this is normal. Do not try to keep HR in zone by thrashing yourself on descents. Count full time (both up and down) for hilly workouts. (Johnston, Ch. 5)
- Vary stride/foot-strike pattern during race to spread muscular damage across different muscle groups. Trail terrain creates this naturally; flat courses benefit more from conscious variation. (Koop, Ch. 4)

### Step 5: Walk/Hike Training
- Practice power-hiking in training -- most ultrarunners neglect this discipline. "Something I don't think enough runners do." (Koop, Ch. 5)
- Flat ground transition: ~13:30-14:00 min/mi -- below this, walking is more economical; above this, running is more economical. (Koop, Ch. 5)
- No universal grade exists for the walk-to-run transition -- it depends on fitness, fatigue, and terrain.
- Use RPE during races: hike when RPE exceeds race plan, run when RPE is in desired range.
- Johnston requires mastery of uphill walking as one of the four locomotion techniques. (https://evokeendurance.com/resources/training-for-mountain-running/)
- Weighted hiking (10-15% body weight) is both ME training and walking practice -- dual benefit. (Johnston)

### Step 6: Monitor and Adjust
- If stride length collapses late in long runs: increase ME training (Johnston approach)
- If HR decoupling exceeds 5% in second half: long run effort was too high
- If athlete dreads long runs: reduce intensity, add variety (new terrain), ensure adequate recovery
- If GI issues emerge: reduce intensity, increase gut training frequency (Koop)
- If athlete consistently cannot complete planned duration: reduce target by 20%, rebuild gradually
- Track workout grades (Johnston A-F system): consistent C grades on long runs = fatigue accumulation, insert recovery week
