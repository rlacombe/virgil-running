# Strength Training

## Summary
Strength training for ultrarunners is the most polarizing topic among the three primary coaches. Johnston treats it as an essential progression from general strength through maximum strength to muscular endurance, integrated throughout the training year. Koop is the most skeptical, arguing that rest and sleep provide equal or better injury prevention benefits at lower cost to the training budget. Magness focuses on neuromuscular coordination -- hill sprints, plyometrics, and strides -- as the most efficient way to improve fiber recruitment and running economy without the overhead of traditional gym work.

## Key Concepts

### Strength vs. Endurance Paradox
Endurance sports typically utilize only about 25% of an athlete's maximum single-rep strength, but for thousands of repetitions. Gaining strength without adding mass is critically important. Strength is largely neurologic; endurance is largely metabolic. (Johnston, Ch. 6, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)

### Concurrent Training Effect
Heavy strength training alongside endurance training does not cause hypertrophy (Blagrow et al. 2017, 24-40 week studies cited by Koop). The interference effect goes the other direction -- endurance training suppresses muscle growth pathways. This means ultrarunners can lift heavy without gaining unwanted mass.

### Running Economy Improvement
Strength training improves running economy by approximately 2-4% (Balsalobre-Fernandez et al. 2016, cited by Koop). This is meaningful but must be weighed against time cost and recovery impact.

### Fiber Recruitment Is Force-Based
"Muscle recruitment is dependent not on speed or intensity, although they are related, but on force output required." Running slightly slower UP A HILL activates MORE muscle than running on flat at a slightly faster pace. During maximal voluntary contractions, a person can NEVER fully recruit all fibers -- there is always a reserve. Well-trained athletes tap into a higher percentage. (Magness, Ch. 17, *Science of Running*)

### Rate of Force Development
Training rate of force development allows: (1) working at lower percentage of max at slow speeds (extending endurance), (2) generating necessary forces at race speeds, (3) reducing ground contact times. Explosive strength training enhanced rapid muscle activation in endurance athletes (Mikkola 2007, cited by Magness).

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **"Athletes strength train to improve performance in their event and for injury prevention, not to become stronger in the gym."** (Ch. 6, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **"Don't devote precious hours to a bench press program when your goal is to improve your vertical kilometer time."** (Uphill Athlete blog)
- **Never train athletes to lift more than 2x bodyweight** in compound lifts.
- **Sufficient strength benchmarks:** Half squat with bodyweight on shoulders = minimum. Squatting 2x bodyweight probably adds nothing to performance.

#### Three-Phase Progression

**Phase 1 -- General Strength (Transition Period, 4-12 weeks)**
- Purpose: establish neuromuscular patterns, correct imbalances, build foundation
- Frequency: 2x/week
- Format: circuit of 8 exercises (Turkish Get-Up, Push-Up, Split Bench Squat, Dips, Box Step-Ups, Pull-Ups, Squat, Hanging Leg Raise)
- Rest: 30 sec between movements, 3 min between circuit laps
- Progression: start with 1 circuit if new, 2 if experienced. Add one lap every two weeks, up to 4 circuits by week 8.
- High reps, low loads

**Assessment gates before progressing:**
1. Air Squat Test: maintain knee alignment over toes
2. Box Step Test: step up on knee-height box without knee collapse >1 inch
3. Sit-to-Stand Test: rise from seated using one leg without knee collapse >1 inch
4. If all pass: move to specific strength

**General Strength Stages (Legs and Hips):**
- Stage 1 (remedial): squats with knee band, single-leg lateral band exercises. 4x20 reps.
- Stage 2 (functional single-leg): box step-up, step-down, front lunge. 3x15 each leg. Progress to 3x30 with perfect knee control.
- Stage 3 (weighted single-leg): same exercises plus weight. 4x6 reps at weight allowing just 8 reps with perfect form. 2-min rest.

**Phase 2 -- Max Strength (Base Period, weeks 8-16)**
- Purpose: teach existing fibers to work coordinatively without adding mass. Strength is neurologic.
- 4 multijoint free-weight exercises as 2 couplets
- Sets: 6 sets progressing from 4 reps down to 2 reps (4, 4, 3, 3, 2, 2)
- Rest: 30 sec within couplet, 2 min between couplets
- Weight increases as reps drop (example: pull-ups 10kg/12kg/15kg; step-ups 30kg/40kg/45kg)
- Intensity: 75-90% of failure on the last rep -- never to complete failure
- Frequency: 2x/week

**Phase 3 -- Muscular Endurance (Post-max, 4-8 weeks)**
- See muscular-endurance.md for detailed protocols
- One ME session per week
- Maintain during intensity phases at 1x/week maintenance

**Hill Sprints / Bounding (Specific Strength, Base Period)**
- Hill grade: 20% minimum (50-60% optimal)
- 6-8 reps of 8-10 sec max efforts
- 2 min easy walk down between reps, 3 min between sets
- Start at 80-85% effort first 2-3 workouts. Real risk of injury.
- Progress by adding 10% bodyweight via vest/pack or steeper terrain
- Frequency: 1-2x/week in Base Period, then maintenance every 12-14 days
- Add 2 reps per week. Never extend beyond 10 seconds per rep.

**Core training:** 2x/week, ~10 exercises. Core muscles are "composed almost entirely of slow twitch fibers."

#### Scheduling
- Transition Period: 3x/week strength (~60 min each)
- Base Period: 2x/week max strength + 1x/week hill sprints
- Sport-Specific Period: 1x/week ME + 1x/week maintenance strength
- During intensity phases: 1x/week maintenance

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **"Not all ultramarathon runners are good candidates for strength training."** (Ch. 11, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **Uses a flowchart** to determine candidacy -- considers available time, injury history, training status.
- **Sits near the TOP of his hierarchy** (marginal gains), not the base. "Training stimuli enhancement" including strength is one of the highest (least important) tiers.

#### The Cost-Benefit Calculus
- Running economy improvement: 2-4% (meaningful but modest)
- Injury reduction from strength: ~66% (Lauersen 2018 meta-analysis)
- Injury reduction from sleep >8 hrs: ~61% (von Rosen et al. 2016) -- comparable
- "Adding two hours of strength training per week -- that's two hours you could have spent simply resting." (Ch. 11)
- Rest (more sleep or more rest days) provides "a five or ten fold advantage" over strength training for injury prevention.
- **"The research on strength training specifically to prevent injury is really weak."** (trainright.com)

#### When Koop Prescribes Strength
Five movement patterns:
1. Push: push-ups, bench press
2. Pull: rows, pull-ups
3. Hinge: Romanian deadlift
4. Squat: squat, lunge, reverse lunge
5. Carry: farmer's carry, woodchoppers, medicine ball slams

**Seasonal periodization:**

| Season | Sets x Reps | Weight | Notes |
|--------|-------------|--------|-------|
| Early (months 1-3) | 1-5 x 1-5 | Heavy | Build foundations; run volume low |
| Mid (months 4-7) | 3 x 8-12 | Moderate (leave 1-2 reps in tank) | Run intensity/volume increasing |
| Late (months 8-9) | Corrective exercises only | Light/bodyweight | All resources to race-specific demands |

**Scheduling:** Place strength days as far as possible from next long/hard run. Pair on evenings of hard run days (run first, lift second). 2 days/week max; 3 days "normally I don't advise." Always prioritize run training.

#### Corrective Exercise Training
- Low effort cost, little impact on run quality
- Target: posterior chain (glutes, hips, hamstrings), weak feet, hip/pelvic stability
- Up to 3 days/week, 2-3 sets of 12 reps or 15-30 sec
- Seek qualified PT if constantly injured

#### Isometric Training
- Increases muscle fiber recruitment and strengthens tendons
- Practical for injury prevention
- Lower recovery cost than dynamic lifting

#### Aging Athletes
- "For aging athletes, strength training isn't just corrective or ergogenic, it's essential for general health and well-being." (Ch. 11)
- Age-related muscle mass loss starts >40, accelerates >60
- "Shift some priority away from running time and toward moving heavy objects a few times a week." (Ch. 11)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Neuromuscular coordination is the key adaptation.** Better 10km performance was partially due to increased pre-activation and decreased ground contact time (Paavolainen 1999). Pre-activation tightens the muscle-tendon system, increasing elastic energy storage.
- **Sprint, hill sprint, and plyometric work increases total recruitable muscle fiber pool.** "The general strength needed can be developed by sprint, plyometric, and explosive power/ballistic training." (Ch. 21) After increasing pool, MUST extend endurance of those fibers or gains are useless.
- **Rate of force development matters more than maximum force.** "Weyend demonstrated that we do not have time to reach max force levels even during sprinting." (Ch. 8)
- **Running is a skill that needs to be learned and refined.** (Ch. 1) Strides at 100-150m at 5k to mile pace, post-run, maintain speed and reinforce good biomechanics.

#### Hill Sprints
- 8-10 seconds maximum power on 30%+ grade (same protocol as Johnston)
- Start with 6-8 reps, add 2 per week
- 2-3 min recovery between reps
- Start at 80-85% effort first 2-3 sessions
- Purpose: increase fiber pool, train rate of force development, neuromuscular coordination
- One per week during base period

#### CNS Fatigue (Often Overlooked)
- "As distance coaches we are used to recognizing fatigue that results from running lots of mileage or hard workouts. What we don't grasp is CNS fatigue." (Ch. 8)
- Sprint and plyometric work creates CNS fatigue different from metabolic fatigue
- Must implement specific recovery strategy, just like recovery from a long run

#### Strides as Neuromuscular Maintenance
- 100-150m at 5k to mile pace, post-run
- Functions: maintain speed, reinforce biomechanics, recruit FT fibers, alter muscle tension
- Can be done very frequently, especially during base
- Low metabolic cost, high neuromuscular benefit

#### Muscle Stiffness and Running Economy
- "Contrary to popular belief, a stiffer muscle is more efficient during running than a flexible one." (Ch. 7)
- Muscle stiffness strongly correlates with Running Economy (Dalleau et al. 1998)
- Over-flexibility may reduce elastic energy storage and return
- Pre-activation of muscles before landing adjusts stiffness, acts as internal cushioning, decreases stress from muscular vibration

## Where They Agree
1. **Strength training should serve running, not replace it.** No one advocates gym work at the expense of run volume.
2. **Hill sprints are valuable for all ultrarunners.** Johnston and Magness prescribe nearly identical protocols (8-10 sec max efforts on 30%+ grade, 6-8 reps, ample recovery).
3. **Heavy beats high-rep for force production.** When strength training is prescribed, heavier weights with lower reps produce better outcomes than light weight circuits (Johnston's max strength phase, Koop's early season prescription).
4. **Core stability is foundational.** All three include core work as baseline maintenance.
5. **Single-leg strength matters.** Running is a single-leg propulsion sport. All include single-leg exercises.
6. **Strength training should be scheduled carefully relative to hard runs.** Never before key sessions. Best paired on the same day as hard runs (run first, lift second) or on easy days.

## Where They Disagree

### Importance and priority
- **Johnston:** Essential pillar. Three-phase progression is a core element of the annual plan. 2-3x/week during base, 1x/week during intensity phases.
- **Koop:** Marginal gain near the top of his hierarchy. Rest and sleep offer comparable or better injury prevention. Not all ultrarunners are candidates. 2x/week max; late season corrective exercises only.
- **Magness:** Important for neuromuscular coordination but best achieved through running-integrated work (hill sprints, strides, plyometrics) rather than traditional gym sessions.

### Gym-based strength vs. running-integrated strength
- **Johnston:** Full gym progression. General strength circuits, max strength couplets, ME circuits -- all in the weight room.
- **Koop:** If prescribed, use 5 movement patterns with traditional sets/reps. But questions whether the time cost is justified vs. simply running more and resting more.
- **Magness:** Prefers hill sprints, plyometrics, and running-based circuits. Gym work is secondary to neuromuscular training done during or after runs.

### Injury prevention mechanism
- **Johnston:** Structural -- stronger muscles, tendons, and ligaments prevent overuse injuries. General → max → ME phases build progressive resilience.
- **Koop:** "The research on strength training specifically to prevent injury is really weak." Rest (more sleep, more rest days) is more efficacious and offers "more bang for your buck."
- **Magness:** Neuromuscular -- improved pre-activation, muscle stiffness, and elastic energy storage reduce impact forces. Stiffer muscles are more efficient and more resilient.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Is the athlete new to running (<2 years)?**
   - Yes: Johnston's Phase 1 general strength is essential. Use assessment tests (air squat, box step, sit-to-stand) to identify weaknesses. 2x/week general strength circuit + core work. No max strength until all assessment gates pass.

2. **Does the athlete have recurring injuries?**
   - Yes: Assess sleep (target >8 hrs, per Koop). Address energy balance (prevent RED-S). Add corrective exercises targeting posterior chain (Koop). If injury pattern persists, consider Johnston's assessment-based progression. Always recommend professional evaluation.

3. **How much time does the athlete have?**
   - Time-limited (<8 hrs/week total): Prioritize running volume. Use Magness approach -- hill sprints 1x/week, strides post-run 3-4x/week, core 2x/week. Skip gym sessions.
   - Moderate (8-12 hrs/week): Johnston's approach is viable. 2x/week strength in base period, reducing to 1x/week as intensity increases.
   - Time-rich (>12 hrs/week): Full Johnston progression is possible. Can include gym-based max strength and ME work alongside high-volume running.

4. **What phase of training?**
   - Transition/Off-season: Highest strength training priority for all three coaches. 2-3x/week.
   - Base period: 2x/week strength (Johnston: max strength phase; Koop: heavy 1-5x1-5; Magness: hill sprints + circuits).
   - Build/Intensity: Reduce to 1x/week maintenance. Schedule after hard runs or on easy days.
   - Peak/Taper: Johnston: cut hill sprints by 30%/week. Koop: corrective exercises only. All: no new strength stimuli.

5. **Is the athlete over 40?**
   - Yes: Strength training shifts from optional to essential. "For aging athletes, strength training isn't just corrective or ergogenic, it's essential for general health and well-being." (Koop, Ch. 11) Minimum 2x/week year-round, even at the expense of some running volume. Focus on heavy compound movements to counter muscle mass loss.
