# Volume & Load Management

## Summary
Volume is the single most agreed-upon driver of ultrarunning performance. All three experts place it at or near the foundation of training, but they differ significantly on how fast to add it, how to structure recovery, and whether the traditional 10% rule is useful or an oversimplified myth. Getting volume management wrong leads to overtraining, injury, or stagnation -- getting it right builds the engine that powers everything else.

## Key Concepts

### Volume Measurement
- **Johnston:** Uses hours, miles, and vertical as three separate load variables. All three must be tracked and progressed independently.
- **Koop:** Insists on tracking volume by TIME, not miles. "A five-mile run can take you one, two, or even three hours depending on the terrain." (Ch. 9, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **Magness:** Tracks weekly mileage but acknowledges time-based tracking for trail athletes.

### CTL (Chronic Training Load) / ATL (Acute Training Load) / TSB
- CTL = 42-day rolling weighted average of daily TSS = fitness proxy
- ATL = 7-day rolling weighted average = fatigue proxy
- TSB = CTL minus ATL = form/freshness
- Maximum safe CTL increase: 3-5 points per week (Johnston/Uphill Athlete)
- CTL should peak just before goal event (Koop)
- TSB target for race day: +5 to +15 (fresh but not detrained)

### The 10% Rule
The traditional guideline that weekly volume should increase no more than 10% per week. Johnston treats it as a maximum. Magness calls it an oversimplification. Koop uses phase-based load management instead of weekly percentage increases.

### Build:Recovery Ratios
The standard cycle of loading weeks followed by a recovery week to allow adaptation. Most commonly 3:1 (three build weeks, one recovery week), sometimes 2:1 for higher loads or older athletes.

### Minimum Maximum Concept (Koop)
The minimum sustained volume needed for successful race completion at a given distance. For 50K/50mi: 6 hrs/week for 3 weeks starting 6 weeks out. For 100K/100mi: 9 hrs/week for 6 weeks starting 9 weeks out.

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **The 10% rule is a maximum, not a target.** "Greater than an average of 10 percent progression in volume leads to trouble in roughly eight weeks." (Uphill Athlete blog)
- **Track distance, vertical, and time increases independently** -- all at 10% or less per week.
- **Do not increase distance and vertical for more than 3 weeks in a row.**
- **Do not increase distance, vertical, AND intensity in a single week.**
- **If increasing 2 variables, keep total increases under 15%** (e.g., 10% distance + 5% vertical).
- **Recovery weeks: drop volume 40-60%** of the biggest week's volume. After intentional overreaching, drop as much as 70%.
- **3:1 build:recovery cycle is standard.** Some athletes need 2:1.
- **Starting volume after a break >4 weeks:** Begin at 50% of previous cycle's average weekly hours. Do not accelerate faster than 7-10% per week.
- **Maximum safe CTL increase: 3-5 points per week.** To reach CTL of 50 takes minimum 10 weeks.
- **No more than one intensity session during a recovery week.**
- **Annual volume guidelines by experience:**
  - Beginners (<350-400 hrs/year): can increase annual load by up to 25%
  - Advanced (>500 hrs/year): max 10% jump in yearly volume
  - Elite: often no change in annual volume; gains come from specificity and intensity distribution
- **Starting annual volume targets by age:**
  - Juniors (15 and under): 300 hours
  - Juniors (16-19): 450 hours
  - Adults 20-35: 400 hours
  - Adults 35-50: 350 hours
  - Over 50: 300 hours
- **Kilian's volume history:** Started at 600 hrs/year at age 13, added ~10% per year, stabilized at 1,000-1,300 hrs/year. "If you want to increase to this high number of training hours, you have to increase no more than 10 percent a year for several years." (Ch. 3)
- **Reference athlete (nationally competitive, full-time job):** 12.5 hrs/week average, 55 mi/week, 9,843 ft vertical/week, 650 hrs/year.
- **Key quote:** "If you can't do the same workout you did yesterday again today, and tomorrow and the day after for days on end, then you are doing too much." (Ch. 5)
- **Key quote:** "It is far better to be slightly undertrained than to be even a little overtrained." (Ch. 4)

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Volume is the base of the hierarchy.** "If you ask me what you should do to improve your performance, I'll almost always say 'train more over long periods of time.'" (Ch. 10, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **The "over long periods of time" caveat is critical** -- it de-emphasizes single long runs or single-week volume in favor of annual volume.
- **Most CTS athletes average 7-10 hours per week**, though some train 15+.
- **Progressive overload within a phase is wrong.** Koop explicitly opposes the traditional model of increasing volume each week within a training block. Instead, do the hardest/longest workouts at the BEGINNING of the phase when freshest. "When athletes apply this type of weekly progressive workload training model, they end up attempting the hardest and/or longest workouts when they are most fatigued." (Ch. 12)
- **Concentrated volume beats distributed volume.** Rather than adding more weekly hours, rearrange existing volume by clustering hard efforts and long runs into fewer days. Researchers found concentrated block training produced bigger physiological improvements (VO2max, threshold pace/power) from the same total workload. (trainright.com)
- **Minimum maximum concept:**
  - 50K/50mi: 6 hrs/week sustained for 3 weeks, starting 6 weeks before race
  - 100K/100mi: 9 hrs/week sustained for 6 weeks, starting 9 weeks before race
  - Outside these windows, lower volume is fine if quality is high.
- **Detraining resilience:** Cutting training to ~50% (3 sessions/week) causes only 5-10% performance decline even over several weeks. Complete cessation: >20% decline.
- **Recovery phase architecture:** 4-6 days of 45-60 min RecoveryRuns, maybe an extra rest day. Don't exceed 7 days easy -- beyond that, fitness decay outweighs recovery benefit.
- **Phase transition triggers:**
  1. Performance declines 3-8% on 2 of last 3 workouts
  2. Feel progressively worse during runs for 3 consecutive days
  3. Maximum 6 weeks in any phase regardless
- **Elite volume reference:** Jim Walmsley 2018: 745 hours, 926,906 ft vert. Courtney Dauwalter 2018: 719 hours, 453,000 ft vert.
- **Long run guideline:** No magic distance. Successful ultramarathoners have long runs ranging from 20-80% of race distance. Minimum 25% of anticipated race time (hours). Weekly volume for several weeks should be at least 50% of anticipated race time.

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Throw out the 10% rule.** "A so-called 10% rule for increasing mileage has developed as a sort of law of training. Throw it out. Just like in adapting to interval training, weights, or any other stimulus, the individuality of what a runner can handle varies a lot." (Ch. 18)
- **Context-dependent volume increases:**
  1. Safe to rapidly return to any level consistently maintained in the past 6-12 months. Example: peaked at 100 mpw, dropped to 70, took 2 weeks off -- can increase to 80-90 mpw in ~3 weeks.
  2. When venturing into new territory: add one mile per day of running.
  3. Factors: injury history, biomechanics, structural build, nutrition (calcium levels).
- **Career-long volume progression is essential.** Total volume should progressively increase throughout a career until maximizing potential. Once a large volume base exists, mileage can decrease while specific intensity increases.
- **African runner advantage:** Large volumes of "unofficial" training (running to school, active lifestyle) create a foundation tens of thousands of miles ahead of Western counterparts. (Ch. 19, Joe Vigil quote)
- **Recovery runs enhance supercompensation.** Short split doubles (e.g., 4mi + 4mi instead of 8mi) are beneficial: shorter runs minimize damage, easier glycogen restoration, and growth hormone release occurs twice. GH increases ~550% from 0-40 min of easy running, then only 40-50% more from 40-60 min. (Ch. 19)
- **"Without a background of high volume training, high intensity training can maintain but seldom improve performance."** (Ch. 11, citing Laursen, 2009)
- **Masters athletes should cut back mileage** due to changes in recovery capacity.

## Where They Agree
1. **Volume is foundational.** All three place running volume at or near the base of training priorities. You cannot be a good ultrarunner without running a lot.
2. **Annual and multi-year thinking trumps weekly thinking.** Single weeks matter less than sustained consistency over months and years.
3. **Recovery weeks are essential.** All prescribe significant volume reductions on regular cycles.
4. **Returning to previous volume levels is safer than venturing into new territory.** Johnston and Magness both note this explicitly.
5. **Detraining is rapid with complete cessation but slow with reduced volume.** Cutting to 50% for weeks preserves most fitness.
6. **Elite ultrarunners train 700-1,300 hours per year.** The volume needed at the top is enormous.

## Where They Disagree

### The 10% Rule
- **Johnston:** Treats 10% as a firm maximum across distance, vertical, and time. "Nothing wrong with holding volume constant from week to week."
- **Koop:** Does not use the 10% rule. Focuses on phase-based load management with concentrated blocks and recovery between phases.
- **Magness:** Explicitly says to throw it out. "It assumes that we are all the same and at the same point in our training." Volume increases should be individualized based on history.

### Progressive overload within a training block
- **Johnston:** Traditional progressive model -- increase volume across 3 build weeks, then recovery week. Conservative, steady ramp.
- **Koop:** Reversed. Do the hardest/longest workouts at the BEGINNING of the phase when freshest. Volume of intensity should DECREASE as the training phase progresses. "This is a huge problem" with the traditional model.
- **Magness:** Non-linear. Emphasis shifts but training components are always present. Neither rigid ramp nor rigid front-loading.

### Concentrated vs. distributed volume
- **Koop:** Strongly favors concentration -- back-to-back long runs, clustering hard efforts. "Essentially worth more benefit in the entirety of the training picture."
- **Johnston:** Prefers steady distribution with adequate recovery. Back-to-back long runs used for specificity weeks but not as default structure.
- **Magness:** Uses long run after hard workout to create residual fatigue and force additional fiber recruitment. Split doubles for recovery enhancement.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Is the athlete returning from a break?**
   - Break <2 weeks: Start at 80% of recent volume.
   - Break 2-4 weeks: Start at 60-70% of recent volume.
   - Break >4 weeks: Start at 50% of previous cycle's average (Johnston).

2. **Is the volume level new territory for this athlete?**
   - No (previously sustained): Can increase relatively quickly (Magness -- up to 80-90% of previous peak within 3 weeks).
   - Yes (never sustained before): Use Johnston's conservative approach. Max 10% per week across each variable. Add one mile per day of running when entering new territory (Magness).

3. **What is the athlete's experience level?**
   - Beginner (<2 years, <400 hrs/year): 3:1 build:recovery. Strict 10% max on volume increases. Focus on consistency.
   - Intermediate (2-5 years, 400-600 hrs/year): Can use 3:1 or 4:1 cycles. Experiment with concentrated blocks (Koop).
   - Advanced (5+ years, >600 hrs/year): Consider front-loading phases (Koop). Can sustain higher density. Gains come from distribution and specificity rather than raw volume increases.

4. **How is the athlete responding to current load?**
   - Workout grades consistently A-B, sleep and HRV normal: Continue progression or add load.
   - Workout grades dropping to C, elevated resting HR, poor sleep: Trigger recovery week immediately (don't wait for scheduled week).
   - Performance declining 3-8% on 2 of last 3 workouts: Phase transition needed (Koop).
   - More than two C-grade workouts in a row, or a C and D in one week: Stop and assess (Johnston).

5. **CTL management guidelines:**
   - Max CTL increase: 3-5 points per week.
   - Sustained TSB below -20: reduce load immediately.
   - TSB -10 to -20: athlete is accumulating fatigue -- only acceptable during deliberate overreaching blocks with planned recovery.
   - Target race-week TSB: +5 to +15.

6. **Vertical progression (separate from distance):**
   - Start first week at 30-50% of goal event's total vertical.
   - Build to 90-100% of event vertical during at least 2 late Base Period weeks.
   - Cut vertical by 40-60% during recovery weeks.
   - TSS add-on: For each 1,000 ft of vertical gain, add 10 to hrTSS. For every 10% bodyweight carried, add another 10 TSS per 1,000 ft.

7. **Overtraining detection (volume-related red flags):**
   - Elevated resting HR on easy runs (Johnston: early warning sign)
   - Persistent leg fatigue not resolved by 1-2 rest days (needs 5-7 days -- Johnston)
   - Inability to hit interval paces previously achievable
   - Performance declining 3-8% on 2 of last 3 workouts (Koop: phase transition trigger)
   - More than two C-grade workouts in a row (Johnston)
   - HRV trending down over 5+ days
   - Sleep disruption (sympathetic nervous system overactivation)
   - Weight loss, loss of appetite, elevated cortisol (advanced OTS symptoms)
   - **Karl Meltzer:** "When I feel tired, I rest, period... 60 miles per week is plenty to win lots of 100s."
   - **Johnston:** "It is far better to be undertrained than to be even a little overtrained."

8. **Recommended minimum peak weekly distances for mountain races (Johnston):**
   - 50K: Comfortable at 25 mi/week before starting plan. Peak week = 50-55 mi.
   - 100K: Comfortable at 50K loads. Peak week = 74-80 mi.
   - 100-mile: Comfortable at 100K loads. Peak week = 90-100 mi.
