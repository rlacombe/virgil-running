# Age & Gender Adaptations

## Summary

Ultrarunning is one of the few sports where female athletes close the performance gap
with males as distance increases, and where athletes in their 40s and 50s regularly
compete at elite levels. Training must account for physiological differences in
hormonal profiles, fiber type distribution, fat oxidation, recovery capacity, and
career development stage. A one-size-fits-all approach to volume, intensity, and
periodization ignores fundamental biological variables that directly affect performance
and health. This file covers sex-specific considerations, menstrual cycle impacts,
menopause adaptations, age-related changes, youth development, and career-long
progression.

## Key Concepts

- **Fat oxidation advantage in females**: Women oxidize 24-56% more fat than males at
  the same relative intensity, providing a natural advantage in ultra-distance events
  where fat becomes the dominant fuel source.

- **Peripheral fatigue resistance**: Female athletes demonstrate lower neuromuscular
  fatigue after ultra-distance events, partly explaining their improved relative
  performance as distances increase.

- **RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)**: Inadequate caloric intake relative
  to training expenditure, affecting both males and females but with different
  presentations. Loss of menstrual function is NOT normal for female athletes and is
  a critical warning sign.

- **Cardiac maturation**: The heart adapts fastest and to the greatest extent during
  adolescence. Training in youth creates structural adaptations that persist even
  through periods of inactivity.

- **Fiber type conversion**: A very long-term process requiring years of training and
  accumulated muscle damage. May partly explain why distance runners peak later in
  their careers than sprint/power athletes.

- **VO2max decline**: Approximately 5-10% per decade after age 30, directly related
  to muscle mass loss. Can be attenuated but not eliminated through continued training.

## What the Experts Say

### Jason Koop (CTS / Training Essentials for Ultrarunning)

#### Female Athletes — Advantages

- **Higher fat oxidation**: Women have 24-56% greater fat oxidation rates than males,
  even at rest. This is a significant advantage in ultra-distance events where fat
  is the primary fuel source (Ch. 2).

- **Greater fatigue resistance**: Women have a higher percentage of Type I
  (fatigue-resistant) muscle fibers, contributing to better endurance at ultra
  distances. Lower neuromuscular fatigue after 100K mountain running compared to
  males (citing Temesi et al. 2015, Ch. 2).

- **Better pacing**: Female ultrarunners show less pace decline over the course of a
  race. Combined with fatigue resistance, this partly explains the closing performance
  gap at distances beyond the marathon.

- **Greater peripheral fatigue resistance**: In ultra events specifically, women
  demonstrate measurably lower peripheral fatigue, allowing them to maintain force
  production deeper into races (Ch. 2).

#### Female Athletes — Challenges

- **Higher GI distress prevalence**: Women have 10% smaller stomachs and slower gastric
  emptying, contributing to higher rates of GI issues during races. Fueling strategies
  must account for this (Ch. 13).

- **Hormonal fluctuations**: Affect metabolism, connective tissue properties, and
  recovery. Greater risk of connective tissue injuries (ACL) due to estrogen's effect
  on elastic properties (Ch. 2).

- **Higher amenorrhea rates**: Up to 70% of female athletes vs. 2-5% in the general
  population. This is a critical RED-S warning sign, not a badge of hard training
  (Ch. 2).

- **LCHF diets ineffective**: Women are already efficient fat burners. LCHF diets are
  "generally ineffective" for female ultrarunners and carry additional health risks
  (Ch. 2).

#### Menstrual Cycle Tracking

- **Early follicular phase** (low estrogen + progesterone): Most likely to report
  negative symptoms. Performance may feel worst.

- **Post-follicular phase** (rising estrogen): Athletes tend to feel best. Estrogen
  benefits include:
  - Glycogen sparing
  - Muscle damage protection
  - Faster recovery
  - Neural pathway activation

- **Progesterone** dampens estrogen's effects. Mid-luteal phase may not feel as good
  despite elevated estrogen.

- **Recommendation**: "Approach training and competing independent of the different
  phases... in as personalized a way as possible" — track your cycle and adjust
  individually rather than following rigid phase-based protocols (Ch. 2).

- **Birth control considerations**:
  - Oral contraceptives: create a chronic hormonal profile similar to the early
    follicular phase (when performance is typically worst); may lower VO2max by ~4.7%
  - Low-dose hormonal IUDs: hormones localized, don't impact circulating levels
  - Both can mask amenorrhea — an important RED-S warning sign
  - Source: Ch. 2

#### Menopause Protocol (5 Recommendations)

Perimenopause typically begins in the mid-40s (range: 36-45) and lasts 4-5 years
before menopause. Estrogen drops cause muscle atrophy (especially Type II fibers),
increased subcutaneous fat, and performance decline. VO2max declines 5-10% per decade
after age 30 (Ch. 2).

1. **Focus on sleep hygiene**: Menopausal athletes have harder time falling and staying
   asleep. Apply full sleep hygiene protocol (cool room 60-67F, consistent times,
   electronics off 90 min before bed).

2. **Lift heavy things**: Counter muscle mass loss actively. Strength training shifts
   from optional to essential. Heavy weights with lower repetitions produce bigger
   improvements than high-rep circuits.

3. **Watch out for heat**: Delayed sweat onset and dulled thirst mechanism during
   menopause. Extra attention to heat acclimation and hydration.

4. **Avoid added fructose**: Declining estrogen increases carb/insulin sensitivity and
   reduces fructose absorption. May contribute to increased GI distress.

5. **Factor in additional recovery**: Move to 10-day training blocks or 2:1 work:rest
   ratio instead of 3:1 or 4:1. Eat more protein — increased muscle catabolism
   requires timing protein in the post-activity window.

#### RED-S

- Applies to both males and females, but female athletes are at higher risk.

- "Canary in the coal mine": losing your period is NOT normal for athletes and signals
  that the body is redirecting energy from metabolism/reproduction to survival.

- Can occur without disordered eating — easy to fall behind on nutrition demands during
  high-volume training.

- Long-term consequences: decreased bone density, cardiovascular issues, thyroid
  dysfunction (Ch. 2).

- Koop lists avoiding RED-S alongside sleep and rest days as primary injury prevention
  strategies (Ch. 11).

#### Aging Athletes

- Age-related VO2max decline is directly related to muscle mass loss, which accelerates
  after 40 and especially after 60.

- "For aging athletes, strength training isn't just corrective or ergogenic, it's
  essential for general health and well-being" (Ch. 11).

- "Shift some priority away from running time and toward moving heavy objects a few
  times a week" (Ch. 11).

- May need more recovery between hard sessions.

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)

#### Starting Volumes by Age

Johnston provides conservative annual starting volume targets (Ch. 4):

| Age Group | Annual Hours |
|-----------|-------------|
| Juniors (15 and under) | 300 |
| Juniors (16-19) | 450 |
| Adults 20-35 | 400 |
| Adults 35-50 | 350 |
| Over 50 | 300 |

These are *starting* targets for athletes building a base. Experienced athletes with
years of consistent training may train at higher volumes.

#### Cardiac Maturation

- The heart reaches full maturation during adolescence, adapting fastest and to the
  greatest extent during this period.

- "Young, as well as older, untrained individuals can see rapid and significant changes
  in the heart's stroke volume because the heart muscle is very trainable; but, only
  up to a genetically predetermined limit" (Ch. 2).

- Adaptations to training come much slower after the teens and twenties and require
  much more work after middle age.

- People with years of endurance training — especially in childhood through
  adolescence — retain many structural adaptations even if relatively inactive
  recently.

#### Recovery Changes with Age

- Krissy Moehl: Recovery timeline "has lengthened as I've gotten older" — 2 months to
  recuperate from 100-milers (Ch. 4).

- Adaptations slow with age. What took 4 weeks at 25 may take 6-8 weeks at 45.

- Recovery weeks may need to be more frequent (2:1 instead of 3:1) for older athletes.

#### Youth Development

- Kilian Jornet started serious training at 13 with a coach — 600 hours in his first
  year. Added ~10% more training hours per year for several years. Last 8+ years:
  1,000-1,300 hours/year (Ch. 3).

- "If you want to increase to this high number of training hours, you have to increase
  no more than 10 percent a year for several years" (Ch. 3).

- "The early years of training are best spent learning about your body and when you
  can push, and when not to push" (Ch. 4, Kilian).

#### VO2max by Gender

- Males: up to 90-95 ml/kg/min. Females: top out at ~80 ml/kg/min (due to lower
  percentage of lean muscle mass to body weight) (Ch. 2).

- This gap narrows in relative importance as race distance increases, because VO2max
  becomes less predictive of ultra performance.

#### Annual Volume Progression by Experience

- Beginners (<350-400 hours/year): can increase training load by up to 25% per year.
- Advanced (>500 hours/year): max 10% jump in yearly volume.
- Elite: often make no change in overall annual volume, seeking gains through
  type/amount of higher-intensity and specific training (Ch. 3).

#### Fasted Training Warning for Women

- Johnston explicitly warns against fasted training for female athletes.
- Health risks include menstrual dysfunction, compromised bone health, and impaired
  immunity
  (https://uphillathlete.com/nutrition/high-fat-low-carb-diet-ultra-endurance-performance/).

#### Long Career Perspective

- "Endurance athletes often have very long careers, continuing to improve even into
  their forties" (Ch. 2) — because structural adaptations (capillary beds,
  mitochondria) continue to accrue for years.

- "Lifetime training history" is one of the most important factors in individuality
  of training response (Ch. 2).

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)

#### Career Development Phases

- **Beginning runners**: Focus on extremes — general aerobic running + general speed
  (strides, sprints). "The first couple of years should be spent on laying a
  foundation" (Ch. 19).

- **Mid-career development**: Progressively increase total volume until maximizing
  potential for the event. Once a large volume base exists, mileage can eventually
  decrease while specific intensity increases (Ch. 19).

- **African vs. American development**: African runners accumulate large volumes of
  "unofficial" training (running to school, active lifestyle) that creates a
  foundation tens of thousands of miles ahead of Western counterparts. Joe Vigil
  cited this as a critical factor (Ch. 19).

- **Key principle**: "Training cannot be seen through a segmented season to season
  viewpoint but should instead be seen through a lifetime and long-term view" (Ch. 8).

#### Masters Adjustments

- Masters athletes should cut back mileage levels due to changes in recovery (Ch. 19).

- As athletes age, more emphasis needed on pure speed training to combat speed losses.
  "Speed and strength training designed to maintain strength levels, Fast Twitch fiber
  numbers, and neural recruitment is a necessity" for older athletes (Ch. 19).

- Younger athletes have superior recoverability and can handle higher density (more
  hard workouts per week). Density should decrease as athletes age and lose the ability
  to recover quickly (Ch. 19).

#### Fiber Type Conversion

- **Rusko (1992)**: After 8 years of training and doubling of training volume,
  percentage of slow-twitch fibers in cross-country skiers increased by 11%.

- Fiber type conversion is a very long-term process requiring large amounts of
  training and accumulated muscle damage (Ch. 8).

- Animal studies show that "a large amount of damage is required for a complete
  transformation" of fiber types. "In particularly resistant fibers, it sometimes
  takes muscle damage combined with chronic stimulation of the fiber to get a full
  fiber type conversion" (Ch. 8, citing Pette 1999).

- This may explain:
  1. Why distance runners peak later than sprint/power athletes — the slow-twitch
     conversion takes years
  2. Part of the African runner advantage — early high-volume lifestyle allows
     conversion to begin at a younger age
  - Source: Ch. 19

#### Individuality by Fiber Type

- **Slow-twitch dominant athletes**:
  - Respond better to more volume
  - Can hold peaks longer
  - Sharpening phase preferred over dramatic taper
  - Higher optimal muscle tension for longer events
  - Source: Ch. 19

- **Fast-twitch dominant athletes**:
  - Respond better to higher intensity
  - Need longer peaking period
  - Respond better to classic taper
  - May react poorly to very slow recovery runs (biomechanics change, muscle tension
    drops, feel flat next day)
  - Source: Ch. 19

- "Even the greatest training system will not work for every runner" (Ch. 17).

- Peter Snell (high mileage Lydiard system) vs. Sebastian Coe (lower mileage, high
  intensity) — both world record holders at 800m/1500m with completely opposite
  training systems (Ch. 17).

#### Gender Notes

- Female Olympic runner case study: 3,000m time improved by 46 seconds while VO2max
  *decreased* from 72 to 66 ml/kg/min (Jones 1998). Performance is not synonymous
  with VO2max (Ch. 5).

- Women showed 0.69% performance improvement over 3 years (vs. 1.77% for men) with
  VO2max stable in both groups. Smaller margins of improvement may reflect the relative
  maturity of training approaches for women in the study period (Ch. 5, citing Legaz
  Arrese 2005).

## Where They Agree

1. **Female athletes have specific ultra-distance advantages.** Koop quantifies them
   (24-56% higher fat oxidation, greater fatigue resistance, better pacing). Johnston
   acknowledges the VO2max gap narrows with distance. Magness's fiber type framework
   supports the slow-twitch advantage for longer events.

2. **RED-S is a serious concern for all athletes, especially women.** Both Koop and
   Johnston explicitly warn about the health consequences of under-fueling. Loss of
   menstrual function is a critical warning sign, not a training badge.

3. **Aging requires more recovery and more strength work.** All three agree that
   masters athletes need longer recovery periods and that strength training becomes
   essential rather than optional after 40.

4. **Youth development should prioritize base-building.** Johnston's starting volumes
   by age, Magness's career development phases, and the universal emphasis on aerobic
   base before intensity all point toward patience in young athletes.

5. **Lifetime training volume matters enormously.** Johnston tracks annual hours across
   years. Magness cites the African development model. Both emphasize that adaptations
   accrue over years and decades, not weeks.

6. **Individuality trumps universal protocols.** All three reject the idea that one
   training system works for all athletes regardless of age, gender, fiber type, or
   training history.

## Where They Disagree

- **Menstrual cycle and training modifications**: Koop provides the most detailed
  treatment — tracking cycle phases, understanding estrogen/progesterone effects,
  birth control considerations, and specific menopause protocols. Johnston touches on
  it primarily through fasted training warnings for women. Magness does not address
  menstrual cycle considerations in the source material.

- **Strength training urgency for aging athletes**: Koop frames strength training as
  essential for aging athletes' general health ("shift some priority away from
  running"). Magness emphasizes speed and power training for neural recruitment and
  fiber preservation. Johnston's ME training naturally addresses this but is framed
  as performance-driven rather than health-driven.

- **The 10% rule applied to youth/beginners**: Johnston allows beginners up to 25%
  annual volume increases. Magness says throw out percentage-based rules entirely.
  Koop doesn't prescribe annual volume increases but focuses on minimum-maximum
  thresholds by race distance.

- **VO2max decline significance**: Koop frames it as inevitable but manageable (5-10%
  per decade). Magness argues VO2max is overrated as a performance metric anyway —
  threshold and economy improvements continue far longer. Johnston emphasizes that
  structural adaptations (capillaries, mitochondria) continue to improve even when
  VO2max has plateaued.

- **Fiber type malleability**: Magness presents the most detail on fiber type
  conversion (Rusko, Pette studies) and argues it requires years of accumulated
  training and damage. Johnston and Koop don't address this explicitly, though
  Johnston's ME training philosophy (recruiting untrained motor units through
  hypergravity) operates on similar principles.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Female athlete, new to ultrarunning**: Establish energy availability as the top
   priority — eating enough to support both training and hormonal health. Track
   menstrual cycle from day one. Avoid fasted training initially (Johnston's warning).
   Apply Koop's fueling targets (200-300 cal/hr) during long runs, accounting for
   potentially higher GI sensitivity (smaller stomach, slower gastric emptying).
   Leverage natural fat oxidation advantage — don't push LCHF diets.

2. **Female athlete, amenorrhea or irregular cycles**: Strongly recommend medical
   consultation immediately. This is a RED-S warning sign, not normal training
   adaptation. Review caloric intake — are they meeting training demands? Check for
   bone density concerns. Reduce training volume if energy balance cannot be restored
   through nutrition alone. "Losing your period is NOT normal for athletes" (Koop).

3. **Female athlete, perimenopause/menopause**: Apply Koop's 5-point protocol:
   (1) Prioritize sleep hygiene, (2) Add heavy strength training 2-3x/week,
   (3) Extra heat awareness and hydration, (4) Reduce added fructose, (5) Lengthen
   recovery cycles to 2:1 or 10-day blocks. Increase protein timing around workouts.
   Watch for declining performance and adjust expectations — not every decline is
   from under-training.

4. **Athlete under 18**: Follow Johnston's starting volumes — 300 hours/year for 15
   and under, 450 for 16-19. Prioritize fun, variety, and skill development. Focus
   on aerobic base and general speed (strides, hill sprints per Magness). Do not
   introduce serious intensity work until aerobic base is established (Johnston's 10%
   test). Allow up to 25% annual volume increases (Johnston). The heart adapts fastest
   during this period — build the aerobic engine now.

5. **Masters athlete (40-55)**: Add strength training if not already doing it —
   "essential for general health" (Koop). Consider moving from 3:1 to 2:1
   build:recovery ratio. Increase emphasis on speed/neural recruitment work to combat
   fast-twitch fiber loss (Magness). May need more recovery days between hard sessions.
   Don't chase volume increases — quality over quantity becomes more important.

6. **Masters athlete (55+)**: Follow Johnston's starting volume guidance (300
   hours/year if new or returning). Strength training 2-3x/week is non-negotiable for
   health. Accept longer adaptation timelines. Monitor recovery closely using HRV and
   subjective markers. Magness's emphasis on strides and hill sprints for neural
   maintenance applies particularly here.

7. **Experienced athlete with plateaued performance**: Assess fiber type tendencies
   (Magness: ST vs. FT dominant). ST-dominant athletes may benefit from more volume
   and less taper. FT-dominant athletes may benefit from more intensity variety and
   classic taper approach. Look at lifetime training volume — is the aerobic base truly
   maximized? (Johnston's category system: <400 hr/year = still building base).
   Consider Magness's fiber type conversion timeline — some athletes simply need more
   years of accumulated training.

8. **Athlete returning after years away**: Structural adaptations from prior training
   are partially retained (Johnston: "people with years of endurance training retain
   many structural adaptations"). Recovery to previous fitness may be faster than a
   true beginner. Start at 50% of previous training load (Johnston) but may progress
   faster than someone with no history. Get blood work done (ferritin, hormones).
   Apply Magness's principle: safe to increase rapidly to previously maintained levels.
