# Taper & Peak

## Summary
The taper is the final phase of training before a goal race, designed to shed accumulated fatigue while preserving hard-earned fitness. For ultrarunners, the taper matters less than for shorter-event athletes -- the performance gap between a perfect taper and reasonable rest is only ~1% -- but getting it wrong can sabotage months of preparation. All three experts emphasize volume reduction over intensity reduction, but they diverge on mechanics: Johnston distinguishes tapering from sharpening, Koop advocates a "fast decay" model with maintained intensity type, and Magness introduces muscle tension management as the secret variable of peaking. The psychological challenge -- taper tantrums -- is universal and must be anticipated.

## Key Concepts

### Taper vs. Sharpening
- **Tapering:** Reducing volume only. Appropriate for longer events where aerobic qualities must be preserved.
- **Sharpening:** Reducing volume AND increasing intensity (or maintaining intensity while decreasing density). Better for shorter events or athletes needing neuromuscular activation before race day.

### A/B/C Race Classification
- **A race:** Full taper. Significant volume reduction. 1-3 per year maximum. Requires significant recovery afterward.
- **B race:** Slight training load reduction. Used as tune-ups to test fitness, strategy, equipment, nutrition. Minimal disruption to training plan.
- **C race:** Train right through. Just another workout. Can be inserted without disrupting the plan.

### Taper Tantrums
- Normal psychological and physical reactions to reduced training: anxiety, irritability, heaviness, sluggishness, doubt, restlessness, disrupted sleep.
- Caused by sudden reduction in exercise-induced endorphins and routine disruption.
- Not a sign that fitness is declining -- trust the training.

### Performance Gain from Taper
- Average improvement: ~3% (Mujika and Padilla 2003).
- For ultrarunning specifically, the difference between no taper and perfect taper is only ~1%.
- Reasonable rest dominates the benefits vs. a perfectly designed taper.

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Timeline:** 1-3 weeks before major races. Ultrarunners with 60+ mile weeks need 10-14 days. Some sports scientists recommend up to a month for an A endurance race. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/tapering-for-race-event-what-to-do/)
- **Volume reduction:** Reduce weekly volume by ~25% per week. Can drop to 50-60% of normal volume. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/tapering-for-race-event-what-to-do/)
- **Taper vs. sharpening distinction:**
  - Tapering = reducing volume only. Appropriate for ultra-distance events.
  - Sharpening = reducing volume + increasing intensity. For shorter events like VK or skimo under 2 hours.
  - Even ultramarathon runners benefit from brief high-intensity sessions during taper: "five minutes of 30 seconds on, 30 seconds off" the day before racing. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/tapering-for-race-event-what-to-do/)
- **Taper week structure from the book:**
  - Volume reduction: ~30% per week
  - Strength: cut hill sprint/bounding by 30% week to week, one core strength session per week
  - Aerobic: long run/ski at ~50% of weekly volume, one other Z2 workout at <10% weekly volume
  - High intensity: keep short with long rests, overall intensity volume drops ~30%/week, just for sharpening
  - Include a few short fast downhill runs or quick-tempo pick-ups. (Ch. 11, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **Goal (race) week:** No strength work. Recovery zone only. Spice 2 workouts with 3-4 x 30-sec accelerations at fun fast pace, 3-min easy jog between. "Nothing you do this week will make you faster, but many things you could do will make you slower." (Ch. 11)
- **Only fully taper for A-level events.** Use B and C races as training opportunities. (https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/tapering-for-race-event-what-to-do/)
- **Common mistakes:**
  - Attempting hard workouts during taper: "don't leave your best race in training"
  - Adding non-training stress (home projects, extra work)
  - Over-traveling before events
  - Ignoring taper tantrums -- anxiety, irritability, heaviness are normal
- **Some athletes perform better with moderate training up to race day** rather than a full taper. Kilian often prefers NOT to taper, especially when racing often -- too much tapering = fitness drop over a season. (Ch. 11)
- **Rico Elmer's pre-race protocol:** Race on Saturday: Wednesday one interval workout (hard but short), Thursday 20 easy minutes on bike then travel, Friday course check with 2-3 quick sprints (last one to max HR for <1 min). (Ch. 11)

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Fast decay model (preferred):** Drop volume abruptly and early, then reduce more gradually.
  - Week 1: 40-50% of normal volume
  - Week 2: 20-30% of normal volume
  - Week 3: <20% of normal volume (max 2 hours)
  - (Ch. 12, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **Duration:** 2-3 weeks before event. Ultramarathon tapers shouldn't look dramatically different from a normal de-loading week -- "maybe a little bit longer or a little bit deeper, but not overly elaborate." (https://trainright.com/tapering-ultrarunning-prevent-taper-tantrum/)
- **Intensity during taper -- the critical rule:** "Reduce the volume, and the volume of intensity, but maintain the type of intensity." Run fewer miles and shorter workouts but at the same pace. (Ch. 12)
  - Fast-decay intensity example:
    - Normal: 4 x 10 min TempoRun
    - Week 1: 3 x 5 min TR (37.5%)
    - Week 2: 2 x 5 min TR (25%)
    - Week 3: 1 x 5 min TR (12.5%)
  - "Maintaining training intensity is a critical part of the tapering process... it prevents a decline in the hard-earned benefits." (Ch. 12)
- **Frequency:** Maintain or reduce by max 1 day/week. (Ch. 12)
- **Terrain specificity during taper:** MAINTAIN terrain and surface specificity. "I see over and over again athletes who train on specific trails for those events but then change that specificity for their taper. They run around the track in Chamonix." (Ch. 12)
- **Performance impact:** A properly constructed taper can improve performance by ~3%, but "performance in ultrarunning is [not] so precise that a taper is going to matter all that much" -- reasonable rest dominates. The difference between no taper and perfect taper is only ~1%. (Ch. 12)
- **Taper tantrum management:** Psychological and physical challenges are normal. Trust the training -- review the quantity of work done over months. Use excess energy for purposeful rest, not obsessive race prep. (https://trainright.com/tapering-ultrarunning-prevent-taper-tantrum/)
- **Koop consulted with Inigo Mujika PhD**, the world's leading taper researcher, on ultrarunning-specific taper protocols. (KoopCast Episode 86)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Two approaches to peaking:**
  1. **Tapering (classic/research-based):** Significant volume drop + significant intensity increase. Better for fast-twitch athletes, shorter events, or overtrained athletes.
  2. **Sharpening (Magness's preference for most distance runners):** Maintain or very slightly drop mileage; slightly decrease volume of intense workouts; decrease density (more recovery between hard sessions). "Less risk, and if done correctly just as high of a reward." (Ch. 19, *The Science of Running*)
- **Taper risks for distance events:** Dramatic taper causes muscle fiber type shift toward fast-twitch end -- beneficial for short events but can erode aerobic abilities. "A dramatic taper for ST/longer distance events can cause a small erosion of the aerobic abilities." (Ch. 19)
- **Muscle tension management -- the secret of peaking:**
  - Within muscle fibers, there is an optimal length for force production (length-tension relationship). Training at specific paces adjusts this resting tension.
  - **Too much slow running:** Body optimizes for moderate contractions, feels "flat" when racing faster.
  - **Too much rest:** Tension drops, runner feels "horribly flat."
  - "The last week is about altering tension, not gaining fitness." (Ch. 19)
- **Tension manipulation guidelines:**
  - Shorter events = higher optimal tension; longer events = lower optimal tension
  - FT athletes generally need higher tension than ST athletes for the same race
  - Make big tension changes 3-7 days out; small tweaks closer to race
- **Tension adjustment tools (most to least effect):**
  1. Increase dramatically: sprints or hill sprints
  2. Increase slightly: 8x200 at 2mi-mile pace
  3. Maintain: race pace work or blend work (fartlek with moderate + fast segments)
  4. Decrease slightly: short threshold run
  5. Decrease dramatically: long run or long threshold run
- **Workout timing:** Workouts generally take 7-10 days minimum before seeing benefits. Last hard workout: ~10 days before peak race. (Ch. 19)
- **Don't suddenly cut all doubles** if athlete is used to doubling. (Ch. 19)
- **Practical examples from the book:**
  - Monday athlete is too bouncy: short threshold run to lower tension; Wednesday/Thursday do 200s at 3200-mile pace to prevent continued lowering
  - Tuesday/Wednesday athlete looks good: combo workout (5min medium, 5 easy, 5 medium, 5 easy, 5min of 10sec sprint/50sec easy) + strides day before race
  - Early week athlete looks flat: faster work or sprints (but watch for soreness if not recently done)

## Where They Agree
1. **Reduce volume, preserve intensity.** All three maintain some form of intensity during the taper. None advocate a complete shutdown. The body needs neuromuscular activation to avoid going stale.
2. **Only fully taper for A races.** B and C races should not disrupt the training flow. Train through C races; moderate load for B races.
3. **Taper tantrums are normal.** Anxiety, irritability, and heaviness during reduced training are universal. Athletes must be warned in advance and trust the process.
4. **Nothing new during taper.** No new workouts, new shoes, new nutrition, new routes. The training is done -- the taper is about removing fatigue, not adding fitness.
5. **Race week should be very light.** Short, easy efforts with a few brief accelerations to maintain neuromuscular readiness. No strength work.
6. **Don't overthink it.** The difference between an imperfect and perfect taper is marginal. Reasonable rest is most of the benefit.

## Where They Disagree

### Volume Reduction Rate
- **Koop:** Fast decay -- drop volume abruptly early (40-50% in week 1), continue dropping. Front-loads the rest.
- **Johnston:** Gradual 25% reduction per week. Can drop to 50-60% of normal. More conservative initial cut.
- **Magness (sharpening):** Maintain or very slightly drop mileage. Decrease workout density rather than total volume. Most conservative approach.

### Taper Duration
- **Koop:** 2-3 weeks. Ultramarathon tapers should not be elaborate -- just a slightly longer/deeper de-load week.
- **Johnston:** 1-3 weeks, with 10-14 days for high-volume ultrarunners. Some sports scientists suggest up to a month for A endurance events.
- **Magness:** Sharpening period mirrors normal training schedule with slight adjustments. Last hard workout ~10 days before race.

### Role of Intensity
- **Koop:** Maintain the TYPE of intensity but fast-decay the volume of intensity. If you were doing TempoRuns, keep doing shorter TempoRuns.
- **Johnston:** Distinguishes tapering (reduce volume only) from sharpening (reduce volume + increase intensity). Even ultrarunners benefit from brief high-intensity work the day before racing.
- **Magness:** Focuses on muscle tension management rather than arbitrary intensity rules. Adjusts tension based on how the athlete feels day to day. May prescribe sprints or threshold work depending on whether the athlete feels flat or bouncy.

### The "Flat" Problem
- **Magness:** Has a specific framework for this -- muscle tension is the underlying variable. Provides concrete tools (sprints to raise tension, threshold runs to lower it). Most sophisticated approach to the stale/flat feeling.
- **Johnston:** Acknowledges some athletes perform better with moderate training up to race day. Kilian prefers not to taper when racing often.
- **Koop:** Addresses it mainly through maintaining intensity type. Terrain specificity during taper also helps maintain neuromuscular readiness.

## Coaching Decision Tree

### Step 1: Classify the Race
- **A race:** Full taper protocol (2-3 weeks). Apply the guidelines below.
- **B race:** Reduce load by ~20-30% for the week. One fewer intensity session. No elaborate protocol needed.
- **C race:** Train through it. Normal training week. Use the race as a training effort.

### Step 2: Determine Taper Duration
- **50K:** 7-10 days is sufficient
- **50 miles:** 10-14 days
- **100K:** 14-18 days
- **100 miles:** 14-21 days
- **Athletes training > 60 miles/week:** minimum 10-14 days regardless of race distance

### Step 3: Select Taper Model Based on Athlete Profile

**Newer athletes or those showing signs of accumulated fatigue (TSB < -15):**
- Use Koop's fast decay model. They likely need more rest, sooner.
- Week 1: 40-50% of normal volume
- Week 2: 20-30% of normal volume
- Week 3 (if applicable): <20% of normal volume
- Maintain 1-2 sessions with race-specific intensity (shortened)

**Experienced athletes in good form (TSB -5 to -10):**
- Use Johnston's gradual approach or Magness's sharpening
- Reduce volume ~25% per week
- Maintain workout frequency; reduce workout duration
- Include brief high-intensity activations (30s on/30s off, strides) in the final days

**Athletes who feel flat or stale (TSB > 0 for extended period):**
- Apply Magness's tension management framework
- Add short, sharp neuromuscular activations: hill sprints, strides at 5K effort
- Do NOT reduce volume further if already very low
- Consider a combo workout 5-7 days out: fartlek with moderate + fast segments

### Step 4: Monitor During Taper
- **Athlete feels anxious/irritable:** Normal taper tantrum. Reassure. Review training log to build confidence.
- **Athlete feels flat/heavy:** Add 2-3 short sprints or strides. Do NOT add a long run.
- **Athlete feels bouncy/restless:** This is ideal. Short threshold effort to slightly reduce tension if needed.
- **Athlete requests a "big workout":** Decline. "Don't leave your best race in training."

### Step 5: Race Week Protocol
- **6-7 days out:** Last meaningful intensity session. Short, not hard. 3-4 x short intervals at race-specific effort.
- **4-5 days out:** Easy runs only, 30-40 min. Maintain terrain specificity.
- **2-3 days out:** Very short easy run (20-30 min) with 3-4 x 30-sec accelerations at comfortable fast pace, 3-min easy jog between.
- **Day before:** Rest or very easy 15-20 min shake-out. Some athletes benefit from a single near-max acceleration for <1 min to set tension.
- **Race morning:** Standard warm-up routine practiced in training.

### Special Considerations
- **Eccentric loading:** Maintain some downhill running during taper. The repeated bout effect decays after ~2 weeks without exposure. Final 2 weeks should still include some climbing and descending. (Koop)
- **Nutrition rehearsal:** Last long run (typically 2-3 weeks out) is the final opportunity to rehearse race nutrition. Do not change nutrition plan after this point.
- **Travel:** Arrive at race location as early as practical. Do not add travel stress in the final 48 hours if avoidable.
- **Frequent racers:** Athletes racing every 3-6 weeks cannot fully taper each time. Classify races honestly -- only 1-3 A races per year deserve a full taper. (Johnston, Magness)
