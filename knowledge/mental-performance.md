# Mental Performance & Fatigue

## Summary

Mental performance is arguably the single greatest differentiator in ultrarunning.
While physiological capacity sets the ceiling, psychological skills determine how much
of that capacity an athlete actually uses on race day. All three major coaching voices
agree that fatigue is not purely a physical phenomenon — the brain regulates exercise
output through anticipatory mechanisms, perceived exertion, and emotional processing.
Training the mind is as important as training the legs, and it can be practiced
systematically.

## Key Concepts

- **Central Governor Model**: The brain acts as a protective regulator, slowing output
  before true physiological failure occurs. Fatigue is partly an emotion, not just a
  metabolic endpoint.

- **Psychobiological Model (PBM)**: Performance is governed by motivation and perception
  of effort. The athlete — not the brain alone — decides how much capacity to access.

- **Perceived Exertion Endpoint Interaction**: Athletes mentally project a line from
  current effort to predicted maximum tolerable exertion at the finish. This works for
  short events but breaks down in ultras because predictive capabilities cannot extend
  far enough.

- **Associative vs. Dissociative Focus**: Two distinct attentional strategies that serve
  different purposes during effort. The key skill is not mastering one or the other, but
  learning to **deliberately switch** between them based on the demands of the moment.

  **Associative focus** (internal, body-aware):
  - **Body scan**: Head-to-toe systems check — jaw tension, shoulder position, arm swing,
    hip alignment, foot strike, breathing pattern. Systematically audit each system.
  - **Breath following**: Count breaths, match breathing to stride cadence (e.g., 3:3 or
    3:2 inhale:exhale pattern), notice depth and rhythm without forcing change.
  - **Effort calibration**: Continuously rate perceived exertion, monitor pace feel,
    assess hydration/nutrition status ("Am I drinking enough? When did I last eat?").
  - **Form cues**: Focus on a single technique element — forward lean, quick turnover,
    light feet, driving arms uphill.
  - **Best for**: Race starts (setting correct effort), technical terrain (foot placement,
    route-finding), eating/drinking windows, climbs, any moment where pacing accuracy
    or safety matters.

  **Dissociative focus** (external, distraction-based):
  - **Counting games**: Count steps to 100 and restart, count trail markers, count
    switchbacks remaining, count other runners.
  - **Mental tasks**: Plan a meal, design a room, compose an email, solve a problem,
    review a memory in detail — tasks that occupy working memory.
  - **Environmental scanning**: Name plants, study geology, watch cloud patterns, notice
    wildlife — outward-facing attention that decouples from effort.
  - **Music/podcasts**: Passive dissociation that lowers perceived exertion, though it
    prevents deliberate attentional practice.
  - **Visualization**: Replay a successful past race, visualize the finish, imagine
    being pulled toward the next aid station.
  - **Best for**: Middle stages of a race (miles 30-70 in a 100), flat/non-technical
    sections, managing pain/discomfort, grinding out monotonous terrain.

  **The switching skill**: The real training goal is the ability to move between these
  modes at will. An athlete who can only associate risks overthinking and anxiety. An
  athlete who can only dissociate risks poor pacing, missed nutrition, and falls on
  technical terrain. Practice switching during training runs — alternate 10 minutes
  associative (body scan) with 10 minutes dissociative (counting, planning) until the
  transition feels natural.

- **Willpower Depletion**: Mental fatigue from cognitively demanding tasks directly
  impairs subsequent physical performance. Pre-race mental energy must be conserved.

- **Threat vs. Challenge Framing**: How the brain interprets a stressor — as a threat
  or a challenge — fundamentally changes the physiological response and performance
  outcome.

## What the Experts Say

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)

- **Central Governor Model**: Magness devotes extensive treatment to Tim Noakes's
  central governor theory, arguing that "exercise is regulated, not catastrophic"
  (Ch. 3). The brain acts as a safety mechanism preventing the body from venturing
  too far from homeostasis. Fatigue products like lactate, hydrogen ions, temperature,
  and glycogen serve as *feedback*, not direct causes of fatigue.

- **Key quote — Noakes (2012), cited by Magness**: "Fatigue is a brain-derived human
  emotion." (Ch. 3)

- **Neurotransmitter evidence**: SSRI studies showed that increased serotonergic
  activity eliminated the "kick" at end of a time trial — "may block access to the
  reserve capacity." Dopamine reuptake inhibitors improved performance in heat by
  allowing better power maintenance. Brain stimulation (tDCS) over temporal and
  insular cortex improved peak power output by 4% in national-class cyclists
  (Ch. 12, citing Roelands 2013, Okano 2013).

- **Four pillars of real toughness**: Pain tolerance in athletes is *higher* than
  non-athletes, but pain *threshold* is similar (Tesarz metaanalysis, 2012). The
  difference is coping strategies — pain tolerance correlates with the number and
  quality of coping strategies developed (Ord, 2003, rowers study). Training duration
  negatively correlates with fear of pain (Ch. 8).

- **Willpower depletion**: Magness cites Marcora (2009) — 90 minutes of cognitively
  demanding tasks before exercise significantly worsened cycle-to-exhaustion
  performance vs. a control group watching documentaries. Mental fatigue directly
  impairs physical output.

- **Carb mouth rinse studies**: Referenced in the context of the brain's role in
  fatigue regulation. Rinsing carbohydrate solution in the mouth (without swallowing)
  improves performance by activating reward centers in the brain — evidence that the
  brain monitors fuel availability and adjusts output accordingly (Ch. 9).

- **Catastrophizing and fear of pain**: Fear of pain creates an anti-analgesic effect,
  counteracting the body's pain inhibition. Catastrophizing — exaggerated negative
  appraisal of pain — functions almost like a minor panic attack. Coping skills
  development is trainable and should be deliberately practiced (Ch. 8).

- **Deception studies**: Cyclists racing an avatar at 102% of their best performance
  produced faster times than ever before, tapping into "anaerobic reserve" more than
  usual. "It was almost as if their brains loosened the reins just a little"
  (Stone, 2012). Cyclists told it was cool when it was actually 32C performed
  identically to those in actually cool conditions (Castle et al., 2012).

### Jason Koop (CTS / Training Essentials for Ultrarunning)

- **PBM model**: Koop adopts the Psychobiological Model of fatigue over Noakes's
  Central Governor Theory: "Your physiological capacity determines the upper limit
  of performance capacity, but your psychological skills are the deciding factor in
  how much of that capacity we ultimately utilize" (Ch. 15). The critical distinction
  is that PBM puts the athlete in the driver's seat, not the brain as a final arbiter.

- **Perceived exertion endpoint interaction**: Athletes constantly draw a mental line
  from current effort to predicted maximum tolerable exertion at the end of the task.
  In ultras, this backfires because the brain cannot accurately predict suffering 20+
  hours ahead. The solution is "staying in the moment" — bypassing inaccurate
  forecasting. This explains many DNFs: "I don't think I can finish" in the moment
  leads to regret at the hotel room (Ch. 15).

- **Associative/dissociative focus**: Koop provides the most practical training framework
  for attention management:
  - Associative focus is the athlete's primary tool for *control* — body scan (head-to-toe
    systems check), breath following, effort calibration. Use it at race starts, on
    technical terrain, during eating/drinking windows, and anytime you need accurate
    pacing.
  - Dissociative focus is the tool for *endurance* — counting colors on other runners'
    gear, planning what you'll eat at the next aid station, solving mental puzzles. Use
    it in the middle stages when pain/monotony management matters more than precision.
  - The critical insight: dissociation lowers perceived exertion but also reduces pacing
    accuracy. Association maintains pacing accuracy but forces you to feel everything.
    The race-day skill is knowing which cost is acceptable at any given moment.
  - "Anybody can zone out... what you're after is the control to move to dissociation
    when you want to" (Ch. 15).
  - **Training protocol**: During training runs, practice alternating blocks — 10-15
    minutes of deliberate body scan followed by 10-15 minutes of deliberate dissociation
    (counting, planning, environmental scanning). Start in easy runs, then apply during
    tempo and long run efforts where the stakes of attention management are higher.
  - **Race-day model**: Associate through aid stations (intake assessment, gear check,
    body audit). Dissociate on non-technical stretches between aid stations when effort
    is steady. Re-associate on climbs, descents, and any terrain change.

- **MAC (Mindfulness-Acceptance-Commitment) framework**: A 4-step process:
  1. Identify what matters in competition vs. what distracts
  2. Practice mindfulness in benign settings (folding laundry, apps like Calm/Headspace)
  3. Learn to accept thoughts as neither good nor bad — notice and redirect
  4. Apply in training using associative/dissociative strategies
  - Spend at least 2 weeks on Steps 2-3 before progressing (Ch. 15).

- **ADAPT framework for race crises**:
  - **A** = Accept the situation
  - **D** = Diagnose the problem
  - **A** = Analyze options
  - **P** = Plan a response
  - **T** = Take action
  - Source: https://trainright.com/adapt-a-5-step-plan-for-when-everything-in-your-race-goes-wrong/

- **Mental skills in the hierarchy**: "Developed mental skills" sit at level 4 in
  Koop's Hierarchy of Ultramarathon Training Needs — above volume, rest, and nutrition.
  "Human physiology can be predicted and measured using various formulas and equations,
  human performance cannot — there's no equation for mental toughness"
  (https://trainright.com/hierarchy-ultramarathon-training-needs-jason-koop/).

- **Music**: A passive dissociative/motivational tool but not effective for learning
  active switching between associative/dissociative modes. Fine to use, but athletes
  should also carve out time for deliberate mental skills practice (Ch. 15).

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)

- **Decision/gratitude/adversity reframing frameworks**: Johnston draws on athlete
  stories to illustrate mental frameworks rather than formal psychological models:

  - **Janelle Smiley's Decision Framework**: Shifted mindset to gratitude during
    extreme duress. "I decided to surrender myself to the circumstances." "Instead of
    turning my back to the wind, I faced into it." The decision to finish transformed
    curiosity into belief (Ch. 2).

  - **Rod Bien's "Nickels and Dimes"**: Separate small problems from DNF decisions.
    Focus on short-term goals and positive reinforcement. "By eliminating the negative
    voices in your head and concentrating on what you can control, you'll develop
    mental tenacity" (Ch. 2). "Don't nickel and dime yourself out of the satisfaction
    of crossing the finish line."

  - **Jared Campbell's Adversity Reframing**: Intentional "adversity training" — seek
    out nasty conditions to build mental resilience. Turn obstacles into positive
    learning opportunities. "In every challenge there is something to be learned and
    a way to be a better person for it" (Ch. 4).

- **Motivation vs. compulsion**: Lack of motivation on a given day is a clear warning
  sign — the body telling you to back off. "Don't train compulsively." Willpower "can
  also be your worst enemy when it clouds your judgment and becomes a merciless
  taskmaster" (Ch. 4).

- **Training as joy**: Kilian Jornet, via Johnston: "I believe it is very important to
  appreciate how nice it is to be out running... I think it is very important that
  training is not stressful in and of itself. It should be fun, enjoyable, with good
  people, and in nice places" (Ch. 3).

- **Dakota Jones on purpose**: "Training is important. But it is only a means of
  facilitating what you love. Don't forget what you are training for" (Ch. 3).

## Where They Agree

1. **Fatigue is not purely physical.** All three reject the idea that fatigue is simply
   muscles running out of fuel or accumulating too much waste. The brain plays a central
   regulatory role.

2. **Mental skills are trainable.** None treat mental toughness as a fixed trait. All
   advocate deliberate practice of coping strategies, whether through formal frameworks
   (MAC, ADAPT) or experiential approaches (adversity training, gratitude reframing).

3. **Staying present matters.** All three advocate some form of "staying in the moment"
   rather than projecting suffering forward. Koop formalizes this as perceived exertion
   endpoint interaction. Magness describes it through pacing psychology. Johnston frames
   it through decision-making and short-term goal-setting.

4. **Pre-race mental energy conservation.** All recognize that arriving at the start
   mentally depleted degrades performance. Koop warns about pre-race anxiety. Magness
   cites Marcora's willpower depletion research. Johnston's athletes talk about
   surrendering to circumstances rather than fighting them.

## Where They Disagree

- **Theoretical framework**: Magness anchors to Noakes's Central Governor model (brain
  as ultimate arbiter), while Koop explicitly prefers the Psychobiological Model
  (athlete in the driver's seat, not the brain). The practical difference is subtle
  but philosophically significant: CGM implies the brain will always limit you; PBM
  implies you can choose to push harder if motivation is high enough.

- **Formality of training**: Koop provides structured, step-by-step mental training
  protocols (MAC framework, ADAPT framework) with specific timelines. Johnston's
  approach is more experiential and narrative-driven — learn through adversity,
  cultivate gratitude, make conscious decisions. Magness is research-heavy, providing
  the scientific basis but fewer practical protocols.

- **Role of dissociation**: Koop sees dissociation as a valuable and trainable tool —
  lower perceived exertion, endure longer. Magness is more cautious: dissociative
  thinking may "clog the pathway" of afferent feedback (Stone, 2012), which can lower
  perceived exertion but also impairs pacing accuracy and may mask warning signs of
  genuine physiological danger (dehydration, hyperthermia). The practical tension: Koop
  says learn to dissociate on command; Magness says stay associated enough to hear what
  your body is telling you. The resolution may be Koop's own switching model — dissociate
  on safe, non-technical terrain; re-associate at decision points (aid stations, climbs,
  any moment requiring judgment). Johnston does not explicitly discuss dissociation as
  a strategy, instead emphasizing gratitude and decision-making frameworks that are
  inherently associative (present-moment, acceptance-based).

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Athlete with no mental skills training**: Start with Koop's MAC framework
   (Steps 1-2 first, 2+ weeks of mindfulness practice in daily life before applying
   to training). Layer in Johnston's adversity reframing on harder training days.

2. **Athlete who DNFs from "I can't go on" rather than injury**: Focus on Koop's
   perceived exertion endpoint interaction — train to bypass projection by breaking
   the race into micro-segments (next aid station, next 10 seconds). Use Magness's
   deception research to explain why the brain is lying.

3. **Athlete who starts too conservatively**: Use Magness's central governor research
   to build confidence that the brain has reserves. Gradually expand the "permission
   envelope" through race-pace training and B-priority races.

4. **Athlete who catastrophizes under discomfort**: Use Magness's pain tolerance
   research (coping strategies are trainable) combined with Johnston's adversity
   training (deliberately seek uncomfortable conditions). Koop's ADAPT framework
   provides an in-race protocol when catastrophizing begins.

5. **Experienced athlete seeking marginal gains**: Combine all three — use Koop's
   associative/dissociative switching as a race-day tool, Johnston's gratitude
   reframing for the dark patches, and Magness's understanding of neurotransmitter
   manipulation (caffeine timing, pre-cooling) to optimize the brain's willingness
   to release reserves.

6. **Pre-race week**: Minimize cognitive load (Marcora's research via Magness).
   Manage taper tantrums with Koop's normalizing language. Use Johnston's joy-centered
   philosophy to maintain perspective.
