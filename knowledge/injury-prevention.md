# Injury Prevention & Management

## Summary

Injury prevention in ultrarunning begins with load management, sleep, and nutrition —
not with foam rollers or corrective exercises. The most common injuries stem from
increasing running volume too fast, accumulating chronic impact stress, and
under-recovering between sessions. All three major coaching voices agree that rest is
the most powerful injury prevention tool, that NSAIDs are dangerous in endurance sport,
and that being slightly undertrained is always preferable to being even a little
overtrained. **Important: this file is a coaching reference, not medical advice. Always
recommend that athletes consult a doctor, physical therapist, or other qualified
professional for any pain, injury, or health concern.**

## Key Concepts

- **Dose-response relationship**: Training stress follows an inverted U-shape — too
  little stimulus produces no adaptation; too much causes maladaptation. Each athlete
  sits at a different point on this curve based on their physiology and training
  history.

- **Connective tissue adaptation lag**: Muscles adapt to training in days to weeks.
  Tendons, ligaments, and fascia take weeks to months of sustained loading. This
  mismatch is where most overuse injuries originate.

- **10% rule (and its limits)**: A commonly cited maximum for weekly volume increases.
  Johnston treats it as a ceiling. Magness argues it should be thrown out because it
  assumes all athletes are identical. Koop focuses on phase architecture rather than
  weekly percentage increases.

- **Overtraining continuum**: Functional Overreaching (normal, hours to days to
  recover) → Non-Functional Overreaching (performance plateaus, intervention window)
  → Overtraining Syndrome (weeks to months to reverse, serious health consequences).

- **RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)**: Inadequate caloric intake relative
  to training expenditure, affecting both males and females. Manifests as hormonal
  disruption, decreased bone density, cardiovascular issues, and impaired immunity.

## What the Experts Say

### Jason Koop (CTS / Training Essentials for Ultrarunning)

- **Rest > strength for injury prevention (5-10x advantage)**: Koop challenges the
  strength-training-for-injury-prevention paradigm directly. He argues that rest —
  either more sleep or more rest days — provides "a five or 10 fold advantage" over
  strength training for preventing injury. "There are a lot of other things that lower
  injury risk, including rest days, sleeping eight or more hours a night, and avoiding
  RED-S" (Ch. 11). He challenges people to provide "good controlled studies" showing
  a marked decrease in injury risk after strength training programs
  (https://trainright.com/strength-training-ultramarathon-runners/).

- **Sleep reduces injury by 61%**: Sleeping >8 hours per night reduces injury chance
  by approximately 61% (citing von Rosen et al. 2016, Ch. 8). Additional sleep data:
  - Sleeping <7 hours makes athletes 3x more likely to develop a cold
  - Sleep efficiency >98% leads to 5.5x lower cold risk vs. <92% efficiency

- **Sleep hygiene protocol**:
  - Room: calm, cool (60-67F), dark, quiet
  - Consistent sleep/wake times
  - Electronics off 90 minutes before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
  - Morning: seek light, move (10-20 min), nourish (hydrate + food)
  - Source: Ch. 8

- **NSAID warnings**: "The use of NSAIDs in endurance and ultra-endurance sports is
  dangerous. The research is unequivocal" (Ch. 8). Specific risks:
  - Increases risk of hyponatremia and acute kidney injury in endurance athletes
  - Can perpetuate existing injuries by masking pain
  - "NSAIDs can perpetuate existing musculoskeletal injuries, primarily through
    masking pain" (Ch. 8)

- **Blister management**:
  - No equipment/training combination eliminates all blister risk
  - Fix problems early at 100K+ distances
  - Do NOT change gait to "run around" blisters — causes chain-reaction issues up
    the kinetic chain
  - Blister kit essentials: adhesive felt/moleskin, needles/scalpel, alcohol pads,
    tape (Leukotape, KT Gold, Elastikon), tincture of benzoin, lubricant, gauze,
    scissors, gloves
  - At Western States: take advantage of aid station foot care professionals — "It's
    time well spent and can make the difference between finishing, failing to meet a
    time cut"
  - Source: https://trainright.com/western-states-strategies-from-coaches/

- **Recovery hierarchy** (in order of importance):
  1. Sleep — gold standard
  2. Energy balance — adequate total caloric intake, prevent RED-S
  3. Time between training — proper workout scheduling
  4. Active recovery — some benefit immediately after but equals passive rest by next day
  5. Compression/massage/foam roller — feel-good, big placebo effect, no proven
     performance benefit
  6. Cold therapies — can blunt training adaptation; avoid chronic use
  - Source: Ch. 8

- **Uphill intervals as injury hedge**: "Uphill running is a hedge against injury. It
  slows you down, therefore decreasing the peak GRFs on every foot strike" (Ch. 5).
  Roughly 80% of all prescribed intervals are uphill.

- **Overtraining/underperformance syndrome**: Koop prefers the terms "under-recovery"
  or "unexplained underperformance syndrome" (UUPS). Symptoms include:
  - Unrelenting fatigue and performance decline
  - Sleep disturbances and elevated resting HR
  - Low HRV and mood disturbances
  - Loss of libido and low motivation
  - Loss of appetite
  - Frequent minor injuries/illnesses
  - Functional overreaching is normal — OTS/UUPS means >2 weeks of complete rest is
    not enough to reverse symptoms
  - "Training isn't the root of the problem — recovery is" (Ch. 8)

- **Strength training candidacy flowchart**: Not all ultrarunners are good candidates
  for strength training. Uses a decision flowchart considering available time, injury
  history, and training status. When appropriate:
  - Five movement patterns: push, pull, hinge, squat, carry
  - Early season: 1-5 x 1-5 heavy
  - Mid season: 3 x 8-12 moderate
  - Late season: corrective exercises only
  - 2 days/week max
  - Always prioritize run training over strength work
  - Source: Ch. 11

- **Aging athletes and strength**: "For aging athletes, strength training isn't just
  corrective or ergogenic, it's essential for general health and well-being" (Ch. 11).
  Age-related muscle mass loss starts >40 and accelerates >60.

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)

- **100-mile leg conditioning threshold**: "You will not really begin running training
  until you have accumulated about 100 miles in your legs. Until then you are just
  conditioning your legs so they can handle daily running" (Ch. 13). Skiers
  transitioning to running are especially prone to injuries — aerobically fit but
  lack running-specific structural strength. Return to running gradually over 4-6
  weeks.

- **"Better undertrained than overtrained"**: "It is far better to be slightly
  undertrained than to be even a little overtrained" (Ch. 4). This is a central
  principle of Johnston's philosophy, repeated across multiple contexts.

- **Connective tissue lag**: "By the time you feel the injury, there is likely to have
  been a lot of damage done and you may lose weeks to recovery" (Ch. 13). Connective
  tissue (tendons, fascia) takes weeks of sustained running to increase in elasticity
  and strength. Each footstrike = at least 2x body weight, much more on downhills.

- **Return-after-illness protocols**:
  - Stop training at the first signs of illness
  - "Imposing an additional training load on a sick body will only further weaken your
    immune system and delay healing" (Ch. 4)
  - Return protocol: one day of easy training for every day sick
  - Return to Z1-2 efforts first
  - Within a week should feel like old self again

- **Return-after-fatigue protocols**:
  - Do not jump back where you left off
  - Start at 50% duration with low intensity for a couple of days
  - If those go well, increase to 75% for a day or so
  - Reassess before returning to full load
  - Source: Ch. 13

- **Volume increase rules**:
  - Keep distance, vertical, and time increases at 10% or less per week
  - Do not increase distance and vertical for more than 3 weeks in a row
  - Do not increase distance, vertical, AND intensity in a single week
  - Follow large jumps (~15%) with a recovery week
  - Recovery weeks: drop 40-60% of last big week's volume
  - 3:1 build:recovery standard; some athletes need 2:1
  - Source: Ch. 4

- **Overtraining three stages**:
  1. Functional Overreaching — normal, recovery allows supercompensation
  2. Non-Functional Overreaching — performance plateaus, critical intervention window
  3. Overtraining Syndrome — accumulated fatigue overwhelms adaptation, weeks/months
     to reverse
  - Source: https://uphillathlete.com/trail-running/overtraining/

- **Early warning signs**: Elevated resting HR on easy runs, persistent leg fatigue,
  inability to hit interval paces, lack of motivation.

- **Advanced overtraining symptoms**: Elevated cortisol, night sweats, insomnia,
  lowered testosterone and HRV, weight loss, amenorrhea, depression. Two or more
  symptoms together warrant immediate rest
  (https://uphillathlete.com/recovery/overtraining-symptoms-in-mountain-athletes/).

- **Workout grading system**:
  - A: Superman, plenty in reserve
  - B: Good, completed fine
  - C: Flat or off
  - D: Couldn't finish/had to reduce
  - F: Couldn't train
  - Rule: More than two Cs in a row, or a C and D in one week = stop and assess
  - Source: Ch. 4

- **Ferritin warning**: Standard "low" ferritin = 12 ng/ml. For endurance athletes:
  - <50 ng/ml = some reduced performance
  - <30 ng/ml = dramatically reduced performance and chronic fatigue
  - Get blood tests when symptoms appear
  - Source: Ch. 4

- **Three Signs of Insufficient Recovery (Steve House)**:
  1. Slow to warm up (takes 30+ min to feel ready)
  2. Poor sleep quality (overstimulated sympathetic nervous system)
  3. Suspected injury (new pain, swelling, even mild)
  - Action: skip planned workout, prioritize sleep, nutrition, active recovery
  - "Think in terms of months and years (and even decades) instead of days and weeks"
  - Source: https://uphillathlete.com/recovery/top-three-signs-you-did-not-recover/

- **Strength progression for injury prevention**: General strength → max strength → ME
  progression. Hip stability exercises prevent knee/lower leg overuse injuries.
  Single-leg strength emphasis (running is a single-leg propulsion sport). Strength
  training 1-2x/week during base/build, 1x/week maintenance during peak. Assessment
  tests: air squat → box step → sit-to-stand (Ch. 6).

- **Notable athlete quotes on rest and overtraining**:
  - Clare Gallagher: "My legs felt so sore after just easy running... my heart
    wouldn't stop racing, even on slow, flat runs."
  - Krissy Moehl: "Taking two weeks off completely could save a year of running in
    your future."
  - Karl Meltzer: "When I feel tired, I rest, period... 60 miles per week is plenty
    to win lots of 100s."

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)

- **Dose-response overload**: The dose-response relationship follows an inverted
  U-shape: too little stimulus = no adaptation; too much = diminishing or negative
  adaptations (Ch. 10, citing Busson 2003). Training is "a high risk, high reward
  venture" when pushed too hard — risk of failure and maladaptation due to overtraining
  (Ch. 16). "A child needs less Advil than an adult for the same effect" — each
  athlete responds to different doses (Ch. 10).

- **Throw out the 10% rule**: "A so-called 10% rule for increasing mileage has
  developed as a sort of law of training. Throw it out." (Ch. 18). Key reasoning:
  - "Just like in adapting to interval training, weights, or any other stimulus, the
    individuality of what a runner can handle varies a lot"
  - Safe to increase rapidly to any level consistently maintained in past 6-12 months
  - Caution only when venturing into new territory: "add one mile per day of running
    to the schedule"
  - Factors: injury history, biomechanics, structural build, nutrition (calcium levels)

- **Motor programming**: "Doing all out workouts where form is broken down completely
  might lead to negative motor programming, or in layman's terms, bad habits" (Ch. 1).
  Part of training is teaching the body how to accurately adjust movement patterns to
  fatigue. Running at excessive fatigue levels can ingrain poor mechanics that increase
  injury risk.

- **Stiffness > flexibility**: "Contrary to popular belief, a stiffer muscle is more
  efficient during running than a flexible one" (Ch. 7). Key details:
  - Muscle stiffness strongly correlates with Running Economy (Dalleau et al. 1998)
  - Over-flexibility may reduce elastic energy storage and return
  - Pre-activation of muscles before landing adjusts stiffness, acting as an internal
    cushioning mechanism
  - Running surface affects pre-activation: soft sand vs. hard concrete changes muscle
    stiffness before landing
  - Source: Ch. 7

- **General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)**: Three stages:
  1. Alarm — initial response, lowering of resistance ("fight or flight")
  2. Resistance — body adapts, resistance increases
  3. Exhaustion — if stress continually applied, resistance lowers considerably
  - Without adequate recovery after resistance stage → overtraining (exhaustion)
  - Source: Ch. 10

- **Pain system exhaustion**: Athletes' pain inhibitory systems may become "exhausted"
  from constant activation, potentially explaining increased sensitivity to injuries
  and overtraining symptoms (Tesarz 2013, Ch. 8). This is a mechanism by which chronic
  overload leads to injury beyond simple tissue damage.

- **Recovery runs done right**: Short split doubles (4mi + 4mi instead of 8mi) enhance
  recovery:
  - Shorter runs minimize mechanical and oxidative damage
  - Easier to restore glycogen after 4-5mi than 10mi
  - May stimulate hormonal release twice per day
  - Growth hormone: ~550% increase from 0-40 min of easy running, then only 40-50%
    more from 40-60 min
  - Source: Ch. 19

- **Overtraining via prolonged high intensity**: "The experience of coaches points to
  a long block of high intensity training leading to decreased performance, most likely
  due to overtraining" (Ch. 11, citing Lydiard 1998). Long blocks of Z3 work are
  particularly risky.

- **CNS fatigue**: "As distance coaches we are used to recognizing fatigue that results
  from running lots of mileage or hard workouts. What we don't grasp is CNS fatigue.
  The fatigue experienced after doing 50m sprints is a totally foreign concept"
  (Ch. 8). Must recognize CNS fatigue and implement recovery strategy, just like
  recovery from a long run.

## Where They Agree

1. **Rest is the most potent injury prevention tool.** Koop quantifies it (5-10x
   advantage over strength training). Johnston enshrines it ("better undertrained than
   overtrained"). Magness frames it through dose-response theory. All prioritize sleep
   and rest days above any supplemental exercise.

2. **NSAIDs are dangerous in endurance sport.** Koop provides the strongest language
   ("the research is unequivocal"). Johnston warns against training through pain with
   NSAIDs (aligned with CLAUDE.md guardrails). Magness addresses the pain masking
   concern through his pain system exhaustion framework.

3. **Volume increases must be individualized and gradual.** Johnston sets 10% as a
   maximum, Magness says throw out the 10% rule but still advocates caution in new
   territory, and Koop focuses on phase architecture rather than weekly increments.
   All agree that rushing volume is the most common cause of injury.

4. **Overtraining is a continuum, not a binary.** All three describe a progression
   from functional overreaching (normal) to overtraining syndrome (pathological), with
   an intervention window in between. All agree that early detection and rest are the
   only remedies.

5. **Sleep is foundational.** Koop cites the 61% injury reduction from >8 hours of
   sleep. Johnston emphasizes sleep quality as a recovery sign. Magness includes it
   in his recovery hierarchy.

6. **Always recommend medical consultation for injury/pain.** This is a coaching
   reference, not a diagnostic tool. When an athlete presents with pain, injury,
   illness, or health concerns, always lead with a recommendation to see a qualified
   medical professional.

## Where They Disagree

- **The 10% rule**: Johnston treats it as a firm ceiling for weekly increases. Magness
  explicitly says to throw it out — individual variation is too high for a universal
  percentage. Koop doesn't use it at all, preferring to structure phases so the hardest
  work comes when the athlete is freshest (beginning of phase, not end).

- **Strength training's role**: Johnston prescribes a systematic General → Max → ME
  strength progression as a core training pillar alongside aerobic volume. Koop is
  notably skeptical — strength training sits near the top of his hierarchy (marginal
  gains) and he argues rest provides better injury prevention. Magness values
  neuromuscular coordination (hill sprints, plyometrics, strides) more than
  traditional gym strength training.

- **Flexibility vs. stiffness**: Magness explicitly argues for stiffness over
  flexibility, citing research that stiffer muscles are more efficient and better at
  elastic energy storage. Neither Johnston nor Koop address this directly, but
  Johnston's ME training and strength work inherently build stiffness through loaded
  movements.

- **Phase architecture and fatigue**: Koop places the hardest/longest workouts at the
  BEGINNING of a training phase when the athlete is freshest, with volume of intensity
  decreasing as fatigue accumulates. Johnston follows a more traditional build (3 weeks
  up, 1 week recovery). Magness takes a non-linear approach where emphasis shifts but
  nothing is ever completely removed.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **Athlete reporting new pain or persistent soreness**: Always recommend medical
   consultation first. Reduce training volume by 50% while awaiting professional
   assessment. Review recent volume changes — was there a >10% jump? Check sleep
   quantity and quality. Check ferritin if fatigue is chronic (Johnston: <50 ng/ml is
   problematic for athletes).

2. **Athlete with TSB < -20 for multiple days**: This is a red flag for accumulated
   fatigue. Cross-reference with subjective markers (Johnston's workout grading: are
   they scoring Cs and Ds?). Consider an unplanned recovery week (40-60% volume
   reduction). Check sleep and nutrition adequacy. If TSB has been < -10 for 2+ weeks
   with declining performance, enforce rest.

3. **Athlete returning from illness**: Apply Johnston's protocol — one day of easy
   training (Z1-Z2 only) for every day sick. Do not resume intensity until the athlete
   feels fully recovered. "Imposing an additional training load on a sick body will
   only further weaken your immune system."

4. **Athlete returning from a break of >2 weeks**: Start at 50% of previous average
   volume (Johnston). If break was >4 weeks, use the Transition Period protocol. Do
   not accelerate faster than 7-10% per week. Prioritize easy aerobic work for the
   first 100 miles to recondition connective tissue.

5. **Athlete asking about strength training for injury prevention**: If they're
   sleeping <8 hours or taking <1 rest day per week, address those first (Koop: 5-10x
   more effective than strength training). If those fundamentals are solid, introduce
   Johnston's General Strength assessment (air squat test → box step test →
   sit-to-stand test) and prescribe appropriate stage. 2x/week during base, 1x/week
   during peak.

6. **Athlete asking about NSAIDs before a race**: Strongly advise against. "The use
   of NSAIDs in endurance and ultra-endurance sports is dangerous. The research is
   unequivocal" (Koop). Increased risk of hyponatremia and acute kidney injury. Masks
   pain signals that prevent further damage. Never recommend NSAIDs for training
   through pain.

7. **Athlete with blisters during a 100-miler**: Apply Koop's blister management: fix
   early, do NOT change gait to compensate. Take advantage of aid station foot care
   when available. Blister kit should be in drop bags at key aid stations.

8. **Masters athlete (40+)**: Strength training shifts from optional to essential for
   general health (Koop). May need 2:1 build:recovery instead of 3:1 (Johnston).
   Expect longer recovery between hard sessions. Magness: increase emphasis on
   speed/strength training to combat age-related fast-twitch fiber loss, but with
   appropriate recovery.
