# Workout Types & Intensity

## Summary
The three primary ultrarunning coaches use different naming conventions and intensity scales, but their workout toolkits overlap substantially. Understanding how CTS workout types map onto Johnston's zone-based sessions and Magness's pace-based classifications is essential for translating between frameworks and prescribing the right session for the athlete's current training phase. The deepest disagreement is in periodization order: Koop starts with high intensity and works toward specificity, while Johnston builds base first and layers intensity on top.

## Key Concepts

### CTS Workout Definitions (Koop)

| Workout | RPE | Duration | Work:Rest | Key Detail |
|---------|-----|----------|-----------|------------|
| RecoveryRun (RR) | 4-5 | Up to 60 min (typical 40 min) | N/A | "Story time" -- full conversation. Only slightly faster breathing than walking. 2-3 per week. |
| EnduranceRun (ER) | 5-6 | 30 min to 6+ hrs | N/A | Primary volume builder. Slow down on uphills to prevent intensity creep. Typical workout: 2 hours. |
| SteadyStateRun (SSR) | 7-8 | 20-60 min intervals | >2:1 (long work, short rest) | Below lactate threshold. Typical: 2x30 min SSR with 4 min recovery. Phase length: 3-6 weeks. Density up to 4x/week in peak blocks. |
| TempoRun (TR) | 8-9 | 8-15 min intervals | 2:1 | At or slightly below LT. Total time at intensity: 40-90 min. Typical: 4x10 min TR with 5 min recovery. Phase length: 3-4 weeks. "Ninety percent of the time this is the phase that grinds athletes down the most." |
| RunningIntervals (RI) | 9-10 | 2-4 min intervals | 1:1 | Target >90% VO2max. Total time at intensity: 12-24 min. Accumulate >10 min at intensity. Typical: 6x3 min hard, 3 min easy. ~80% of intervals prescribed uphill. Phase length: 2-4 weeks. |

**Peak and Fade RI (Advanced):** Go as hard as possible for first 60-90 sec, then fade. Produces higher mean VO2 than even-paced intervals. Requires 2-3 years of experience.

**Optimal RI design:** 4x3 min at maximum with 1:1 rest. Too short (12x1 min): can't sustain >90% VO2max. Too long (2x6 min): athletes pace too conservatively. Takes 60-90 sec just to reach >90% VO2max.

**RI progression examples:**
- Beginner: 4x3 min hard, 3 min easy (12 min at intensity)
- Advanced: 6x3 min hard, 3 min easy (18 min at intensity)
- Elite: 5x4 min hard, 4 min easy (20 min at intensity)

**TR progression examples:**
- Typical: 4x10 min TR with 5 min recovery
- SSR typical: 2x30 min SSR with 4 min recovery
- SSR density peak: up to 4 SSR sessions per week in certain weeks

### Johnston's Zone-Based Sessions

| Session Type | Zone | Duration | Work:Rest | Key Detail |
|-------------|------|----------|-----------|------------|
| Aerobic Base | Z1-Z2 | 30 min to several hours | N/A | 80-90% of total annual training volume. Pick-ups/strides (6x15s to 8x30s) can be added once per week after a few weeks. |
| Tempo | Z3 | 20-60 min continuous | N/A | Hold steady effort. Not a time trial. 10-20 min warm-up and cool-down. |
| Progressive Distance | Z1 to Z4 | 90 min to 2+ hours | N/A | Start slowly, build through zones. Example: 40 min Z1 → 30 min Z2 → 20 min Z3 → 5 min Z4. Once every 10-14 days. |
| Threshold Intervals | Z3 | 3-30 min work periods | 3:1 or 4:1 | Total work: 20-60 min. Experienced: up to 3x20 or 2x30 min. Allow 72 hrs between Z3 and Z4 sessions. |
| Aerobic Power Intervals | Z4 | 30 sec to 8 min | 1:1 | Near 95% max HR. Total work: 15-30 min. Seiler study: 4x8 min showed best endurance gains. |
| 30/30s | Z4 | 30s hard / 30s easy | 1:1 | Introduction to Z4. Build HR to Z4 by second rep. Progression: 2x6 min → 2x15 min → 1x20 min. Goal: 30 min total. |
| Hill Sprints | Z5 | 8-10 sec max | 2+ min rest | On 30%+ grade. Start with 6-8 reps. Add 2 reps/week. Never exceed 10 seconds. One per week during Base. |

### Magness's Pace-Based Classification (Canova System)

| Category | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| General | Far from race pace | Easy running, recovery runs |
| Support (Aerobic) | Moderate proximity -- LT/threshold | Tempo runs, steady-state efforts |
| Support (Anaerobic) | Faster than race pace | VO2max intervals, speed work |
| Specific | At or very near goal race pace | Race-pace long runs, specific endurance |
| Pure Speed | Neuromuscular, not metabolic | Sprints, hill sprints, strides |

### Magness's "Stuff" System (Add-ons to Easy Runs)
- **Strides:** 100-150m at 5k to mile pace, post-run. Maintain speed, reinforce biomechanics, recruit FT fibers. Can be done frequently, especially during base.
- **Surges:** 15-60 sec gradual increase to 5k pace or faster IN the middle of a run. Train pace transitions. Max 2x/week, decreasing as intensity is added.
- **Pickups:** Sustained gradual increase over final 2-10 min. Ingrain finishing strong. 1-2x/week during base.
- **Progressions:** Start easy, progress to marathon pace or faster. Pre-fatigue easy fibers, forcing harder-to-recruit fibers to engage. Weekly during base if no other hard workouts.

**Surge progression example:**
1. 8mi run with 6x30 sec surge with 2:30 easy
2. 8mi run with 6x45 sec surge with 2:15 easy
3. 8mi run with 6x60 sec surge with 2:00 easy

### Long Run Considerations (All Three)
- Johnston: one long workout per week = 30-40% of weekly aerobic volume. Done at Z1 (if AeT within 10% of LT) or Z2 (if ADS). "Should leave you tired from the duration, not the intensity." (Ch. 10)
- Koop: no magic long run distance. Should be at least 25% of anticipated race time (hours). Weekly volume for several weeks at least 50% of anticipated race time. Minimum 4 hours for practicing nutrition.
- Magness: long run benefits include structural preparation (muscles, bones, joints adapt to pounding), fiber recruitment training (as duration extends, additional FT fibers are recruited and trained aerobically), and fuel system adaptation (only workout that stresses total fuel supply enough to trigger glycogen storage increases).

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Base-first intensity progression.** Intensity is only introduced when the 10% test shows AeT within 10% of AnT. Start Z3 at 5% of weekly volume. When Z3 reaches 10% of weekly volume comfortably, begin replacing Z3 with Z4. Replace 2 min Z3 with 1 min Z4.
- **Intensity guidelines once ready:** Z3 work-to-rest 4:1 or 5:1. Z4 under 4 min work periods: 1:1 ratio. Z4 over 5 min: 2:1 ratio. Start with ~5% of weekly aerobic volume in high-intensity work. Space hard workouts 48-72 hours apart.
- **Kilian's preferred types:** "I think of my high-intensity training as falling into a few different, simple types: VO2 max / Z4, muscular endurance / Z3, and strength like hill sprints." (Ch. 5, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
- **Kilian's favorite Z4 session:** 2 min high-tempo/high HR running → 2 min active rest → 2 min long strides on uphill terrain. Repeat until pace/HR drops off.
- **"Endurance training is a high volume of low-intensity sprinkled with a small volume of quite high-intensity."** (Uphill Athlete podcast)

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Reverse periodization for ultras.** VO2max work first (early season), then tempo and steady-state (mid-season), then race-specific endurance (late season). "Starting off with low intensity and moving to higher intensities is a stereotypical -- and flawed -- way of organizing training, particularly for ultrarunners." (Ch. 2, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **"Time at intensity is the most critical aspect to achieving positive adaptations."** (Ch. 9) Too much = intensity drops below target. Too little = insufficient stimulus.
- **All intervals are prescribed within total ER duration.** Example: "2-hr ER with 3x10-min TR." Warm-up: 10-30 min ER plus 4-8x20 sec RunningStrides. At least 10 min cooldown.
- **"Do the work periods hard and the easy periods easy."** Increasing recovery intensity normally shifts entire workout to an easier overall intensity, negating the purpose.
- **Uphill intervals for most sessions.** ~80% of all intervals prescribed are uphill. Higher cardiovascular response, lower injury risk (slower speed = lower peak ground reaction forces), and more time is spent climbing than descending in ultras.
- **12 key interval workouts** are variations of just 3 simple workout types. "Ultrarunning workouts don't need to be complicated."
- **RI should NEVER be done downhill** -- nearly impossible to achieve >90% VO2max descending.

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Easy runs must be truly easy.** "A pace that could be run at without even concentrating. Breathing should be very easy, and a conversation should be possible." (Ch. 18)
- **"Many runners get caught in a no man's land of training, where there is no difference between their recovery pace and their normal distance pace. This is a bad practice."** (Ch. 18)
- **Recovery run guidelines:** Pace should be slow enough that minimal muscle damage occurs and primary fuel source is fat. Some Kenyan runners do recovery at 8-9+ min/mile. Range: a couple miles to ~10 miles. Over 10 miles, recovery benefit diminishes.
- **Hard/easy distribution:** "Most programs combine two to three hard workouts per week, 1-2 medium workouts, and several easy runs." Workouts should be placed based on when needed and recovery requirements, not fixed days.
- **"Don't get caught up in a polarized system of very hard workouts with very slow running."** (Ch. 19) Magness values medium-effort "support" days that aren't in the typical polarized model.
- **Base training is NOT just easy running.** Even during base, include strides, surges, hill sprints, and progressions. These maintain neuromuscular coordination and recruit fast-twitch fibers without the high metabolic cost of formal interval sessions.
- **Circuits as strength endurance.** General exercises separated by runs at ~70% effort. Start at aerobic interval duration (e.g., 10k runner doing 5x2000m would do circuits ~5-6 min each x5). Non-specific lactate accumulation avoids negative effects on aerobic abilities while training lactate clearance.

## Where They Agree
1. **Recovery runs should be genuinely easy.** All three flag "no man's land" training as destructive.
2. **Uphill is the preferred terrain for intervals.** Higher cardiovascular demand, lower injury risk, more sport-specific for mountain ultras.
3. **Hard days should be hard, easy days easy.** The polarized distribution principle is endorsed by all, even if Magness adds nuance about "medium" days.
4. **Total time at intensity matters more than individual interval length.** All prescribe accumulated time at target intensity as the key variable.
5. **Strides and short accelerations are valuable year-round.** Even Johnston includes pick-ups during base period.

## Where They Disagree

### Periodization order of intensity types
- **Johnston:** Base first. No intensity until 10% test passes. Z3 before Z4. Intensity is layered onto an aerobic foundation.
- **Koop:** Reverse periodization. VO2max (RI) first → Tempo (TR) second → Steady State (SSR) last. "Training should move from least specific to most specific, not from low to high intensity."
- **Magness:** Non-linear. All components are present in every phase; only emphasis changes. Supports aerobic base preceding intensity (citing Quinn 2002: base-then-intensity group improved 15% vs. 5.3%).

### "In-between" training days
- **Johnston:** Avoids Z3 except when earned through the 10% test. Z3 is "seductive because it gives immediate boost" but should make up no more than 10% of annual volume.
- **Koop:** Doesn't address the concept of "in-between" days. Clear workout types at defined RPE levels.
- **Magness:** Explicitly values medium-effort days (surges, progressions, steady runs) that provide a different stimulus from pure easy or pure hard. "Don't get caught up in a polarized system."

### The role of VO2max intervals for ultrarunners
- **Johnston:** Used sparingly. Z4 work only after Z3 is established. Total work 15-30 min per session.
- **Koop:** VO2max intervals are the FIRST intensity type introduced in the training cycle. 2-4 week blocks. "Raise the roof" to lift all thresholds below.
- **Magness:** VO2max itself plateaus early in trained athletes and is a poor performance predictor. Acknowledges VO2max work triggers adaptations via AMPK pathway but cautions against overemphasis.

## Coaching Decision Tree

1. **What phase is the athlete in?**
   - Early season / base building: Easy volume (all three agree). Add strides, surges, hill sprints for neuromuscular maintenance (Magness, Johnston). No formal intervals unless athlete passes 10% test (Johnston).
   - Mid-season / build phase:
     - Johnston approach: Add Z3 at 5% of weekly volume, progress to 10%, then begin Z4.
     - Koop approach: RI blocks first (2-4 weeks), then TR blocks (3-4 weeks), then SSR blocks (3-6 weeks).
     - Magness approach: Shift emphasis gradually from general to support to specific. Keep all components present.
   - Late season / race-specific: All converge on race-specific volume and terrain matching.

2. **How should the workout be structured?**
   - Embed intervals within an EnduranceRun (Koop): "2-hr ER with 3x10 min TR."
   - Warm-up: 10-30 min easy, plus 4-8 x 20-sec strides before intervals.
   - Cooldown: at least 10 min easy.
   - Recovery between intervals: active, not standing. Keep easy enough to actually recover.

3. **How many hard sessions per week?**
   - Beginners: 1 per week maximum (Johnston).
   - Intermediate: 2 per week with 48-72 hours between (all three).
   - Advanced: 2-3 per week, potentially on consecutive days during concentrated blocks (Koop).
   - Recovery week: maximum 1 intensity session (Johnston).

4. **What terrain?**
   - Uphill for VO2max intervals (Koop: 80% of RI prescribed uphill, never downhill).
   - Match race terrain for steady-state and tempo work (Koop: feet-per-mile gradient matching).
   - Flat or slight uphill for threshold intervals (Johnston).
   - Variable terrain for progressive runs and surges (Magness).

5. **Athlete struggling with "no man's land" training?**
   - If all runs are at the same moderate effort: enforce separation. Use RPE 4-5 for easy days (Koop). Use HR ceiling at AeT for easy days (Johnston). Add specific hard sessions with clear intensity targets.

6. **Workout construction checklist:**
   - Define the primary adaptation target (aerobic capacity, LT, VO2max, neuromuscular)
   - Set time at intensity based on athlete level (beginner: lower end of range, advanced: upper)
   - Choose terrain that matches purpose (uphill for RI, race-specific for SSR/TR)
   - Prescribe warm-up (10-30 min easy + 4-8 strides) and cooldown (10+ min easy)
   - Set work:rest ratio appropriate to intensity (RI 1:1, TR 2:1, SSR >2:1, Z3 threshold 3:1 or 4:1)
   - Embed intervals within total ER duration when using CTS format
   - Flag if proposed workout exceeds typical TSS for athlete's current fitness level
   - Schedule relative to other hard sessions (48-72 hrs between intensity sessions)

7. **Recovery needed by workout type:**
   - Recovery run: 8-12 hours (Magness: GH increases ~550% in first 40 min)
   - Endurance run (2-3 hrs): 24-48 hours for glycogen restoration
   - SSR/Tempo session: 48 hours minimum
   - RI/VO2max session: 48-72 hours (Johnston: "Allow 72 hours between Z3 and Z4 sessions")
   - Hill sprints: 48 hours (CNS fatigue -- Magness: "a totally foreign concept" for distance coaches)
   - ME session: 72+ hours initially (Johnston: "Allow 3+ days recovery")
   - Long run (>3 hrs): 48-72 hours (structural + metabolic recovery)

8. **Terrain specificity reminders:**
   - Koop's feet-per-mile gradient matching: Western States = (18,090 + 22,970) / 100.2 = 410 ft/mile. Match this in training.
   - Johnston: "Mountain running and road running are, at best, distant cousins."
   - Koop: "You will not learn to play Chopin by practicing 'Chopsticks.'" (Ch. 2) -- train on race-similar terrain.
   - Practice walking in training -- "Something I don't think enough runners do." (Koop, Ch. 5)
   - Flat ground walk/run transition: ~13:30-14:00 min/mi. Below this, walking is more economical.
