# Heat & Altitude

## Summary
Heat and altitude are environmental stressors that sit near the top of Koop's hierarchy of ultramarathon training needs -- they are enhancements that matter only after all the fundamentals are in place. However, for races like Western States (heat), Leadville (altitude), or Hardrock (both), failing to prepare for these conditions can turn a well-trained athlete into a DNF. Koop provides the most comprehensive protocols: 7-10 days of consecutive heat exposure, sauna protocols, and specific altitude arrival guidelines. Magness contributes the neuroscience of anticipatory thermoregulation -- the brain reduces muscle activation in heat before core temperature becomes dangerous. Johnston's coverage is limited, largely confined to altitude's effect on aerobic training, but his aerobic base philosophy indirectly supports performance at altitude. All three agree that acclimatization is real, trainable, and time-dependent.

## Key Concepts

### Heat Acclimatization Physiology
- Exercise generates 5-15x more heat at rest.
- Four heat transfer methods: conduction, convection, radiation, evaporation.
- Key adaptations from heat acclimatization: earlier sweat onset, increased sweat rate, lower sweat sodium concentration, plasma volume expansion, lower core and skin temperature at given workload, lower exercise heart rate.
- Acclimatized person can sweat up to 4 liters/hour vs. 1.5 liters/hour non-acclimatized.
- Critical core temperature: ~40 degrees Celsius (104 degrees F). The body shuts down exercise before reaching it.

### Altitude Physiology
- Reduced partial pressure of O2, not less total air.
- Acute response (24-72 hrs): hemoconcentration, elevated HR, feel worst.
- Long-term adaptation (1-3 weeks): EPO release, erythropoiesis (more red blood cells), expanded plasma volume.
- Cardiac output is reduced in hypoxic conditions -- not because muscles need less oxygen, but as a regulatory mechanism.
- Individual response to altitude training varies widely.

### The Decay Problem
- Heat acclimatization decays at ~2.5% per day after the first 48 hours without exposure.
- After a 2-week taper with no heat exposure: ~35% decay in HR control, ~6% decay in core temp control, ~30% decay in sweat rate improvements.
- Can reacclimate in as few as 4 days if previously acclimatized.
- Maintenance: every 3 days after initial acclimatization period.

### Altitude Arrival Windows
- **The 24-72 hour window:** Acute altitude response is worst during this period. If you cannot arrive weeks early, arrive the night before or morning of the race to avoid this window entirely.

## What the Experts Say

### Scott Johnston (Evoke / Uphill Athlete)
- **Limited direct coverage:** *Training for the Uphill Athlete* does not provide detailed heat or altitude acclimatization protocols. The book is primarily focused on training methodology rather than environmental preparation.
- **Altitude references are indirect:**
  - Anna Frost's Everest Marathon story mentions acclimatization through pre-race exploration at altitude (8 weeks in Nepal, progressive elevation). (Ch. 3, *Training for the Uphill Athlete*)
  - Kilian's childhood at altitude in the Pyrenees is referenced as background.
  - Altitude and sleep deprivation mentioned as major stressors in Luke Nelson's Nolan's 14 account.
- **Aerobic base and altitude performance:** Johnston's aerobic base philosophy is indirectly relevant to altitude. At altitude, aerobic capacity is reduced, making AeT even more important. A high AeT allows more sustained effort at altitude.
  - Case study: David Gottler and Ueli Steck on Island Peak. Gottler's AeT at sea level ~155 bpm; max HR during Island Peak climb was only 133 bpm while gaining 1,500m in 2h40m. They cut 30 minutes off the previous year's identical climb (8% improvement) and felt less tired. (https://evokeendurance.com/resources/our-training-philosophy-put-to-a-test-by-elite-alpinists/)
  - "No amount of high-intensity training can provide the needed metabolic or structural adaptations for optimal performance at altitude." (https://evokeendurance.com/resources/our-training-philosophy-put-to-a-test-by-elite-alpinists/)
- **Post-heavy-training immunity:** "Especially during the first hour immediately following the workout. Hence the age-old admonition to change into dry clothes and wear a hat in winter." (Ch. 4) -- relevant to training in heat when immune system is already stressed.
- **Practical takeaway:** Johnston's limited coverage of heat/altitude is a gap. For athletes coached in the Johnston/Uphill Athlete framework, supplement with Koop's protocols for environmental preparation.

### Jason Koop (CTS)
- **Heat acclimation -- the 7-10 day rule:** "You need 7-10 days of heat exposure in a row to elicit positive adaptations." (https://trainright.com/ultrarunners-heat-acclimation-cheat-sheet/)
- **Two-phase heat acclimation approach:** Specific timing considerations for building and maintaining heat readiness. (https://trainright.com/heat-acclimation-when-to-start-and-how-long-it-takes/)
- **Heat physiology (from the book):**
  - Exercise generates 5-15x more heat than rest
  - Four heat transfer methods: conduction, convection, radiation, evaporation
  - Key adaptations: earlier sweat onset, increased sweat rate, lower sweat sodium, plasma volume expansion, lower core/skin temp, lower exercise HR
  - (Ch. 7, *Training Essentials for Ultrarunning*)
- **Sauna protocols (preferred method -- passive heating post-exercise):**
  - Enter "pre-warmed" by exercise -- less total time needed in sauna
  - Maintain workout quality in natural environment, then add heat stress afterward
  - Target: core temperature ~101 degrees F (38.5 degrees C) for ~90 minutes
  - Duration: 7-10 consecutive days minimum
  - Maintenance: every 3 days after initial acclimatization
  - Decay: ~2.5% per day after first 48 hours without exposure
  - After 2-week taper with no heat exposure: ~35% decay in HR control, ~6% core temp control, ~30% sweat rate improvements
  - Can reacclimate in as little as 4 days
  - **Protocol 1 (single exposure):** 7-10 days leading into race; taper more than normal to compensate for additional heat stress
  - **Protocol 2 (repeated exposure):** First bout ~6 weeks out, maintenance every 3-4 days, final reacclimation in last week before race
  - Monitor core temperature and hydration status; consult physician if concerns
  - (Ch. 7)
- **Training in heat:** Perform endurance runs (not intervals) in hot environments because these lower-intensity sessions are not as detrimentally affected by heat, and the exposure aids acclimatization. (https://trainright.com/hot-weather-training-ultrarunning/)
- **Managing unexpected early-season heat:** Have strategies for isolated hot days during spring training that fall outside normal acclimatization windows. (https://trainright.com/heat-challenge-strategies-exercising-isolated-hot-days/)
- **Race-day cooling strategies:**
  - Pack ice on torso, neck, head; ice arm sleeves
  - Light-colored, loose clothing
  - Dump water on yourself (evaporative cooling)
  - Drink cool fluids
  - Maintain hydration status -- keep sweat response intact
  - If needed: stop running, walk slowly, or stop 10-30 min with ice and fluids
  - (Ch. 7)
- **Fluid strategy in heat:** Separate calories from hydration. As temperature increases, fluid needs increase independently from calorie needs. High-carb drinks cause GI distress when you need more fluid in heat. "You can come back from a caloric deficit easily and within minutes by eating. Returning to normal hydration status takes much longer." (Ch. 13)
- **Altitude physiology:**
  - Reduced partial pressure of O2
  - Acute response (24-72 hrs): hemoconcentration, elevated HR, feel worst
  - Long-term (1-3 weeks): EPO release, erythropoiesis, expanded plasma volume
  - Keep training intensity low (<70% VO2max) on first days at altitude
  - (Ch. 7)
- **Pre-race altitude arrival guidelines:**
  - Races at 1,640-6,561 ft (500-2,000m): arrive 3-5 days early
  - Races at 6,561-9,842 ft (2,000-3,000m): arrive 1-2 weeks early
  - Races above 9,842 ft (3,000m): arrive at least 3 weeks early
  - OR: arrive night before/morning of (within 24-hour window before acute response kicks in)
  - (Ch. 7)
- **Live High, Train Low:** Best protocol (general consensus). Live High, Train High/Train Low: most efficacious (low-intensity at altitude, high-intensity at lower elevation). Optimal living altitude: 5,900-9,840 ft (1,800-3,000m) -- no added benefit above 9,840 ft. Minimum: 2 weeks; ideal: 3-4 weeks. Minimum to avoid feeling terrible: arrive 1 week early. (Ch. 7)
- **Iron panel before altitude racing:** Get iron panel 3 months before altitude race. Serum ferritin <35 ng/mL = cannot produce red blood cell mass increase even after 4 weeks at altitude. (Ch. 7)
- **Altitude tents:** Still uncertain; 3-4 weeks exposure needed for changes. Researchers struggle to show meaningful changes for athletes living below 5,900 ft. May work if living at ~5,500-6,500 ft and supplementing to 8,200-9,840 ft simulated. Weigh cost, sleep quality impact, and current elevation. (Ch. 7)
- **Flatlander's guide:** If you cannot arrive early, fly in the night before or morning of the race to avoid the 24-72 hour window. Once per 2-3 weeks, incorporate a session that exceeds your goal elevation gain/loss per mile by ~10%. (https://trainright.com/flatlander-guide-training-for-mountainous-ultramarathons/)
- **Hierarchy position:** Heat acclimation and altitude training sit near the very top of Koop's training needs hierarchy -- they are enhancements that matter only after all the fundamentals (volume, rest, nutrition, intensity distribution, mental skills) are in place. "Focus on the things that matter more. Training concepts near the big base of the pyramid will matter much more than the nuance of whether you spend eight or ten days in the sauna." (Ch. 10)

### Steve Magness (Science of Running)
- **Anticipatory thermoregulation:** The brain monitors rate of heat storage and adjusts muscle activation early in exercise, BEFORE critical temperature is reached. Lower EMG levels seen in hotter temperatures even when core temperatures were essentially the same -- this is an anticipatory mechanism, not a reactive one. (Ch. 8, *The Science of Running*, citing Tucker et al. 2004)
- **Critical core temperature:** ~40 degrees Celsius. Body shuts down exercise before reaching it. "In a time trial in the heat, your pace slows early on, despite not having enough time to reach a critical core temperature." (Ch. 3)
- **Deception study (Castle et al. 2012):** Cyclists told it was cool (actually 32 degrees C) performed same distance/power as in actual cool conditions. External feedback profoundly affects anticipatory regulation. Implication: mental preparation and positive self-talk in heat may have real physiological effects on pacing. (Ch. 8)
- **Heat training provides altitude-like benefits:** Training in warm/humid conditions provides similar physiological stress to altitude -- reduced blood flow/oxygen to working muscles at slower paces. Adaptation: increase in blood volume following heat/humidity training. Frank Shorter used this in 1970s: splitting training between Florida and Boulder, CO. (Ch. 8)
- **Sweat rate adaptation:** Acclimatized person can sweat up to 4 liters/hour vs. 1.5 liters/hour non-acclimatized. (Ch. 8, citing Brooks et al. 2004)
- **Heat countermeasures:** Ice vests pre-race (used by elite marathoners at 2004 Athens Olympics). Drinking ice-cold fluids to combat core temperature rise. Understanding that heat regulation may be the primary cause of fatigue, not "fitness." (Ch. 8)
- **Altitude physiology:** Individual response to altitude training varies widely (Chapman et al. 1998). Hypoxia reduces cerebral oxygen, causing decreased voluntary activation of muscles -- including muscles not involved in exercise (Goodall 2012). Cardiac output is reduced in hypoxic conditions -- not because muscles need less oxygen, but as a regulatory mechanism. When supplementary oxygen provided, cardiac output immediately returns to normal (Noakes 2004). (Ch. 5, Ch. 10)
- **Key insight for coaches:** Heat and altitude fatigue are primarily regulatory, not catastrophic. The brain reduces output as a protective mechanism. An athlete who understands this can learn to push closer to actual limits through training and mental preparation -- but always within the safety margin.

## Where They Agree
1. **Acclimatization is real and trainable.** All three acknowledge that the body adapts to heat and altitude stress through specific, measurable physiological changes (plasma volume expansion, sweat adaptation, erythropoiesis).
2. **Time is the critical variable.** You cannot shortcut acclimatization. A minimum of 7-10 days for heat and 1-3 weeks for altitude is required for meaningful adaptation.
3. **The brain regulates exercise in heat and at altitude.** Performance declines in these conditions are primarily regulatory, not catastrophic failure. The brain reduces output before critical limits are reached.
4. **Heat training and altitude training produce overlapping benefits.** Heat acclimatization increases plasma volume, which also benefits altitude performance. Training in hot/humid conditions provides altitude-like physiological stress.
5. **Fundamentals come first.** No amount of sauna sessions or altitude tents compensates for inadequate training volume, sleep, or nutrition.

## Where They Disagree

### Emphasis and Depth
- **Koop:** Provides the most comprehensive, actionable protocols. Specific sauna timing, arrival guidelines, decay rates, and race-day cooling strategies. Treats heat/altitude as a distinct training variable with its own periodization.
- **Johnston:** Limited direct coverage. The book focuses on training methodology. Altitude is addressed indirectly through the aerobic base framework -- a well-developed AeT is Johnston's primary answer to altitude performance. Heat protocols are not explicitly addressed.
- **Magness:** Provides the neuroscience of WHY heat and altitude affect performance (anticipatory regulation, EMG reduction, cerebral oxygen reduction) but fewer specific protocols. His contribution is conceptual rather than prescriptive.

### Sauna vs. Training in Heat
- **Koop:** Explicitly prefers passive heating (sauna) post-exercise. Rationale: maintain workout quality in normal conditions, then add heat stress afterward. This separates the training stimulus from the acclimatization stimulus.
- **Magness:** References heat training during exercise (Frank Shorter's Florida approach). Does not distinguish between passive and active heat exposure methods.
- **Johnston:** No explicit position.

### Role of Aerobic Base at Altitude
- **Johnston:** The primary tool for altitude performance is a high AeT. "No amount of high-intensity training can provide the needed metabolic or structural adaptations for optimal performance at altitude." Building aerobic capacity at sea level is more valuable than altitude-specific training for most athletes.
- **Koop:** Acknowledges altitude is primarily an aerobic challenge but provides specific acclimatization protocols that supplement base fitness. Altitude preparation is a separate layer on top of fitness.
- **Magness:** Emphasizes that altitude reduces cerebral oxygen and causes decreased voluntary activation -- a regulatory mechanism independent of aerobic fitness. Even well-trained athletes face altitude-specific challenges that fitness alone does not solve.

## Coaching Decision Tree

### Heat Preparation

**Step 1: Assess the race conditions**
- Expected temperature range on race day
- Humidity levels
- Sun exposure (exposed ridges vs. shaded forest)
- Time of day for hottest sections
- History of heat-related DNFs at this event

**Step 2: Determine protocol timeline**

*If race is in >6 weeks:*
- Use Koop's Protocol 2 (repeated exposure):
  - First sauna bout ~6 weeks out
  - Maintenance every 3-4 days
  - Final reacclimation in last week before race
  - Monitor hydration closely -- sauna sessions are dehydrating

*If race is in 2-4 weeks:*
- Use Koop's Protocol 1 (single exposure):
  - 7-10 consecutive days of sauna immediately post-exercise
  - Target: core temperature ~101 degrees F for ~90 minutes per session
  - Taper more aggressively than normal to compensate for additional heat stress
  - Do not start new heat exposure with <7 days to race

*If race is in <2 weeks and no prior acclimatization:*
- Focus on race-day cooling strategies (ice, wet bandanas, cold fluid pre-loading)
- Adjust pacing expectations downward -- expect 5-10% slower in severe heat
- Do NOT attempt crash heat acclimatization in the final week

**Step 3: During-race heat management**
- Ice on neck, head, torso at every aid station in hot sections
- Light-colored, loose clothing
- Increase fluid intake proactively -- do not wait until thirsty
- Separate calories from hydration (Koop's recommendation)
- If core temp feels dangerously elevated: walk, dump water on body, stop for 10-30 min with ice if needed
- Remember: pace will slow before temperature becomes critical (anticipatory regulation). Accept the slowdown rather than fighting it.

**Step 4: Monitor for heat illness**
- Confusion, disorientation, cessation of sweating = medical emergency
- This is beyond coaching scope -- get medical help immediately
- Recommend consulting a physician before any heat acclimatization protocol for athletes with cardiovascular risk factors

### Altitude Preparation

**Step 1: Classify the race altitude**
- **Low altitude (< 5,000 ft / 1,500m):** No specific preparation needed
- **Moderate altitude (5,000-8,000 ft / 1,500-2,500m):** Some preparation beneficial; performance impact ~3-8%
- **High altitude (8,000-14,000 ft / 2,500-4,300m):** Significant preparation required; performance impact ~10-20%+
- **Very high altitude (> 14,000 ft / 4,300m):** Mountaineering territory; beyond scope of most ultrarunning

**Step 2: Determine arrival strategy**

*If athlete can arrive 2+ weeks early:*
- Ideal scenario. Progressive acclimatization at race altitude.
- Keep training intensity low (<70% VO2max) for first 3-5 days
- Gradually reintroduce intensity as body adapts
- Use Live High, Train Low if possible (sleep at 5,900-9,840 ft, do hard sessions lower)

*If athlete can arrive 1-2 weeks early:*
- Minimum recommended for races at 6,561-9,842 ft
- First 3 days: easy activity only, focus on hydration and sleep
- Days 4-7: gradually return to normal easy running volume
- Days 8-14: can include shortened intensity sessions

*If athlete can arrive 3-5 days early:*
- Suitable for races at 1,640-6,561 ft only
- At higher altitudes, this puts you in the worst of the acute response window

*If athlete cannot arrive early:*
- Arrive the night before or morning of the race to avoid the 24-72 hour window of worst acute response
- This is Koop's explicit recommendation for athletes who cannot acclimatize
- Accept a performance decrement but avoid the acute misery of days 2-3

**Step 3: Pre-race preparation (months out)**
- Get iron panel 3 months before altitude race
- Serum ferritin must be >35 ng/mL to produce red blood cell mass increase from altitude exposure
- If ferritin is low: supplement iron under medical guidance before attempting altitude training
- Build the strongest aerobic base possible -- Johnston's AeT emphasis is particularly valuable for altitude racing
- Consider altitude tents only if living at 5,500-6,500 ft already and adding simulated elevation to 8,200-9,840 ft

**Step 4: Race-day adjustments at altitude**
- Reduce pace expectations by 5-15% depending on altitude and acclimatization status
- Increase fluid intake -- dehydration is accelerated at altitude
- Monitor for symptoms of acute mountain sickness (headache, nausea, dizziness)
- Caffeine may help -- it increases ventilation and can partially offset altitude-induced cognitive decline
- Walking/hiking thresholds shift lower at altitude -- expect to hike gradients you would normally run

### Combined Heat + Altitude (e.g., Hardrock, Leadville)
- Prioritize altitude acclimatization (arrive early) over heat acclimatization (sauna protocol)
- If living at altitude pre-race: sauna protocol is compatible with altitude living
- If arriving at altitude and hot conditions expected: keep all efforts easy for first 3-5 days, allow body to handle both stressors simultaneously
- Hydration becomes doubly important -- both heat and altitude increase fluid requirements
- Expect combined performance decrement of 10-20% compared to sea-level, mild conditions
- Iron status is critical -- get tested 3 months out
